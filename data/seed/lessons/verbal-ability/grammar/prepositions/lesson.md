# Prepositions

## Explanations

### Introduction

**Prepositions** are small but powerful words that show the relationship between a noun (or pronoun) and another element in a sentence. They indicate time, place, direction, manner, and logical relationships. Despite being among the shortest words in English, prepositions are responsible for an enormous amount of meaning — and an enormous number of errors on the Civil Service Examination.

### Why Prepositions Matter

Prepositions are the connective tissue of English sentences. They answer critical questions:
- **Where?** — The report is *on* the desk.
- **When?** — The deadline is *on* Friday.
- **How?** — She solved it *by* elimination.
- **Why?** — He resigned *because of* the scandal.

Without correct prepositions, sentences become ambiguous or nonsensical. In government communication — memoranda, legal documents, policy directives — a wrong preposition can change the meaning of an entire clause.

### Why Prepositions Are Tested in the CSE

The Grammar and Correct Usage section of the Philippine Civil Service Examination consistently tests prepositions because:
- Government documents require precise spatial and temporal references ("Submit *on or before* December 15" vs. "Submit *by* December 15")
- Preposition errors are among the most frequent mistakes Filipino English speakers make, often due to L1 interference from Filipino/Tagalog
- Correct preposition usage signals professional-level English competence
- Many idiomatic expressions tested in the CSE hinge on a single preposition choice

### Common Mistakes Examinees Make

1. Confusing **in/on/at** for time and place ("I will see you *in* Monday" instead of "*on* Monday")
2. Using **between** instead of **among** (and vice versa)
3. Adding unnecessary prepositions ("Where did you go *at*?" instead of "Where did you go?")
4. Wrong verb-preposition collocations ("depend *in*" instead of "depend *on*")
5. Confusing **since/for** in duration expressions
6. Using **beside** when **besides** is meant (and vice versa)
7. Omitting required prepositions ("She is capable *—*" instead of "She is capable *of*")

---

### Learning Objectives

After this lesson, you should be able to:
- Identify different types of prepositions and their grammatical functions
- Correctly use prepositions of time (at, on, in, since, for, by, until, during)
- Correctly use prepositions of place (in, on, at, under, above, between, among)
- Correctly use prepositions of direction and movement (to, into, onto, toward, through, across)
- Recognize and use prepositional phrases as modifiers
- Distinguish commonly confused preposition pairs (in vs. into, on vs. onto, between vs. among, beside vs. besides, since vs. for, by vs. until)
- Apply correct adjective-preposition, verb-preposition, and noun-preposition collocations
- Identify and correct unnecessary or missing prepositions
- Solve CSE-style preposition questions with confidence under time pressure

### 4.1 What Are Prepositions?

#### Definition

A **preposition** is a word (or group of words) placed before a noun or pronoun to show its relationship to another word in the sentence. The word "preposition" itself means "placed before" (pre- + position).

#### Grammatical Function

Prepositions never stand alone. They always take an **object** — a noun, pronoun, or noun phrase — to form a **prepositional phrase**. The prepositional phrase then functions as either an adjective (modifying a noun) or an adverb (modifying a verb, adjective, or another adverb).

**Structure:** Preposition + Object = Prepositional Phrase

#### Examples by Difficulty

**Easy:**
- The book is **on** the table. (place)
- She arrived **at** 9:00 AM. (time)
- He walked **to** the office. (direction)

**Medium:**
- The memorandum was distributed **among** all department heads. (relationship)
- The employee has been absent **since** Monday. (time reference point)
- The resolution was passed **in accordance with** the rules. (manner)

**Hard:**
- The committee deliberated **on** the matter **for** three hours **without** reaching a consensus **on** the proposed amendment **to** the charter. (multiple prepositions showing different relationships)
- Pursuant **to** Section 5, Article III **of** the Constitution, no law shall be passed abridging freedom **of** speech. (legal/formal register)

**CSE-Style:**
- The supervisor reminded the staff ___ the new policy.
  - Answer: **of** (remind someone *of* something)
- The applicant must comply ___ all requirements.
  - Answer: **with** (comply *with* something)

### 4.2 Types of Prepositions

#### Simple Prepositions

Single words that function as prepositions. These are the most common:

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| Common | in, on, at, to, for, with, by, from, of |
| Position | under, over, above, below, beside, between, among |
| Direction | into, onto, toward, through, across, along, around |
| Time | before, after, during, since, until, till |
| Others | about, against, despite, except, without |

#### Compound Prepositions

Formed by combining a prefix (a-, be-) with a noun, adjective, or adverb:

| Compound Preposition | Meaning | Example |
|---------------------|---------|---------|
| aboard | on (a vehicle) | She got *aboard* the ship. |
| about | concerning | The memo is *about* the new policy. |
| above | higher than | The temperature rose *above* 30°C. |
| across | from one side to another | Walk *across* the street. |
| along | following the length of | Drive *along* the highway. |
| among | in the middle of (3+) | Distribute *among* the employees. |
| around | surrounding | The fence goes *around* the building. |
| before | earlier than | Submit *before* the deadline. |
| behind | at the back of | The parking lot is *behind* the building. |
| below | lower than | Scores *below* 80 are failing. |
| beneath | under | The pipes run *beneath* the floor. |
| beside | next to | Sit *beside* me. |
| between | in the middle of (2) | Choose *between* Option A and Option B. |
| beyond | farther than | This is *beyond* my authority. |
| despite | in spite of | He passed *despite* the difficulty. |
| inside | within | Stay *inside* the building. |
| outside | not within | Wait *outside* the office. |
| within | inside; not exceeding | Reply *within* five days. |
| without | lacking | He left *without* permission. |

#### Phrase Prepositions (Multi-Word Prepositions)

Groups of words that function as a single preposition:

| Phrase Preposition | Example |
|-------------------|---------|
| according to | *According to* the memo, the meeting is at 3 PM. |
| because of | The event was canceled *because of* the typhoon. |
| in front of | Park *in front of* the building. |
| in spite of | She succeeded *in spite of* the obstacles. |
| instead of | Use email *instead of* fax. |
| on behalf of | I am writing *on behalf of* the director. |
| with regard to | *With regard to* your inquiry, please see the attachment. |
| in accordance with | Act *in accordance with* the guidelines. |
| by means of | The data was collected *by means of* a survey. |
| in addition to | *In addition to* the report, submit the appendix. |
| prior to | Complete the form *prior to* the interview. |
| due to | The delay was *due to* heavy traffic. |
| as a result of | *As a result of* the audit, changes were implemented. |
| for the purpose of | This form is *for the purpose of* documentation. |
| in case of | *In case of* emergency, call 911. |
| on account of | He was late *on account of* the flood. |

#### Participial Prepositions

Present or past participles that function as prepositions:

| Participial Preposition | Example |
|------------------------|---------|
| concerning | I have a question *concerning* the schedule. |
| considering | *Considering* the circumstances, the decision was fair. |
| during | *During* the meeting, no phones are allowed. |
| following | *Following* the announcement, employees were briefed. |
| given | *Given* the budget constraints, we must prioritize. |
| including | All staff, *including* contractuals, must attend. |
| regarding | *Regarding* your request, it has been approved. |
| pending | The case is *pending* review. |

### 4.3 Prepositions of Time

This is one of the most heavily tested areas on the CSE. Master these rules:

#### AT — Exact Times and Specific Moments

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| Clock times | The meeting starts **at** 8:00 AM. |
| Mealtimes | We eat lunch **at** noon. |
| Specific moments | **At** that moment, the alarm rang. |
| Night (general) | He studies **at** night. |
| Holidays (as a period) | **At** Christmas, offices are closed. |
| Age | She passed the CSE **at** 23. |

#### ON — Days and Dates

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| Days of the week | The exam is **on** Saturday. |
| Specific dates | Submit it **on** March 15, 2025. |
| Special days | **On** Independence Day, there is no work. |
| Day + time of day | **On** Monday morning, we have a meeting. |
| Birthdays/anniversaries | **On** her birthday, she received a promotion. |

#### IN — Longer Periods

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| Months | The results come out **in** April. |
| Years | He was hired **in** 2019. |
| Seasons | Enrollment opens **in** summer. |
| Centuries/decades | **In** the 21st century, technology dominates. |
| Parts of the day | **In** the morning, I review my notes. |
| Duration (future) | The report will be ready **in** two weeks. |

> **Exception:** We say "at night" (NOT "in the night") but "in the morning/afternoon/evening."

#### DURING — Throughout a Period

- **During** the seminar, participants must not use phones.
- She was promoted **during** her second year of service.

*"During" answers "when?" and refers to the entire span of an event.*

#### SINCE — From a Point in the Past Until Now

- He has worked here **since** 2015.
- **Since** Monday, no updates have been received.

*"Since" requires a specific starting point and is used with perfect tenses.*

#### FOR — Duration of Time

- She has been waiting **for** three hours.
- He studied **for** six months before the exam.

*"For" answers "how long?" and specifies a length of time.*

#### UNTIL / TILL — Up to a Point in Time

- The office is open **until** 5:00 PM.
- Wait here **until** I return.

*"Until" marks the endpoint; the action continues up to that moment and stops.*

#### BY — No Later Than (Deadline)

- Submit the report **by** Friday.
- The project must be completed **by** December 31.

*"By" means "at or before" — it sets a deadline.*

#### Common Confusion: SINCE vs. FOR

| Since | For |
|-------|-----|
| Specific point in time | Duration/length of time |
| since 2020 | for four years |
| since Monday | for three days |
| since 8:00 AM | for two hours |
| since January | for six months |

**Test yourself:**
- She has been absent ___ last week. → **since** (point in time)
- She has been absent ___ five days. → **for** (duration)

#### Common Confusion: BY vs. UNTIL

| By | Until |
|----|-------|
| Deadline (action completed before) | Continuous action up to a point |
| Submit the form **by** Friday. | The office is open **until** 5 PM. |
| Finish **by** noon. | Stay here **until** I call you. |

**Key difference:** "By" = completed before that time. "Until" = continuing up to that time.

### 4.4 Prepositions of Place

#### AT — Specific Points/Locations

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| Exact address | She lives **at** 45 Rizal Street. |
| Specific location | Meet me **at** the entrance. |
| Events | He is **at** the conference. |
| Workplace (general) | She is **at** work. |
| Institutions (activity) | The children are **at** school. |

#### IN — Enclosed Spaces / Areas

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| Countries | She works **in** the Philippines. |
| Cities/towns | He lives **in** Manila. |
| Rooms/buildings | The files are **in** the cabinet. |
| Enclosed spaces | The letter is **in** the envelope. |
| Water/liquids | The fish swims **in** the pond. |
| Books/newspapers | I read it **in** the newspaper. |

#### ON — Surfaces / Lines

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| Surfaces | The memo is **on** the bulletin board. |
| Floors | Her office is **on** the third floor. |
| Streets (without number) | The building is **on** Ayala Avenue. |
| Public transport | She is **on** the bus. |
| Electronic devices | I saw it **on** television. |
| Pages | Sign **on** page 5. |

#### AT vs. IN vs. ON — Quick Reference

| Preposition | Think of it as... | Examples |
|-------------|-------------------|----------|
| **at** | A point/dot on a map | at the corner, at the bus stop, at the office |
| **in** | Inside a container/area | in the room, in Manila, in the Philippines |
| **on** | Touching a surface/line | on the table, on the wall, on Rizal Street |

#### Other Place Prepositions

| Preposition | Meaning | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| under | directly below | The box is **under** the desk. |
| below | lower than (not directly) | The temperature dropped **below** zero. |
| above | higher than (not touching) | The sign is **above** the door. |
| over | directly above / covering | A bridge goes **over** the river. |
| beside / next to | at the side of | Sit **beside** me. |
| between | in the middle of TWO | The office is **between** the bank and the pharmacy. |
| among | in the middle of THREE+ | She distributed the forms **among** the applicants. |
| in front of | facing the front | Park **in front of** the building. |
| behind | at the back | The storage room is **behind** the office. |
| inside | within | Stay **inside** the premises. |
| outside | not within | Wait **outside** the door. |
| near / close to | not far from | The post office is **near** the city hall. |
| opposite | facing, across from | The library is **opposite** the park. |

### 4.5 Prepositions of Direction and Movement

These prepositions show WHERE something is going:

#### TO — General Direction/Destination

- She walked **to** the office.
- Send the letter **to** the director.
- He went **to** Cebu for a seminar.

#### INTO — Movement from Outside to Inside

- She walked **into** the room.
- Pour the water **into** the glass.
- He got **into** the car.

#### ONTO — Movement to a Surface

- The cat jumped **onto** the table.
- Place the documents **onto** the tray.

#### TOWARD(S) — In the Direction of (Not Necessarily Arriving)

- She walked **toward** the exit.
- The country is moving **toward** digitalization.

#### THROUGH — From One Side to the Other (Passing Inside)

- Walk **through** the corridor.
- The bullet went **through** the wall.
- She read **through** the entire document.

#### ACROSS — From One Side to the Other (On a Surface)

- Walk **across** the street.
- The bridge goes **across** the river.

#### ALONG — Following the Length of

- Walk **along** the hallway.
- Trees line **along** the road.

#### AROUND — In a Circular Path / Surrounding

- The guard walked **around** the building.
- There is a fence **around** the compound.

#### Key Distinction: IN vs. INTO

| In (Static/Location) | Into (Movement/Direction) |
|----------------------|--------------------------|
| She is **in** the office. | She walked **into** the office. |
| The files are **in** the drawer. | Put the files **into** the drawer. |
| He is **in** the pool. | He jumped **into** the pool. |

**Rule:** Use **in** for location (where something IS). Use **into** for movement (where something is GOING).

#### Key Distinction: ON vs. ONTO

| On (Static/Location) | Onto (Movement/Direction) |
|---------------------|--------------------------|
| The book is **on** the shelf. | Put the book **onto** the shelf. |
| She is **on** the stage. | She climbed **onto** the stage. |

### 4.6 Prepositional Phrases

#### Structure

A prepositional phrase consists of:
**Preposition + (Modifiers) + Object (Noun/Pronoun)**

| Component | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| Preposition | in |
| Modifier(s) | the large |
| Object | conference room |
| Full phrase | **in the large conference room** |

#### Functions in a Sentence

**As an Adjective (modifies a noun):**
- The man **in the gray suit** is the director. (Which man?)
- The report **on the desk** needs your signature. (Which report?)
- The meeting **at 3 PM** has been canceled. (Which meeting?)

**As an Adverb (modifies a verb, adjective, or adverb):**
- She works **with great efficiency**. (How does she work?)
- The memo was sent **before the deadline**. (When was it sent?)
- He is good **at solving problems**. (Good in what way?)

#### Multiple Prepositional Phrases

Sentences often contain several prepositional phrases:
- **In the morning**, the director **of the department** spoke **to the staff** **about the new policy** **for overtime compensation**.

Each phrase adds specific information about time, identity, recipient, topic, and purpose.

### 4.7 Commonly Confused Prepositions

#### IN vs. INTO

| In | Into |
|----|------|
| Location (static) | Movement (dynamic) |
| She is **in** the room. | She walked **into** the room. |
| The keys are **in** my bag. | I put the keys **into** my bag. |

**CSE Trap:** "He jumped *in* the pool" is technically acceptable in informal speech but grammatically, "He jumped *into* the pool" is correct for the exam.

#### ON vs. ONTO

| On | Onto |
|----|------|
| Position on a surface | Movement to a surface |
| The paper is **on** the desk. | Place the paper **onto** the desk. |

#### AMONG vs. BETWEEN

| Between | Among |
|---------|-------|
| TWO items/people | THREE or more |
| Choose **between** A and B. | Distribute **among** all employees. |
| **Between** you and me, ... | She is popular **among** her peers. |

**Exception:** Use "between" for three or more when each item is considered individually:
- The agreement is **between** the Philippines, Japan, and Korea. (Each is a distinct party.)

#### BESIDE vs. BESIDES

| Beside | Besides |
|--------|---------|
| Next to (physical position) | In addition to / Apart from |
| Sit **beside** me. | **Besides** English, she speaks Filipino. |
| The tree is **beside** the gate. | **Besides** the report, submit the appendix. |

**Memory trick:** "BesideS" has an S for "Something extra/additional."

#### SINCE vs. FOR

| Since | For |
|-------|-----|
| Starting point | Duration |
| **since** 2020 | **for** four years |
| **since** January | **for** six months |
| **since** last Monday | **for** a week |

**Rule:** "Since" + point in time. "For" + length of time.

#### BY vs. UNTIL

| By | Until |
|----|-------|
| Deadline (completed before) | Continuation up to a point |
| Finish **by** 5 PM. (done before 5) | Work **until** 5 PM. (keep working up to 5) |
| Submit **by** Friday. | The office is open **until** Friday. |

#### AT vs. IN vs. ON (Summary Table)

| | AT | IN | ON |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Time** | exact time (at 9 AM) | months/years/long periods (in March, in 2024) | days/dates (on Monday, on June 12) |
| **Place** | specific point (at the door) | enclosed area (in the room, in Manila) | surface (on the table, on the 3rd floor) |

| In time | On time |
|---------|---------|
| Not too late; with time to spare | Punctually; at the scheduled time |

| We finished **in time** for the deadline. | He is always **on time**. |

### 4.12 Unnecessary Prepositions

Some sentences incorrectly add prepositions where none is needed:

| Incorrect | Correct | Rule |
|-----------|---------|------|
| Where are you **at**? | Where are you? | "Where" already implies location |
| Where did he go **to**? | Where did he go? | "Where" already implies direction |
| She entered **into** the room. | She entered the room. | "Enter" already means "go into" |
| He returned **back** the book. | He returned the book. | "Return" already means "give back" |
| They discussed **about** the issue. | They discussed the issue. | "Discuss" is transitive (no preposition) |
| She emphasized **on** the importance. | She emphasized the importance. | "Emphasize" is transitive |
| He requested **for** a transfer. | He requested a transfer. | "Request" is transitive |
| The meeting comprised **of** five items. | The meeting comprised five items. | "Comprise" means "consist of" already |
| She mentioned **about** the policy. | She mentioned the policy. | "Mention" is transitive |

| Incorrect | Correct |
|-----------|---------|

| interested *on* | interested **in** |
| capable *in* | capable **of** |
| different *with* | different **from** |
| familiar *of* | familiar **with** |

| Incorrect | Correct |
|-----------|---------|
| *in* Monday | **on** Monday |

| *at* January | **in** January |

| Incorrect | Correct |

### 4.14 Step-by-Step Rules for Choosing Prepositions

#### Rule 1: Identify the Relationship

Ask yourself: What relationship does the preposition show?
- **Time?** → at/on/in/since/for/by/until/during
- **Place?** → at/on/in/under/above/beside/between/among
- **Direction?** → to/into/onto/toward/through/across/along
- **Manner?** → by/with/without/in
- **Cause?** → because of/due to/on account of

#### Rule 2: Check for Fixed Collocations

Many preposition choices are NOT about logic — they are about memorized combinations:
- depend **on** (not "depend in")
- comply **with** (not "comply to")
- interested **in** (not "interested on")

**If the sentence has an adjective, verb, or noun that takes a fixed preposition, use that preposition regardless of "logic."**

#### Rule 3: Static vs. Dynamic

- **Static (location/position):** in, on, at
- **Dynamic (movement/direction):** into, onto, to

Ask: Is something STAYING or MOVING?
- Staying → in/on/at
- Moving → into/onto/to

#### Rule 4: Specificity Scale for Time and Place

**Time:** at (most specific) → on (medium) → in (least specific)
- **at** 3:00 PM → **on** Monday → **in** January

**Place:** at (point) → on (surface/line) → in (area/volume)
- **at** the door → **on** the street → **in** the city

#### Rule 5: Elimination Technique for the CSE

When unsure:
1. Read the sentence with each option.
2. Eliminate choices that sound grammatically awkward.
3. Check if a fixed collocation applies.
4. If two options remain, apply the static/dynamic or specificity rule.

### 4.15 Exam Strategies

#### Strategy 1: Listen for Natural Collocations

Read the sentence aloud in your mind. Filipino English speakers often "hear" the correct preposition even when they cannot explain the rule. Trust your ear — but verify with rules.

#### Strategy 2: Identify the Keyword

Look for signal words:
- A specific time (8:00 AM, noon) → **at**
- A day or date (Monday, June 12) → **on**
- A month, year, or long period (March, 2024, summer) → **in**
- A starting point (2020, last week) → **since**
- A duration (three hours, two years) → **for**
- A deadline (Friday, December 31) → **by**

#### Strategy 3: Check the Verb/Adjective/Noun

If the blank follows a verb, adjective, or noun, check if it has a fixed preposition:
- "comply ___" → always **with**
- "interested ___" → always **in**
- "reason ___" → always **for**

#### Strategy 4: Eliminate Unnecessary Prepositions

If the sentence already has a transitive verb that doesn't need a preposition:
- discuss (NOT discuss about)
- enter (NOT enter into, for physical entry)
- mention (NOT mention about)
- request (NOT request for)
- emphasize (NOT emphasize on)

#### Strategy 5: Between vs. Among

- Count the items. TWO = between. THREE or more = among.
- Exception: "between" for three+ when each is individually named.

### 4.16 Real CSE-Like Examples with Analysis

#### Easy

**1.** The office opens ___ 8:00 AM.
- (a) in (b) on (c) at (d) by
- **Answer: (c) at**
- **Analysis:** "At" is used for exact clock times.

**2.** The meeting is scheduled ___ Friday.
- (a) at (b) in (c) on (d) for
- **Answer: (c) on**
- **Analysis:** "On" is used for days of the week.

**3.** She has been working here ___ 2018.
- (a) for (b) since (c) from (d) at
- **Answer: (b) since**
- **Analysis:** "Since" is used with a specific starting point in time (2018). The present perfect tense confirms duration from past to present.

#### Medium

**4.** The supervisor reminded the staff ___ the new guidelines.
- (a) about (b) of (c) on (d) for
- **Answer: (b) of**
- **Analysis:** The correct collocation is "remind someone OF something."

**5.** The committee is composed ___ seven members.
- (a) with (b) by (c) of (d) from
- **Answer: (c) of**
- **Analysis:** "Composed of" is the correct collocation (similar to "consist of").

**6.** All employees must comply ___ the dress code.
- (a) to (b) in (c) for (d) with
- **Answer: (d) with**
- **Analysis:** "Comply with" is the fixed verb-preposition collocation.

#### Hard

**7.** The memorandum was issued pursuant ___ Executive Order No. 201.
- (a) with (b) to (c) of (d) in
- **Answer: (b) to**
- **Analysis:** "Pursuant to" is a fixed legal/formal phrase meaning "in accordance with."

**8.** The discrepancy ___ the two reports must be reconciled ___ the end of the fiscal year.
- (a) among / until (b) between / by (c) between / until (d) among / by
- **Answer: (b) between / by**
- **Analysis:** "Between" is correct for TWO items. "By" indicates a deadline (completed before that date).

**9.** She was absolved ___ all charges after the investigation proved her innocence.
- (a) from (b) of (c) with (d) for
- **Answer: (b) of**
- **Analysis:** "Absolve someone OF something" is the correct collocation.

- **Answer: (b) among** — Five performers = three or more.

**6.** He insisted ___ reviewing the contract himself.
- (a) in (b) for (c) on (d) to
- **Answer: (c) on** — "Insist on" is the correct collocation.

**7.** The new policy is different ___ the previous one.
- (a) with (b) to (c) than (d) from

**8.** Walk ___ the hallway and turn left at the end.

**10.** The manager spoke ___ behalf ___ the entire department.

- **Answer: (b) on / of** — "On behalf of" is a fixed phrase.

- (a) in (b) at (c) on (d) over
- **Answer: (c) on** — Floors use "on."

**13.** He apologized ___ arriving late to the meeting.
- (a) about (b) for (c) of (d) on
- **Answer: (b) for** — "Apologize for" is the correct collocation.

**14.** The budget allocation depends ___ the committee's decision.
- (a) in (b) to (c) on (d) with
- **Answer: (c) on** — "Depend on" is the correct collocation.

**15.** They discussed the matter ___ great detail.
- (a) with (b) in (c) at (d) on

**16.** The files were transferred ___ the old office ___ the new one.

- **Answer: (c) for** — "Wait for" is the correct collocation.

- **Answer: (b) during** — "During" indicates within the span of an event.

### 4.18 Quick Recap

**Major Preposition Rules:**
1. **AT** = exact times, specific points
2. **ON** = days/dates, surfaces, floors
3. **IN** = months/years/long periods, enclosed spaces, areas
4. **SINCE** = starting point (with perfect tenses)
5. **FOR** = duration/length of time
6. **BY** = deadline (completed before)
7. **UNTIL** = continuation up to a point
8. **BETWEEN** = two items; **AMONG** = three or more
9. **IN** = static location inside; **INTO** = movement inside
10. **ON** = static on surface; **ONTO** = movement to surface

**Top Collocations to Memorize:**
- depend ON, comply WITH, consist OF, interested IN, capable OF
- responsible FOR, familiar WITH, different FROM, afraid OF, good AT
- agree WITH (person), agree TO (plan), agree ON (topic)
- remind OF, apologize FOR, insist ON, believe IN, refer TO

### 4.19 Memory Aids

#### Mnemonic: "AT-ON-IN" Time Ladder

Think of specificity as a ladder — most specific at the top:
```
AT ← exact time (at 3 PM, at noon, at midnight)
ON ← day/date (on Monday, on June 12)
IN ← long period (in March, in 2024, in the morning)
```
**Memory phrase:** "**A**t the **T**ime, **ON** the day, **IN** the month."

#### Mnemonic: "AT-ON-IN" Place Scale

Think of size — smallest to largest:
```
AT ← point (at the door, at the bus stop)
ON ← surface/line (on the table, on Rizal Street)
IN ← area/volume (in the room, in Manila, in the Philippines)
```
**Memory phrase:** "**A**t a dot, **ON** a line, **IN** a box."

#### Mnemonic: SINCE vs. FOR

- **S**ince = **S**tarting point (since 2020, since Monday)
- **F**or = **F**or how long (for three hours, for two years)

#### Mnemonic: BY vs. UNTIL

- **BY** = **B**efore (deadline — action done before that time)
- **UNTIL** = **U**p to (continuous — action continues up to that time)

#### Mnemonic: BETWEEN vs. AMONG

- **B**etween = **B**oth (two things)
- **A**mong = **A** lot (three or more)

## Worked Examples

### Worked Example 1: Choosing Between Time Prepositions

**Problem:** The training will be held ___ January ___ the 15th ___ 9:00 AM.

**Step 1:** Identify what each blank refers to:
- January = month → **in**
- the 15th = specific date → **on**
- 9:00 AM = exact time → **at**

**Answer:** The training will be held **in** January **on** the 15th **at** 9:00 AM.

### Worked Example 2: Fixed Collocation

**Problem:** The success of the project depends ___ the cooperation of all departments.

**Step 1:** Identify the verb: "depends"
**Step 2:** Recall the fixed collocation: depend + **on**
**Step 3:** Verify: "depends on the cooperation" — sounds natural and correct.

**Answer:** depends **on**

### Worked Example 3: Between vs. Among

**Problem:** The budget was divided ___ the five regional offices.

**Step 1:** Count the items: five regional offices = more than two
**Step 2:** Apply the rule: three or more = **among**
**Step 3:** Verify: "divided among the five regional offices" — correct.

**Answer:** **among**

## Key Takeaways

- Prepositions show relationships of time, place, direction, and manner between words in a sentence.
- The AT/ON/IN system follows a specificity scale: AT is most specific (exact time, point), ON is medium (day/surface), IN is broadest (month/area).
- Fixed collocations (depend on, comply with, interested in, capable of) must be memorized — they do not follow predictable logic.
- Static prepositions (in, on, at) describe WHERE something IS; dynamic prepositions (into, onto, to) describe WHERE something is GOING.
- SINCE marks a starting point; FOR marks a duration. BY sets a deadline; UNTIL marks continuation.
- BETWEEN is for two; AMONG is for three or more (with exceptions for individually named items).
- Several transitive verbs (discuss, enter, mention, request, emphasize) do NOT take prepositions — adding one is a common CSE trap.
- When in doubt on the CSE, check for fixed collocations first, then apply the specificity/static-dynamic rules, then use elimination.

## Summary

Prepositions are essential connectors that establish relationships between nouns and other sentence elements. For the Civil Service Examination, mastery of prepositions requires three things: (1) understanding the systematic rules for time, place, and direction prepositions (the AT/ON/IN framework); (2) memorizing fixed collocations with adjectives, verbs, and nouns; and (3) recognizing common errors like unnecessary prepositions and confused pairs. The most frequently tested areas are AT/ON/IN usage, SINCE vs. FOR, BETWEEN vs. AMONG, and verb-preposition collocations like "comply with," "depend on," and "consist of." Apply the elimination strategy on exam day: identify the relationship type, check for fixed collocations, distinguish static from dynamic contexts, and eliminate grammatically awkward options.
