## Explanations

### What is Subject-Verb Agreement?

Subject-verb agreement is a grammar rule that requires the verb in a sentence to match its subject in number (singular or plural). A singular subject takes a singular verb; a plural subject takes a plural verb. This is one of the most frequently tested grammar concepts in the Philippine Civil Service Examination (CSE) for both Professional and Sub-Professional levels.

### Singular Subjects Take Singular Verbs

A singular subject refers to one person, place, thing, or idea. Singular verbs in English typically end in -s or -es in the present tense.

**Rule:** When the subject is singular (he, she, it, a person's name, or any single noun), use a singular verb.

Examples:
- The employee **works** overtime every Friday.
- She **submits** her report on time.
- The government **provides** public services.

Common singular subjects: each, every, everyone, everybody, everything, anyone, anybody, anything, someone, somebody, something, no one, nobody, nothing, either, neither.

### Plural Subjects Take Plural Verbs

A plural subject refers to more than one person, place, thing, or idea. Plural verbs in English do NOT end in -s in the present tense.

**Rule:** When the subject is plural (they, we, two or more nouns joined by "and"), use a plural verb.

Examples:
- The employees **work** overtime every Friday.
- They **submit** their reports on time.
- The departments **provide** various services.

### Compound Subjects

Compound subjects are two or more subjects connected by a conjunction.

**Rule 1: Subjects joined by "and"** — Use a plural verb.
- The manager and the supervisor **are** responsible for the project.
- Rice and fish **are** staple foods in the Philippines.

**Exception:** When two nouns refer to the same person or thing, use a singular verb.
- The secretary and treasurer **is** Mr. Santos. (one person holding both roles)
- Bread and butter **is** my favorite breakfast. (considered as one item)

**Rule 2: Subjects joined by "or" or "nor"** — The verb agrees with the subject NEAREST to it.
- Either the manager or the employees **are** attending the seminar.
- Either the employees or the manager **is** attending the seminar.
- Neither the teacher nor the students **were** prepared.
- Neither the students nor the teacher **was** prepared.

### Indefinite Pronouns

Indefinite pronouns can be singular, plural, or both depending on context.

**Always singular:** each, every, everyone, everybody, everything, anyone, anybody, anything, someone, somebody, something, no one, nobody, nothing, either, neither, one, another, much.
- Everyone **is** required to attend the meeting.
- Each of the applicants **has** submitted a resume.
- Neither **is** acceptable.

**Always plural:** both, few, many, several, others.
- Both **are** qualified for the position.
- Many **have** applied for the scholarship.
- Several **were** absent yesterday.

**Singular or plural (depending on the noun they refer to):** all, any, more, most, none, some.
- All of the water **is** contaminated. (water = singular/uncountable)
- All of the documents **are** ready. (documents = plural)
- Some of the information **is** incorrect. (information = singular/uncountable)
- Some of the employees **are** on leave. (employees = plural)

### Intervening Phrases and Clauses

Words or phrases that come between the subject and verb do NOT change the number of the subject.

**Rule:** Ignore prepositional phrases, relative clauses, and other modifiers between the subject and verb. The verb must agree with the actual subject, not with the nearest noun.

Common intervening phrases: together with, along with, as well as, in addition to, accompanied by, including, besides, except, no less than.

Examples:
- The list of requirements **is** posted on the bulletin board. (subject = list, singular)
- The employees, along with their supervisor, **are** attending the training. (subject = employees, plural)
- The supervisor, together with the employees, **is** attending the training. (subject = supervisor, singular)
- The quality of the products **has** improved. (subject = quality, singular)
- One of the students **has** been selected. (subject = one, singular)

**Important:** "Together with," "along with," "as well as," "in addition to," and "accompanied by" do NOT make a subject plural. Only "and" creates a compound subject.

### Collective Nouns

Collective nouns refer to a group of people or things (team, committee, family, group, class, jury, audience, staff, public, crowd, government).

**Rule:** Use a singular verb when the group acts as a single unit. Use a plural verb when members of the group act individually.

- The committee **has** reached a decision. (acting as one unit)
- The committee **have** disagreed among themselves. (acting individually)
- The staff **is** required to attend. (as a unit)
- The family **are** going their separate ways. (individually)

In Philippine CSE exams, collective nouns are almost always treated as singular unless the sentence clearly indicates individual action.

### Inverted Sentences

In inverted sentences, the subject comes AFTER the verb. You must identify the true subject to determine verb agreement.

**Common patterns:**

1. **Questions:** Does the applicant **meet** the requirements? (subject = applicant)
2. **There/Here sentences:** There **are** several options available. (subject = options)
   - There **is** a problem with the system. (subject = problem)
3. **Prepositional phrase first:** On the table **are** the documents. (subject = documents)
   - In the office **is** the new employee. (subject = employee)

**Rule for "there is/are":** Look at the noun that follows the verb to determine singular or plural.
- There **is** a pen on the desk. (pen = singular)
- There **are** pens on the desk. (pens = plural)

### Titles, Amounts, and Special Cases

**Titles of books, movies, organizations:** Always singular, even if the title contains plural words.
- "The Chronicles of Narnia" **is** a popular book series.
- The United Nations **is** headquartered in New York.
- The Philippines **is** an archipelago.

**Amounts of money, time, distance, weight:** Singular when referring to a total amount.
- Five hundred pesos **is** not enough for the seminar fee.
- Three hours **is** too long to wait.
- Ten kilometers **is** a long distance to walk.

**Plural in form but singular in meaning:** news, mathematics, physics, economics, politics, measles, mumps, athletics.
- The news **is** encouraging.
- Mathematics **is** my favorite subject.
- Politics **is** a complex field.

**Always plural:** scissors, trousers, pants, pliers, glasses (eyewear), thanks, congratulations, proceeds, goods, premises.
- The scissors **are** on the table.
- His trousers **are** too long.

### The Number vs. A Number

- **"The number of"** + plural noun takes a SINGULAR verb (because "the number" is the subject).
  - The number of applicants **has** increased.
- **"A number of"** + plural noun takes a PLURAL verb (because it means "several/many").
  - A number of applicants **have** submitted their forms.

### Relative Pronouns (Who, Which, That)

When a relative pronoun (who, which, that) is the subject of a clause, the verb agrees with the pronoun's antecedent (the word it refers to).

- The employee who **works** hard will be promoted. (who refers to employee = singular)
- The employees who **work** hard will be promoted. (who refers to employees = plural)
- She is one of the students who **have** passed. (who refers to students = plural)
- She is the only one of the students who **has** passed. (who refers to one = singular)

### Subject-Verb Agreement with "Either...Or" and "Neither...Nor" (Advanced)

When "either...or" or "neither...nor" connects subjects of different number, the verb agrees with the subject CLOSEST to it (proximity rule).

- Neither the supervisor nor the clerks **have** finished the report.
- Neither the clerks nor the supervisor **has** finished the report.
- Either you or I **am** responsible.
- Either I or you **are** responsible.

This rule is heavily tested in Professional-level CSE questions.

### Fractional and Percentage Expressions

When fractions or percentages are used as subjects, the verb agrees with the noun in the "of" phrase.

- Half of the work **is** done. (work = uncountable singular)
- Half of the employees **are** present. (employees = plural)
- Seventy percent of the budget **has** been allocated. (budget = singular)
- Seventy percent of the students **have** passed. (students = plural)
- One-third of the land **is** cultivated. (land = singular)
- Two-thirds of the members **are** in favor. (members = plural)

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Identifying the Correct Verb with Intervening Phrases

**Problem:** Choose the correct verb.
"The report, along with the supporting documents, (was/were) submitted yesterday."

**Step-by-step solution:**
1. Identify the subject: "The report" (singular)
2. Identify the intervening phrase: "along with the supporting documents" — this does NOT make the subject plural
3. Apply the rule: singular subject → singular verb
4. **Answer: "was"**

The complete sentence: "The report, along with the supporting documents, **was** submitted yesterday."

### Example 2: Compound Subjects with "Or"

**Problem:** Choose the correct verb.
"Either the department heads or the director (approve/approves) the budget."

**Step-by-step solution:**
1. Identify the compound subject: "the department heads" OR "the director"
2. Connected by "or" — use proximity rule
3. The subject nearest to the verb is "the director" (singular)
4. **Answer: "approves"**

The complete sentence: "Either the department heads or the director **approves** the budget."

### Example 3: Indefinite Pronouns

**Problem:** Choose the correct verb.
"Each of the government employees (is/are) required to file a SALN."

**Step-by-step solution:**
1. Identify the subject: "Each" (indefinite pronoun, always singular)
2. "of the government employees" is a prepositional phrase — ignore it for agreement purposes
3. Apply the rule: "each" is always singular → singular verb
4. **Answer: "is"**

The complete sentence: "Each of the government employees **is** required to file a SALN."

### Example 4: "There" Sentences (Inverted)

**Problem:** Choose the correct verb.
"There (is/are) several reasons for the policy change."

**Step-by-step solution:**
1. This is an inverted sentence starting with "There"
2. Find the real subject: "several reasons" (plural)
3. Apply the rule: plural subject → plural verb
4. **Answer: "are"**

The complete sentence: "There **are** several reasons for the policy change."

### Example 5: "The Number" vs. "A Number"

**Problem:** Choose the correct verb.
"A number of applicants (has/have) already been interviewed."

**Step-by-step solution:**
1. "A number of" means "several/many" — treated as plural
2. Apply the rule: "a number of" + plural noun → plural verb
3. **Answer: "have"**

The complete sentence: "A number of applicants **have** already been interviewed."

### Example 6: Collective Nouns

**Problem:** Choose the correct verb.
"The committee (has/have) submitted its final report."

**Step-by-step solution:**
1. "The committee" is a collective noun
2. "its final report" indicates the group is acting as a single unit
3. Apply the rule: collective noun acting as one → singular verb
4. **Answer: "has"**

The complete sentence: "The committee **has** submitted its final report."

### Example 7: Relative Pronouns

**Problem:** Choose the correct verb.
"She is one of the employees who (has/have) been promoted."

**Step-by-step solution:**
1. Identify the relative pronoun: "who"
2. Find its antecedent: "employees" (plural) — because multiple employees have been promoted, and she is one of them
3. Apply the rule: "who" refers to "employees" → plural verb
4. **Answer: "have"**

The complete sentence: "She is one of the employees who **have** been promoted."

## Key Takeaways

- The verb must always agree with the subject in number — singular subjects take singular verbs, plural subjects take plural verbs
- Ignore intervening phrases (together with, along with, as well as, in addition to) — they do NOT change the subject's number
- With "or/nor," the verb agrees with the NEAREST subject (proximity rule)
- Indefinite pronouns like each, every, everyone, anyone, someone, nobody are ALWAYS singular
- "The number of" is singular; "A number of" is plural
- Collective nouns are usually singular in CSE contexts unless individual action is clearly indicated
- In inverted sentences (there is/are, questions), identify the TRUE subject after the verb
- Titles, amounts of money/time/distance are singular when referring to a total
- With relative pronouns (who, which, that), the verb agrees with the antecedent
- Practice identifying the subject FIRST before choosing the verb — this is the most reliable strategy for CSE questions

## Summary

Subject-verb agreement is a foundational grammar rule tested extensively in both the Professional and Sub-Professional Civil Service Examinations. The core principle is simple: singular subjects require singular verbs, and plural subjects require plural verbs. However, the CSE tests this rule through complex sentence structures designed to confuse test-takers — intervening phrases, inverted word order, compound subjects with "or/nor," indefinite pronouns, and collective nouns.

The most effective exam strategy is to always identify the true subject first by stripping away prepositional phrases and modifiers, then match the verb to that subject. For "either...or" and "neither...nor" constructions, remember the proximity rule. For indefinite pronouns, memorize which are always singular (each, every, everyone, anyone, etc.) and which depend on context (all, some, most, none). Master these patterns and you will handle SVA questions with confidence on exam day.
