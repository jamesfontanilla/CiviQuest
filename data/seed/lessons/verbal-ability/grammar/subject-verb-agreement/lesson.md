# Subject-Verb Agreement

## Explanations

### Introduction

**Subject-verb agreement** is the grammar rule that requires the verb in a sentence to match its subject in number — singular subjects take singular verbs, and plural subjects take plural verbs. Despite its apparent simplicity, this rule becomes complex when sentences contain intervening phrases, inverted structures, compound subjects, indefinite pronouns, and collective nouns. It is one of the most frequently tested grammar concepts in the Philippine Civil Service Examination (CSE) at both Professional and Sub-Professional levels.

### Why Subject-Verb Agreement Matters

Subject-verb agreement is the foundation of grammatically correct English. Errors in agreement:
- Signal poor grammar competence in professional writing
- Create ambiguity about who or what is performing an action
- Undermine the credibility of official government documents
- Can change meaning entirely ("The data **shows**" vs. "The data **show**" implies different interpretations of "data")

### Why Subject-Verb Agreement Is Tested in the CSE

The Grammar and Correct Usage section of the CSE consistently tests subject-verb agreement because:
- Government memoranda, legal documents, and official correspondence demand grammatical precision
- Agreement errors are among the most common grammar mistakes Filipino professionals make
- The CSE uses complex sentence structures (intervening phrases, inverted order) to test whether examinees can identify the true subject
- Mastery of SVA demonstrates command of English sentence structure at a professional level

### Common Mistakes Examinees Make

1. Matching the verb to the nearest noun instead of the actual subject ("The list of requirements **were** posted" instead of "**was** posted")
2. Treating "along with," "together with," and "as well as" as equivalent to "and" ("The manager, together with the staff, **are**" instead of "**is**")
3. Using plural verbs with singular indefinite pronouns ("Everyone **are** required" instead of "**is** required")
4. Confusing "the number of" (singular) with "a number of" (plural)
5. Misidentifying the subject in inverted sentences ("There **is** several problems" instead of "There **are** several problems")
6. Using singular verbs with "a number of" ("A number of applicants **has** applied" instead of "**have** applied")
7. Failing to apply the proximity rule with "either...or" and "neither...nor"
8. Treating all collective nouns as plural ("The committee **have** decided" when acting as a unit)

### Learning Objectives

After this lesson, you should be able to:
- Apply the basic rule of subject-verb agreement in simple and complex sentences
- Identify the true subject by stripping away intervening phrases and modifiers
- Correctly handle compound subjects joined by "and," "or," and "nor"
- Apply agreement rules for indefinite pronouns (always singular, always plural, and context-dependent)
- Determine verb agreement with collective nouns, titles, amounts, and special nouns
- Use the proximity rule for "either...or" and "neither...nor" constructions
- Handle inverted sentences (there is/are, questions, prepositional phrases first)
- Apply agreement rules for relative pronouns, fractions, and percentages
- Solve CSE-style SVA questions with confidence under time pressure

### 4.1 The Basic Rule

#### Core Principle

A **singular subject** takes a **singular verb**; a **plural subject** takes a **plural verb**.

In the present tense, singular verbs typically end in **-s** or **-es**; plural verbs do NOT.

| Subject Type | Verb Form | Example |
|-------------|-----------|---------|
| Singular (he, she, it, one noun) | verb + -s/-es | The employee **works**. |
| Plural (they, we, two+ nouns) | base form (no -s) | The employees **work**. |

#### How to Identify Singular vs. Plural Verbs

| Verb | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| work | works | work |
| submit | submits | submit |
| have | has | have |
| do | does | do |
| be (present) | is | are |
| be (past) | was | were |

**Key Insight:** Notice the paradox — singular NOUNS usually do NOT end in -s (employee), but singular VERBS DO end in -s (works). Plural NOUNS end in -s (employees), but plural VERBS do NOT (work). This reversal confuses many test-takers.

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** The government employee ___ to work at 8:00 AM.
- (a) report (b) **reports** (c) reporting (d) reported
- *Explanation:* "The government employee" is singular → singular verb "reports."

**Medium:** The new policies ___ all government agencies nationwide.
- (a) affects (b) **affect** (c) affecting (d) has affected
- *Explanation:* "The new policies" is plural → plural verb "affect" (no -s).

**Hard:** The implementation of the revised guidelines ___ effective immediately.
- (a) are (b) **is** (c) were (d) have been
- *Explanation:* Subject is "implementation" (singular), not "guidelines." Prepositional phrase "of the revised guidelines" is a modifier — ignore it for agreement.

### 4.2 Intervening Phrases and Clauses

#### The Rule

Words, phrases, or clauses that come BETWEEN the subject and verb do NOT change the number of the subject. The verb must agree with the actual subject, not with the nearest noun.

#### Common Intervening Structures

| Phrase Type | Example | Subject | Verb |
|-------------|---------|---------|------|
| Prepositional phrase | The box **of supplies** | box (singular) | is |
| "Together with" | The director, **together with the staff**, | director (singular) | is |
| "Along with" | The report, **along with the attachments**, | report (singular) | was |
| "As well as" | The manager, **as well as the clerks**, | manager (singular) | has |
| "In addition to" | The memo, **in addition to the letters**, | memo (singular) | was |
| "Accompanied by" | The senator, **accompanied by aides**, | senator (singular) | is |
| "Including" | The package, **including all documents**, | package (singular) | has |
| Relative clause | The employee **who handles complaints** | employee (singular) | is |

**Critical Distinction:** "Together with," "along with," "as well as," "in addition to," and "accompanied by" do NOT create compound subjects. Only **"and"** creates a true compound subject that takes a plural verb.

#### Strategy: Strip the Sentence

To find the true subject, mentally remove everything between the subject and verb:

- The list ~~of requirements~~ **is** posted. ✅
- The employees, ~~along with their supervisor,~~ **are** attending. ✅
- The quality ~~of the products~~ **has** improved. ✅
- One ~~of the students~~ **has** been selected. ✅

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** The box of documents ___ on the table.
- (a) are (b) **is** (c) were (d) have been
- *Explanation:* Subject is "box" (singular). "Of documents" is a prepositional phrase — ignore it.

**Medium:** The supervisor, together with the employees, ___ attending the training.
- (a) are (b) **is** (c) were (d) have
- *Explanation:* Subject is "supervisor" (singular). "Together with the employees" does NOT make it plural.

**Hard:** The recommendations of the committee, which were submitted last month, ___ still under review by the department secretary.
- (a) **are** (b) is (c) was (d) has been
- *Explanation:* Subject is "recommendations" (plural). Both "of the committee" and "which were submitted last month" are intervening modifiers — strip them away.

### 4.3 Compound Subjects

#### Rule 1: Subjects Joined by "And" — Plural Verb

When two or more subjects are connected by "and," they form a compound subject that takes a plural verb.

- The manager and the supervisor **are** responsible for the project.
- Rice and fish **are** staple foods in the Philippines.
- The report and the memorandum **have** been submitted.
- Honesty and integrity **are** essential qualities for civil servants.

#### Exceptions to the "And" Rule

**Exception 1:** When two nouns refer to the SAME person or thing, use a singular verb.
- The secretary and treasurer **is** Mr. Santos. (one person, two roles)
- The director and chief executive **was** present. (one person)
- Bread and butter **is** my favorite breakfast. (one concept/dish)
- The long and short of it **is** that we failed. (one idea)

**How to tell:** If there is only ONE article/determiner before both nouns, they refer to the same entity:
- **The** secretary and treasurer **is**... (one person)
- **The** secretary and **the** treasurer **are**... (two people)

**Exception 2:** When "each" or "every" precedes compound subjects, use a singular verb.
- Every man and woman **is** expected to vote.
- Each student and teacher **has** a responsibility.
- Every desk and chair **was** inspected.

#### Rule 2: Subjects Joined by "Or" or "Nor" — Proximity Rule

When subjects are connected by "or," "nor," "either...or," or "neither...nor," the verb agrees with the subject NEAREST to it.

| Construction | Nearest Subject | Verb | Example |
|-------------|----------------|------|---------|
| Either A or B | B | agrees with B | Either the clerks or the manager **is** responsible. |
| Either A or B | B | agrees with B | Either the manager or the clerks **are** responsible. |
| Neither A nor B | B | agrees with B | Neither the teacher nor the students **were** prepared. |
| Neither A nor B | B | agrees with B | Neither the students nor the teacher **was** prepared. |

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** The principal and the teachers ___ present at the ceremony.
- (a) is (b) was (c) **are** (d) has been
- *Explanation:* Two subjects joined by "and" → plural verb "are."

**Medium:** Either the department heads or the director ___ the final budget.
- (a) approve (b) **approves** (c) are approving (d) have approved
- *Explanation:* "Either...or" → proximity rule. Nearest subject is "the director" (singular) → "approves."

**Hard:** Neither the memorandum nor the attachments that were requested by the auditing team ___ been received by the records section.
- (a) has (b) **have** (c) is (d) was
- *Explanation:* "Neither...nor" → proximity rule. Nearest subject is "attachments" (plural). Ignore the relative clause "that were requested by the auditing team." → "have."

### 4.4 Indefinite Pronouns

#### Always Singular

These indefinite pronouns are ALWAYS singular, regardless of what follows them:

| Pronoun | Example |
|---------|---------|
| each | Each of the applicants **has** a number. |
| every | Every employee **is** required to attend. |
| everyone / everybody | Everyone **is** present. |
| everything | Everything **was** in order. |
| anyone / anybody | Anyone **is** welcome to apply. |
| anything | Anything **is** possible. |
| someone / somebody | Someone **has** left a message. |
| something | Something **is** wrong. |
| no one / nobody | No one **was** absent. |
| nothing | Nothing **has** changed. |
| either | Either **is** acceptable. |
| neither | Neither **was** approved. |
| one | One **needs** to be careful. |
| another | Another **has** been assigned. |
| much | Much **remains** to be done. |

**Common Trap:** "Each of the employees" — the subject is "each" (singular), NOT "employees." The prepositional phrase "of the employees" is a modifier.

#### Always Plural

These indefinite pronouns are ALWAYS plural:

| Pronoun | Example |
|---------|---------|
| both | Both **are** qualified. |
| few | Few **have** applied. |
| many | Many **were** absent. |
| several | Several **have** been interviewed. |
| others | Others **are** still waiting. |

#### Context-Dependent (Singular OR Plural)

These pronouns can be singular or plural depending on the noun in the "of" phrase:

| Pronoun | Singular Context | Plural Context |
|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| all | All of the water **is** contaminated. | All of the documents **are** ready. |
| any | Any of the information **is** useful. | Any of the employees **are** eligible. |
| more | More of the work **is** needed. | More of the applicants **are** coming. |
| most | Most of the budget **has** been spent. | Most of the staff **have** arrived. |
| none | None of the evidence **is** admissible. | None of the reports **have** been filed. |
| some | Some of the food **is** spoiled. | Some of the chairs **are** broken. |

**Rule:** Look at the noun in the "of" phrase. If it's uncountable or singular → singular verb. If it's plural → plural verb.

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** Everyone in the office ___ required to attend the seminar.
- (a) are (b) **is** (c) were (d) have been
- *Explanation:* "Everyone" is always singular → "is."

**Medium:** Some of the information ___ incorrect, but some of the data ___ still usable.
- (a) are...is (b) is...are (c) **is...are** (d) are...are
- *Explanation:* "Information" is uncountable → singular "is." "Data" (in modern usage, especially in CSE context) can be treated as plural → "are." (Note: "data" is increasingly accepted as singular in informal use, but CSE typically treats it as plural.)

**Hard:** Each of the government agencies that participated in the inter-agency task force ___ required to submit a separate compliance report.
- (a) are (b) **is** (c) were (d) have been
- *Explanation:* Subject is "each" (always singular). "Of the government agencies" and "that participated in the inter-agency task force" are both modifiers — strip them away.

### 4.5 Collective Nouns

#### The Rule

Collective nouns refer to a group of people or things acting as a unit. They take a **singular verb** when the group acts as ONE unit, and a **plural verb** when members act INDIVIDUALLY.

#### Common Collective Nouns

team, committee, family, group, class, jury, audience, staff, public, crowd, government, board, council, panel, faculty, majority, minority, couple, pair, dozen

#### Singular (Group as a Unit)

- The committee **has** reached a decision.
- The team **is** preparing for the competition.
- The staff **was** informed of the changes.
- The jury **has** delivered its verdict.
- The government **is** implementing new policies.

#### Plural (Members Acting Individually)

- The committee **have** disagreed among themselves.
- The couple **are** going their separate ways.
- The staff **are** taking their individual vacations.
- The jury **are** divided in their opinions.

#### How to Determine: Unit vs. Individual

| Clue | Interpretation | Verb |
|------|---------------|------|
| "its" (singular possessive) | acting as unit | singular |
| "their" (plural possessive) | acting individually | plural |
| single action/decision | unit | singular |
| disagreement/separate actions | individual | plural |

**CSE Default:** In Philippine CSE exams, collective nouns are almost always treated as **singular** unless the sentence clearly indicates individual action. When in doubt, choose singular.

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** The committee ___ submitted its final report to the director.
- (a) have (b) **has** (c) are (d) were
- *Explanation:* "Its" signals the committee is acting as a unit → singular "has."

**Medium:** The majority of the employees ___ in favor of the new schedule.
- (a) is (b) **are** (c) was (d) has been
- *Explanation:* "The majority of the employees" — look at the "of" phrase: "employees" is plural → plural verb "are."

**Hard:** The panel of examiners, after reviewing all submissions, ___ announced that the results will be released next week.
- (a) have (b) **has** (c) are (d) were
- *Explanation:* Subject is "panel" (singular collective noun acting as a unit — one announcement). "Of examiners" and "after reviewing all submissions" are intervening modifiers.

### 4.6 Inverted Sentences

#### The Rule

In inverted sentences, the subject comes AFTER the verb. You must identify the true subject to determine agreement.

#### Pattern 1: "There Is / There Are"

"There" is NEVER the subject. The real subject follows the verb.

| Sentence | Real Subject | Verb |
|----------|-------------|------|
| There **is** a problem. | problem (singular) | is |
| There **are** several options. | options (plural) | are |
| There **was** an error. | error (singular) | was |
| There **were** many applicants. | applicants (plural) | were |
| There **has** been a change. | change (singular) | has been |
| There **have** been complaints. | complaints (plural) | have been |

**Common Error:** "There's many reasons..." ❌ → "There **are** many reasons..." ✅

#### Pattern 2: "Here Is / Here Are"

Same rule — "here" is not the subject.

- Here **is** the document you requested. (document = singular)
- Here **are** the documents you requested. (documents = plural)

#### Pattern 3: Prepositional Phrase First

When a sentence begins with a prepositional phrase, the subject follows the verb.

- On the table **are** the documents. (subject = documents, plural)
- In the office **is** the new employee. (subject = employee, singular)
- Among the applicants **was** a former government official. (subject = official, singular)
- Behind the building **are** the parking lots. (subject = lots, plural)

#### Pattern 4: Questions

In questions, the auxiliary verb comes before the subject.

- **Does** the applicant meet the requirements? (subject = applicant, singular)
- **Do** the applicants meet the requirements? (subject = applicants, plural)
- **Has** the report been submitted? (subject = report, singular)
- **Have** the reports been submitted? (subject = reports, plural)

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** There ___ several reasons for the policy change.
- (a) is (b) **are** (c) was (d) has been
- *Explanation:* Real subject is "reasons" (plural) → "are."

**Medium:** Among the documents submitted to the commission ___ a letter of complaint from a concerned citizen.
- (a) are (b) **was** (c) were (d) have been
- *Explanation:* Inverted sentence. Real subject is "a letter of complaint" (singular) → "was."

**Hard:** There ___, according to the latest audit report, several discrepancies in the financial records that need immediate attention.
- (a) is (b) **are** (c) was (d) has been
- *Explanation:* Real subject is "discrepancies" (plural). "According to the latest audit report" is an intervening phrase — ignore it.

### 4.7 "The Number of" vs. "A Number of"

#### The Rule

| Expression | Meaning | Verb | Example |
|-----------|---------|------|---------|
| **The number of** + plural noun | a specific quantity | SINGULAR | The number of applicants **has** increased. |
| **A number of** + plural noun | several / many | PLURAL | A number of applicants **have** submitted forms. |

#### Why This Works

- "**The** number" — the subject is "number" (a singular noun referring to a quantity)
- "**A** number of" — functions as a quantifier meaning "many/several," so the plural noun controls agreement

#### Memory Aid

- **THE** number = **T**otal (one number) → singular
- **A** number = **A** lot (many) → plural

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** The number of examinees ___ increasing every year.
- (a) are (b) **is** (c) were (d) have been
- *Explanation:* "The number of" → singular verb "is."

**Medium:** A number of employees ___ already submitted their leave applications for the holiday.
- (a) has (b) **have** (c) is (d) was
- *Explanation:* "A number of" = many → plural verb "have."

**Hard:** The number of complaints filed against the agency ___ risen sharply, and a number of these cases ___ already been forwarded to the Ombudsman.
- (a) have...has (b) **has...have** (c) has...has (d) have...have
- *Explanation:* "The number of complaints" → singular "has." "A number of these cases" → plural "have."

### 4.8 Relative Pronouns (Who, Which, That)

#### The Rule

When a relative pronoun (who, which, that) is the subject of a clause, the verb agrees with the pronoun's **antecedent** — the word the pronoun refers to.

#### Finding the Antecedent

| Sentence | Relative Pronoun | Antecedent | Verb |
|----------|-----------------|------------|------|
| The employee who **works** hard... | who | employee (singular) | works |
| The employees who **work** hard... | who | employees (plural) | work |
| The report that **was** submitted... | that | report (singular) | was |
| The reports that **were** submitted... | that | reports (plural) | were |

#### The Tricky "One of...Who" Construction

This is one of the most heavily tested patterns on the CSE:

**Pattern 1:** "one of the [plural noun] who [PLURAL verb]"
- She is **one of the employees** who **have** been promoted.
- (Multiple employees have been promoted; she is one of them.)
- "Who" refers to "employees" → plural verb.

**Pattern 2:** "the only one of the [plural noun] who [SINGULAR verb]"
- She is **the only one** of the employees who **has** been promoted.
- (Only one person was promoted; she is that person.)
- "Who" refers to "one" → singular verb.

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** The applicant who ___ all the requirements will be prioritized.
- (a) meet (b) **meets** (c) meeting (d) have met
- *Explanation:* "Who" refers to "applicant" (singular) → "meets."

**Medium:** He is one of the officers who ___ assigned to the new project.
- (a) is (b) was (c) **are** (d) has been
- *Explanation:* "Who" refers to "officers" (plural — multiple officers are assigned) → "are."

**Hard:** She is the only one of the candidates who ___ passed all three phases of the screening process without any deficiency.
- (a) have (b) **has** (c) are (d) were
- *Explanation:* "The only one" makes "one" the antecedent of "who" → singular "has."

### 4.9 Titles, Amounts, and Special Nouns

#### Titles — Always Singular

Titles of books, movies, organizations, and countries are always singular, even if they contain plural words.

- "The Chronicles of Narnia" **is** a popular book series.
- The United Nations **is** headquartered in New York.
- The Philippines **is** an archipelago.
- "Les Misérables" **is** a famous musical.
- The Bureau of Internal Revenue **is** a government agency.

#### Amounts — Singular When a Total

Amounts of money, time, distance, and weight take a singular verb when referring to a single total amount.

- Five hundred pesos **is** not enough for the seminar fee.
- Three hours **is** too long to wait.
- Ten kilometers **is** a long distance to walk.
- Two-thirds of the budget **has** been allocated. (budget = singular)
- Fifty percent of the work **is** complete. (work = singular)

**But:** When referring to individual units, use plural:
- Five hundred pesos **were** scattered on the floor. (individual coins/bills)

#### Plural in Form, Singular in Meaning

These nouns look plural (-s ending) but take a SINGULAR verb:

| Noun | Example |
|------|---------|
| news | The news **is** encouraging. |
| mathematics | Mathematics **is** my favorite subject. |
| physics | Physics **is** a challenging course. |
| economics | Economics **is** required for the degree. |
| politics | Politics **is** a complex field. |
| statistics (the subject) | Statistics **is** offered every semester. |
| measles | Measles **is** a contagious disease. |
| mumps | Mumps **is** preventable through vaccination. |
| athletics | Athletics **is** important for physical development. |

**Exception:** When "statistics" means "data/figures," it takes a plural verb:
- The statistics **show** an increase in enrollment. (= the figures)

#### Always Plural

These nouns always take a PLURAL verb:

| Noun | Example |
|------|---------|
| scissors | The scissors **are** on the table. |
| trousers / pants | His trousers **are** too long. |
| pliers | The pliers **are** in the toolbox. |
| glasses (eyewear) | Her glasses **are** new. |
| thanks | Thanks **are** due to the committee. |
| congratulations | Congratulations **are** in order. |
| proceeds | The proceeds **go** to charity. |
| goods | The goods **have** arrived. |
| premises | The premises **are** well-maintained. |
| earnings | Her earnings **have** increased. |

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** The news about the promotion ___ everyone happy.
- (a) make (b) **makes** (c) are making (d) have made
- *Explanation:* "News" is singular in meaning → "makes."

**Medium:** Three thousand pesos ___ the registration fee for the seminar.
- (a) are (b) **is** (c) were (d) have been
- *Explanation:* Amount as a total → singular "is."

**Hard:** The statistics released by the Philippine Statistics Authority ___ that unemployment ___ decreased by two percent.
- (a) shows...has (b) **show...has** (c) show...have (d) shows...have
- *Explanation:* "Statistics" here means "data/figures" → plural "show." "Unemployment" is singular → "has."

### 4.10 Fractional and Percentage Expressions

#### The Rule

When fractions or percentages are used as subjects, the verb agrees with the noun in the "of" phrase.

| Expression | "Of" Noun | Verb | Example |
|-----------|-----------|------|---------|
| Half of the work | work (uncountable) | singular | Half of the work **is** done. |
| Half of the employees | employees (plural) | plural | Half of the employees **are** present. |
| Seventy percent of the budget | budget (singular) | singular | Seventy percent of the budget **has** been spent. |
| Seventy percent of the students | students (plural) | plural | Seventy percent of the students **have** passed. |
| One-third of the land | land (singular) | singular | One-third of the land **is** cultivated. |
| Two-thirds of the members | members (plural) | plural | Two-thirds of the members **are** in favor. |

#### "Rest," "Remainder," "Part," and "Majority"

These follow the same rule — look at the "of" phrase:

- The rest of the money **is** in the vault. (money = uncountable)
- The rest of the employees **are** on leave. (employees = plural)
- The majority of the work **has** been completed. (work = uncountable)
- The majority of the voters **have** decided. (voters = plural)

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** Fifty percent of the work ___ already been completed.
- (a) have (b) **has** (c) are (d) were
- *Explanation:* "Work" is uncountable → singular "has."

**Medium:** Two-thirds of the employees ___ in favor of the new policy, but one-third ___ opposed.
- (a) is...are (b) **are...is** (c) are...are (d) is...is
- *Explanation:* "Employees" is plural → "are." "One-third" refers back to employees (plural), but "one-third" as a portion can be singular → "is." (In CSE, the "of" noun controls: one-third of the employees → plural "are" is also acceptable, but the singular reading of "one-third" as a single portion is standard.)

**Hard:** More than half of the budget allocated for the infrastructure projects ___ been reallocated to disaster relief efforts, while the remainder of the funds ___ been placed in a trust account.
- (a) have...has (b) **has...have** (c) has...has (d) have...have
- *Explanation:* "Half of the budget" — "budget" is singular → "has." "The remainder of the funds" — "funds" is plural → "have."

### 4.11 Special Constructions

#### "Not only...but also"

The verb agrees with the subject AFTER "but also" (proximity rule, same as "or/nor"):
- Not only the employees but also the manager **is** attending.
- Not only the manager but also the employees **are** attending.

#### "Either...or" and "Neither...nor" with Different Persons

When subjects differ in person (I, you, he/she), the verb agrees with the nearest subject:
- Either you or I **am** responsible.
- Either I or you **are** responsible.
- Neither he nor they **are** to blame.
- Neither they nor he **is** to blame.

#### Gerunds and Infinitives as Subjects

Gerund phrases and infinitive phrases used as subjects are SINGULAR:
- **Swimming** every morning **is** good exercise.
- **To err** **is** human.
- **Filing** documents on time **requires** discipline.
- **Reading** and **writing** **are** fundamental skills. (two gerunds joined by "and" = plural)

#### "What" Clauses as Subjects

"What" clauses can be singular or plural depending on the complement:
- What we need **is** more time. (complement "more time" is singular)
- What we need **are** more volunteers. (complement "more volunteers" is plural)
- What matters **is** your integrity. (singular concept)

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** Swimming in the morning ___ good for one's health.
- (a) are (b) **is** (c) were (d) have been
- *Explanation:* Gerund phrase as subject → singular "is."

**Medium:** Not only the department heads but also the undersecretary ___ expected to attend the briefing.
- (a) are (b) **is** (c) were (d) have been
- *Explanation:* "Not only...but also" → proximity rule. Nearest subject is "undersecretary" (singular) → "is."

**Hard:** What the commission requires from all applicants ___ a valid government-issued ID and two copies of the latest community tax certificate.
- (a) is (b) **are** (c) was (d) has been
- *Explanation:* "What" clause as subject. The complement is "a valid government-issued ID and two copies" (compound/plural) → "are."

### 4.12 Common SVA Errors and How to Avoid Them

#### Error 1: Matching Verb to Nearest Noun (Not the Subject)

❌ The list of requirements **were** posted on the bulletin board.
✅ The list of requirements **was** posted on the bulletin board.
- *Subject is "list" (singular), not "requirements."*

❌ The quality of the products **have** improved.
✅ The quality of the products **has** improved.
- *Subject is "quality" (singular), not "products."*

#### Error 2: Treating "Together With" as "And"

❌ The supervisor, together with the employees, **are** attending.
✅ The supervisor, together with the employees, **is** attending.
- *"Together with" does NOT create a compound subject.*

❌ The report, as well as the attachments, **were** submitted.
✅ The report, as well as the attachments, **was** submitted.

#### Error 3: Singular Indefinite Pronouns with Plural Verbs

❌ Everyone **are** required to attend the meeting.
✅ Everyone **is** required to attend the meeting.

❌ Each of the employees **have** submitted their forms.
✅ Each of the employees **has** submitted their forms.

#### Error 4: "There Is" with Plural Subjects

❌ There **is** many reasons for the delay.
✅ There **are** many reasons for the delay.

❌ There **has** been several complaints.
✅ There **have** been several complaints.

#### Error 5: Wrong Verb with "The Number" / "A Number"

❌ The number of applicants **have** increased.
✅ The number of applicants **has** increased.

❌ A number of employees **has** been promoted.
✅ A number of employees **have** been promoted.

#### Error 6: Collective Nouns Treated Incorrectly

❌ The committee **have** reached its decision. (mixed: plural verb + singular possessive)
✅ The committee **has** reached its decision. (consistent: singular verb + singular possessive)
✅ The committee **have** reached their decision. (consistent: plural verb + plural possessive)

### Exam Strategies

### Strategy 1: Find the Subject FIRST

Before looking at the answer choices, identify the true subject:
1. Cross out prepositional phrases (of, in, with, for, to, by, at, on, from, between, among)
2. Cross out phrases starting with "together with," "along with," "as well as," "in addition to," "accompanied by," "including"
3. Cross out relative clauses (who..., which..., that...)
4. What remains before the verb is your subject

### Strategy 2: Check for Inversion

If the sentence starts with "There," "Here," or a prepositional phrase:
- The subject is AFTER the verb
- Find the real subject, then match the verb

### Strategy 3: Apply the Proximity Rule for "Or/Nor"

For "either...or," "neither...nor," "not only...but also," and "or":
- Look at the subject CLOSEST to the verb
- Match the verb to THAT subject

### Strategy 4: Memorize Key Patterns

| Pattern | Verb |
|---------|------|
| "The number of" + plural noun | SINGULAR |
| "A number of" + plural noun | PLURAL |
| Each / Every / Everyone / Anyone / Someone / Nobody | SINGULAR |
| Both / Few / Many / Several | PLURAL |
| All / Some / Most / None + uncountable noun | SINGULAR |
| All / Some / Most / None + plural noun | PLURAL |
| Collective noun (acting as unit) | SINGULAR |
| Fraction/Percent + uncountable noun | SINGULAR |
| Fraction/Percent + plural noun | PLURAL |

### Strategy 5: Elimination Technique

1. Identify the subject and its number (singular or plural)
2. Eliminate all choices that don't match in number
3. Among remaining choices, eliminate those with wrong tense (if context provides time clues)
4. Choose the answer that matches both number and tense

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---------|-------|---------|------|
| Verb matches nearest noun | The list of items **were** checked. | The list of items **was** checked. | Subject = "list" (singular) |
| "Together with" = "and" | She, together with her friends, **are** going. | She, together with her friends, **is** going. | "Together with" ≠ compound subject |
| Singular indefinite + plural verb | Everyone **are** present. | Everyone **is** present. | "Everyone" is always singular |
| "A number of" + singular verb | A number of students **has** enrolled. | A number of students **have** enrolled. | "A number of" = many → plural |
| "There is" + plural subject | There **is** many problems. | There **are** many problems. | Subject is "problems" (plural) |
| Wrong proximity rule | Neither he nor I **is** going. | Neither he nor I **am** going. | Nearest subject = "I" → "am" |
| Collective noun mismatch | The team **have** won its game. | The team **has** won its game. | "Its" = unit → singular verb |

### Memory Aids

### Mnemonic: "THE = Total, A = A lot"
- **The** number of applicants **has** increased. (THE = one total number → singular)
- **A** number of applicants **have** applied. (A = a lot/many → plural)

### Mnemonic: "EACH = ONE at a time"
- Each, every, everyone, everybody, anyone, anybody, someone, somebody, no one, nobody → always ONE → always singular

### Mnemonic: "Strip and Match"
1. **Strip** — Remove all phrases between subject and verb
2. **Match** — Match the naked subject to the verb

### Mnemonic: "CLOSEST Wins" (for or/nor)
- Either A or **B** → verb matches **B**
- Neither A nor **B** → verb matches **B**

### Recognition Trick: Singular vs. Plural Verb Paradox
- Singular NOUN: no -s (employee) → Singular VERB: has -s (works)
- Plural NOUN: has -s (employees) → Plural VERB: no -s (work)
- Remember: "Nouns and verbs are OPPOSITES with -s"

### Example 1: Identifying the Correct Verb with Intervening Phrases

**Problem:** Choose the correct verb.
"The report, along with the supporting documents, (was/were) submitted yesterday."

**Step-by-step solution:**
1. Identify the subject: "The report" (singular)
2. Identify the intervening phrase: "along with the supporting documents" — this does NOT make the subject plural
3. Apply the rule: singular subject → singular verb
4. **Answer: "was"**

The complete sentence: "The report, along with the supporting documents, **was** submitted yesterday."

---

### Example 2: Compound Subjects with "Or"

**Problem:** Choose the correct verb.
"Either the department heads or the director (approve/approves) the budget."

**Step-by-step solution:**
1. Identify the compound subject: "the department heads" OR "the director"
2. Connected by "or" — use proximity rule
3. The subject nearest to the verb is "the director" (singular)
4. **Answer: "approves"**

The complete sentence: "Either the department heads or the director **approves** the budget."

---

### Example 3: Indefinite Pronouns

**Problem:** Choose the correct verb.
"Each of the government employees (is/are) required to file a SALN."

**Step-by-step solution:**
1. Identify the subject: "Each" (indefinite pronoun, always singular)
2. "of the government employees" is a prepositional phrase — ignore it for agreement purposes
3. Apply the rule: "each" is always singular → singular verb
4. **Answer: "is"**

The complete sentence: "Each of the government employees **is** required to file a SALN."

### Example 4: "There" Sentences (Inverted)

**Problem:** Choose the correct verb.
"There (is/are) several reasons for the policy change."

**Step-by-step solution:**
1. This is an inverted sentence starting with "There"
2. Find the real subject: "several reasons" (plural)
3. Apply the rule: plural subject → plural verb
4. **Answer: "are"**

The complete sentence: "There **are** several reasons for the policy change."

### Example 5: "The Number" vs. "A Number"

**Problem:** Choose the correct verb.
"A number of applicants (has/have) already been interviewed."

**Step-by-step solution:**
1. "A number of" means "several/many" — treated as plural
2. Apply the rule: "a number of" + plural noun → plural verb
3. **Answer: "have"**

The complete sentence: "A number of applicants **have** already been interviewed."

### Example 6: Collective Nouns

**Problem:** Choose the correct verb.
"The committee (has/have) submitted its final report."

**Step-by-step solution:**
1. "The committee" is a collective noun
2. "its final report" indicates the group is acting as a single unit
3. Apply the rule: collective noun acting as one → singular verb
4. **Answer: "has"**

The complete sentence: "The committee **has** submitted its final report."

### Example 7: Relative Pronouns

**Problem:** Choose the correct verb.
"She is one of the employees who (has/have) been promoted."

**Step-by-step solution:**
1. Identify the relative pronoun: "who"
2. Find its antecedent: "employees" (plural) — because multiple employees have been promoted, and she is one of them
3. Apply the rule: "who" refers to "employees" → plural verb
4. **Answer: "have"**

The complete sentence: "She is one of the employees who **have** been promoted."

## Key Takeaways

- The verb must always agree with the subject in number — singular subjects take singular verbs, plural subjects take plural verbs
- Ignore intervening phrases (together with, along with, as well as, in addition to) — they do NOT change the subject's number
- With "or/nor," the verb agrees with the NEAREST subject (proximity rule)
- Indefinite pronouns like each, every, everyone, anyone, someone, nobody are ALWAYS singular
- "The number of" is singular; "A number of" is plural
- Collective nouns are usually singular in CSE contexts unless individual action is clearly indicated
- In inverted sentences (there is/are, questions), identify the TRUE subject after the verb
- Titles, amounts of money/time/distance are singular when referring to a total
- With relative pronouns (who, which, that), the verb agrees with the antecedent
- For fractions and percentages, look at the noun in the "of" phrase to determine singular or plural
- Practice identifying the subject FIRST before choosing the verb — this is the most reliable strategy for CSE questions

## Summary

Subject-verb agreement is a foundational grammar rule tested extensively in both the Professional and Sub-Professional Civil Service Examinations. The core principle is simple: singular subjects require singular verbs, and plural subjects require plural verbs. However, the CSE tests this rule through complex sentence structures designed to confuse test-takers — intervening phrases, inverted word order, compound subjects with "or/nor," indefinite pronouns, and collective nouns.

The most effective exam strategy is to always identify the true subject first by stripping away prepositional phrases and modifiers, then match the verb to that subject. For "either...or" and "neither...nor" constructions, remember the proximity rule. For indefinite pronouns, memorize which are always singular (each, every, everyone, anyone, etc.) and which depend on context (all, some, most, none). Master these patterns and you will handle SVA questions with confidence on exam day.
