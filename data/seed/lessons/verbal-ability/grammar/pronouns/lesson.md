# Pronouns

## Explanations

### Introduction

**Pronouns** are words that take the place of nouns in a sentence. They prevent repetitive use of nouns and make communication smoother, clearer, and more natural. In the Philippine Civil Service Examination (CSE), pronoun questions appear consistently in the Grammar and Correct Usage section at both Professional and Sub-Professional levels.

### Why Pronouns Matter

Pronouns are essential building blocks of English grammar because they:
- Replace nouns to avoid awkward repetition ("Maria submitted Maria's report" becomes "Maria submitted her report")
- Create cohesion between sentences and paragraphs
- Signal relationships between ideas (who does what to whom)
- Reflect formality and precision in professional writing

### Why Pronouns Are Tested in the CSE

The Civil Service Examination tests pronouns because:
- Government documents require absolute clarity about who is responsible for what
- Pronoun errors in official memoranda can create legal ambiguity
- Correct pronoun usage signals professional competence in written English
- Pronoun-antecedent agreement is one of the most error-prone areas for Filipino English writers

### Common Mistakes Examinees Make

1. Confusing subject and object pronouns ("Him and me went" instead of "He and I went")
2. Pronoun-antecedent disagreement ("Everyone must submit their form" — singular vs. plural)
3. Ambiguous pronoun references ("John told Mark that he was promoted" — who is "he"?)
4. Misusing reflexive pronouns ("Please submit the form to myself" instead of "to me")
5. Confusing who/whom ("Who did you give it to?" vs. "To whom did you give it?")
6. Wrong possessive forms ("it's" vs. "its", "your" vs. "you're")

---

### Learning Objectives

After this lesson, you should be able to:
- Identify all types of pronouns and their grammatical functions
- Apply correct pronoun case (subject, object, possessive) in any sentence structure
- Recognize and fix pronoun-antecedent agreement errors
- Distinguish who from whom in formal and informal contexts
- Detect and correct ambiguous pronoun references
- Solve CSE-style pronoun questions with confidence using elimination strategies

### 4.1 What Are Pronouns?

#### Definition

A **pronoun** is a word that replaces a noun (or noun phrase) to avoid repetition. The noun that a pronoun replaces or refers to is called its **antecedent**.

#### Function in Sentences

Pronouns can serve every role that a noun can:
- **Subject:** *She* submitted the report. (replaces "The employee")
- **Object:** The supervisor called *him*. (replaces "the clerk")
- **Possessive:** That desk is *hers*. (replaces "Maria's")
- **Object of preposition:** Give the memo to *them*. (replaces "the staff")

#### The Antecedent Relationship

The **antecedent** is the noun that a pronoun refers back to. Clear pronoun-antecedent relationships are critical for readable writing.

**Clear:**
- **Director Santos** reviewed the budget. **She** approved it immediately.
  - Antecedent of "She" = Director Santos
  - Antecedent of "it" = the budget

**Unclear (ambiguous):**
- Director Santos told Supervisor Reyes that **she** would handle the report.
  - Who is "she"? Santos or Reyes? This is a pronoun reference error.

#### Types of Pronouns Overview

| Type | Examples | Function |
|------|----------|----------|
| Personal | I, you, he, she, it, we, they | Replace specific nouns |
| Possessive | mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs | Show ownership |
| Reflexive | myself, yourself, himself, herself | Reflect action back to subject |
| Intensive | myself, yourself (for emphasis) | Emphasize a noun |
| Demonstrative | this, that, these, those | Point to specific things |
| Interrogative | who, whom, whose, which, what | Ask questions |
| Relative | who, whom, whose, which, that | Introduce dependent clauses |
| Indefinite | everyone, somebody, each, neither | Refer to nonspecific persons/things |
| Reciprocal | each other, one another | Express mutual action |

### 4.2 Personal Pronouns

Personal pronouns refer to specific people or things. They change form based on **person**, **number**, and **case**.

#### The Three Cases

| Case | Function | Question It Answers |
|------|----------|-------------------|
| **Subjective (Nominative)** | Subject of verb | Who/what does the action? |
| **Objective (Accusative)** | Object of verb/preposition | Who/what receives the action? |
| **Possessive (Genitive)** | Shows ownership | Whose is it? |

#### Complete Personal Pronoun Chart

| Person | Subject | Object | Possessive Adjective | Possessive Pronoun |
|--------|---------|--------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 1st singular | I | me | my | mine |
| 2nd singular | you | you | your | yours |
| 3rd singular (m) | he | him | his | his |
| 3rd singular (f) | she | her | her | hers |
| 3rd singular (n) | it | it | its | its |
| 1st plural | we | us | our | ours |
| 2nd plural | you | you | your | yours |
| 3rd plural | they | them | their | theirs |

#### Subject Pronouns — When to Use

Use subject pronouns when the pronoun is:

1. **The subject of a verb:**
   - **I** submitted the report on time.
   - **They** attended the orientation seminar.

2. **A predicate nominative (after linking verbs like "is," "was," "are"):**
   - The winner of the award is **she**. (formal)
   - It was **he** who filed the complaint. (formal)
   - *Note:* In informal speech, "It's me" is accepted, but the CSE tests formal grammar.

3. **Part of a compound subject:**
   - **She and I** prepared the presentation. (NOT "Her and me")
   - **He and the director** signed the memorandum.

**Trick for compound subjects:** Remove the other person and test:
- "Her prepared the presentation" — sounds wrong → use "She"
- "Me prepared the presentation" — sounds wrong → use "I"

#### Object Pronouns — When to Use

Use object pronouns when the pronoun is:

1. **Direct object (receives the action):**
   - The supervisor praised **her** for the excellent work.
   - The committee interviewed **them** yesterday.

2. **Indirect object (to/for whom the action is done):**
   - The director gave **him** a commendation.
   - Please send **us** the updated schedule.

3. **Object of a preposition:**
   - The memo was addressed to **her**.
   - Between you and **me**, the policy needs revision. (NOT "between you and I")

4. **Part of a compound object:**
   - The manager assigned the project to **him and me**. (NOT "he and I")
   - They invited **her and me** to the conference. (NOT "she and I")

**Trick for compound objects:** Remove the other person and test:
- "assigned the project to I" — sounds wrong → use "me"
- "invited I to the conference" — sounds wrong → use "me"

#### Common Errors with Personal Pronouns

| Error | Correction | Rule |
|-------|-----------|------|
| ❌ Him and me went to the meeting. | ✅ He and I went to the meeting. | Subject position → subject pronouns |
| ❌ Between you and I, this is wrong. | ✅ Between you and me, this is wrong. | After preposition → object pronoun |
| ❌ The award was given to she and I. | ✅ The award was given to her and me. | Object of preposition → object pronouns |
| ❌ Us employees deserve a raise. | ✅ We employees deserve a raise. | Subject position → subject pronoun |
| ❌ The director called we employees. | ✅ The director called us employees. | Object position → object pronoun |

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** The supervisor assigned the task to ___ and my colleague.
- (a) I (b) **me** (c) myself (d) mine
- *Explanation:* "To" is a preposition; object of preposition requires object pronoun "me."

**Medium:** Neither the manager nor ___ was informed about the policy change.
- (a) **I** (b) me (c) myself (d) mine
- *Explanation:* The pronoun is the subject of "was informed" → subject pronoun "I."

**Hard:** The committee determined that the most qualified applicant was ___.
- (a) him (b) **he** (c) himself (d) his
- *Explanation:* After the linking verb "was," a predicate nominative requires the subject pronoun "he" in formal grammar.

### 4.3 Possessive Pronouns

Possessive pronouns show ownership WITHOUT needing a noun after them. They stand alone.

#### Possessive Pronouns vs. Possessive Adjectives

| Possessive Adjective (before noun) | Possessive Pronoun (stands alone) |
|-------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| This is **my** report. | This report is **mine**. |
| That is **your** desk. | That desk is **yours**. |
| She submitted **her** form. | The form she submitted is **hers**. |
| He finished **his** task. | The finished task is **his**. |
| The dog wagged **its** tail. | — (no standalone "its") |
| We completed **our** project. | The completed project is **ours**. |
| They submitted **their** requirements. | The submitted requirements are **theirs**. |

#### Critical Rule: NO APOSTROPHES in Possessive Pronouns

Possessive pronouns NEVER use apostrophes. This is one of the most tested rules:

| Correct (Possessive) | Incorrect | What the Incorrect Form Actually Means |
|---------------------|-----------|---------------------------------------|
| **its** (belonging to it) | it's | it is / it has |
| **your** (belonging to you) | you're | you are |
| **their** (belonging to them) | they're | they are |
| **whose** (belonging to whom) | who's | who is / who has |

**CSE Trap Examples:**

- ❌ The agency must review **it's** policies annually.
- ✅ The agency must review **its** policies annually.

- ❌ **Your** expected to submit the report by Friday.
- ✅ **You're** expected to submit the report by Friday.

- ❌ **Their** going to the seminar tomorrow.
- ✅ **They're** going to the seminar tomorrow.

- ❌ The employee **who's** performance was outstanding received an award.
- ✅ The employee **whose** performance was outstanding received an award.

#### Usage Patterns

1. **After linking verbs (ownership):**
   - The responsibility is **ours**, not theirs.
   - That corner office is **hers**.

2. **In comparisons:**
   - My report is longer than **yours**.
   - Their department's budget is larger than **ours**.

3. **Double possessive (of + possessive pronoun):**
   - A colleague of **mine** recommended the training.
   - That idea of **his** was brilliant.

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** The employees must submit ___ weekly reports every Friday.
- (a) **their** (b) they're (c) there (d) theirs
- *Explanation:* A possessive adjective is needed before the noun "reports." "Their" is the possessive adjective; "they're" = they are; "there" = location.

**Medium:** The office renovation is complete; the new workspace is now ___.
- (a) our (b) **ours** (c) us (d) we
- *Explanation:* After the linking verb "is," a possessive pronoun standing alone is needed → "ours."

**Hard:** The department ___ budget was cut must submit a revised proposal.
- (a) who's (b) **whose** (c) which (d) that
- *Explanation:* "Whose" is the possessive relative pronoun showing the budget belongs to the department. "Who's" = who is.

### 4.4 Reflexive Pronouns

Reflexive pronouns end in **-self** (singular) or **-selves** (plural). They "reflect" the action back to the subject.

#### Complete List

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | myself | ourselves |
| 2nd | yourself | yourselves |
| 3rd (m) | himself | themselves |
| 3rd (f) | herself | themselves |
| 3rd (n) | itself | themselves |

#### When to Use Reflexive Pronouns

1. **When the subject and object are the same person:**
   - She prepared **herself** for the examination.
   - The director excused **himself** from the meeting.
   - We pride **ourselves** on excellent public service.

2. **After certain verbs that require reflexive objects:**
   - Please help **yourself** to the refreshments.
   - He busied **himself** with the paperwork.
   - They enjoyed **themselves** at the team-building event.

3. **After prepositions when referring back to the subject:**
   - She kept the information to **herself**.
   - He was angry at **himself** for the mistake.

#### When NOT to Use Reflexive Pronouns

**CRITICAL:** Reflexive pronouns cannot replace subject or object pronouns when the subject and object are different people.

| Error | Correction | Why |
|-------|-----------|-----|
| ❌ Please submit the form to **myself**. | ✅ Please submit the form to **me**. | "You" (subject) ≠ "me" (object) — different people |
| ❌ **Myself** and the director will attend. | ✅ The director and **I** will attend. | Subject position needs subject pronoun |
| ❌ The memo was sent to the manager and **myself**. | ✅ The memo was sent to the manager and **me**. | Object of preposition; subject ≠ object |
| ❌ Contact either the supervisor or **myself**. | ✅ Contact either the supervisor or **me**. | Object position; different people involved |

**The Rule:** Use a reflexive pronoun ONLY when the subject and the receiver of the action are the SAME entity.

#### Common Nonstandard Forms to Avoid

| Nonstandard | Standard |
|-------------|----------|
| ❌ hisself | ✅ himself |
| ❌ theirself | ✅ themselves |
| ❌ theirselves | ✅ themselves |
| ❌ ourself | ✅ ourselves |

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** The new employee introduced ___ to the team.
- (a) him (b) **himself** (c) his (d) he
- *Explanation:* The subject "employee" and the object are the same person → reflexive pronoun "himself."

**Medium:** Please forward the documents to the director and ___.
- (a) myself (b) **me** (c) I (d) mine
- *Explanation:* The subject is "you" (implied), the object is "me" — different people, so reflexive "myself" is incorrect. Use object pronoun "me."

**Hard:** The committee members congratulated ___ on the successful audit.
- (a) theirselves (b) **themselves** (c) theirself (d) them
- *Explanation:* Subject and object are the same (committee members congratulated committee members) → reflexive "themselves." "Theirselves" is nonstandard.

### 4.5 Demonstrative Pronouns

Demonstrative pronouns **point to** specific things. They indicate proximity (near or far) and number (singular or plural).

#### The Four Demonstrative Pronouns

| | Singular | Plural |
|--|----------|--------|
| **Near** (close in space/time) | this | these |
| **Far** (distant in space/time) | that | those |

#### Usage

1. **Pointing to physical objects:**
   - **This** is my workstation. (near)
   - **That** is the director's office. (far)
   - **These** are the approved forms. (near, plural)
   - **Those** are the archived documents. (far, plural)

2. **Referring to ideas or statements:**
   - The budget was cut by 20%. **This** will affect all departments.
   - She said the deadline was moved. **That** surprised everyone.

3. **Referring to time:**
   - **This** is going to be a productive week. (current/upcoming)
   - **That** was the most difficult exam I ever took. (past)

#### Demonstrative Pronouns vs. Demonstrative Adjectives

- **Pronoun (stands alone):** **This** is important.
- **Adjective (before a noun):** **This** memo is important.

#### Common Errors

| Error | Correction | Rule |
|-------|-----------|------|
| ❌ **This** books are mine. | ✅ **These** books are mine. | Plural noun → plural demonstrative |
| ❌ **Those** is the correct answer. | ✅ **That** is the correct answer. | Singular reference → singular demonstrative |
| ❌ I prefer **these** kind of tasks. | ✅ I prefer **this** kind of tasks. | "Kind" is singular → singular demonstrative |

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** ___ are the documents you requested last week.
- (a) This (b) That (c) **These** (d) It
- *Explanation:* "Documents" is plural and presumably being handed over (near) → "These."

**Medium:** The new policy is stricter than ___ implemented last year.
- (a) this (b) these (c) **that** (d) those
- *Explanation:* Refers to a single policy (singular) from the past (far) → "that."

### 4.6 Interrogative Pronouns

Interrogative pronouns are used to ask questions. They inquire about people, things, or possession.

#### The Five Interrogative Pronouns

| Pronoun | Asks About | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **who** | person (subject) | **Who** submitted the report? |
| **whom** | person (object) | **Whom** did you call? |
| **whose** | possession | **Whose** signature is this? |
| **which** | choice among limited options | **Which** form should I use? |
| **what** | thing/information (open-ended) | **What** is the deadline? |

#### Who vs. Whom (Interrogative)

**Who** = subject (does the action)
**Whom** = object (receives the action)

**Substitution Trick:**
- If you can answer with "he/she/they" → use **who**
- If you can answer with "him/her/them" → use **whom**

| Question | Test | Answer |
|----------|------|--------|
| **Who** called the meeting? | He called it. → "he" works | ✅ who |
| **Whom** did you invite? | I invited him. → "him" works | ✅ whom |
| **Who** is responsible? | She is responsible. → "she" works | ✅ who |
| To **whom** was the memo addressed? | It was addressed to her. → "her" works | ✅ whom |

#### Which vs. What

- **Which** — limited, defined set of options:
  - **Which** department handles procurement? (among known departments)
- **What** — open-ended, unlimited possibilities:
  - **What** is your position? (could be anything)

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** ___ is responsible for filing the quarterly report?
- (a) Whom (b) **Who** (c) Whose (d) Which
- *Explanation:* The pronoun is the subject of "is responsible" → subject form "Who."

**Medium:** ___ did the director assign to lead the project?
- (a) Who (b) **Whom** (c) Whose (d) Which
- *Explanation:* "The director" is the subject; the pronoun is the object of "assign" → "Whom." Test: "The director assigned him" → "him" works → whom.

**Hard:** ___ of the two proposals do you think the committee will approve?
- (a) What (b) **Which** (c) Who (d) Whom
- *Explanation:* "Of the two proposals" indicates a limited set → "Which."

### 4.7 Relative Pronouns

Relative pronouns introduce **dependent (relative) clauses** that modify a noun. They connect additional information to the main clause.

#### The Relative Pronouns

| Pronoun | Used For | Example |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **who** | people (subject) | The employee **who** filed the report was promoted. |
| **whom** | people (object) | The applicant **whom** we interviewed was qualified. |
| **whose** | possession (people/things) | The officer **whose** record is clean was commended. |
| **which** | things/animals | The memo, **which** was issued yesterday, clarified the policy. |
| **that** | people or things (restrictive) | The form **that** you submitted is incomplete. |

#### Restrictive vs. Nonrestrictive Clauses

**Restrictive (essential)** — no commas, uses "that" or "who":
- The employees **who passed** the exam will be promoted.
  - (Only those who passed — the clause identifies which employees)

**Nonrestrictive (extra info)** — commas required, uses "which" or "who":
- The employees, **who passed** the exam, will be promoted.
  - (All employees passed — the clause adds extra information)

#### Key Rules

| Rule | Example |
|------|---------|
| Use **that** for restrictive clauses (things) | The policy **that** was revised is now effective. |
| Use **which** for nonrestrictive clauses (things) | The policy, **which** was revised last month, is now effective. |
| Use **who/whom** for people | The clerk **who** processed the application was efficient. |
| Use **whose** for possession | The department **whose** budget was cut must adjust. |

#### Who vs. Whom in Relative Clauses

Apply the same subject/object test:
- The employee **who** submitted the report... (he submitted → who)
- The employee **whom** the director praised... (the director praised him → whom)

#### Common Errors

| Error | Correction | Rule |
|-------|-----------|------|
| ❌ The employee which was hired... | ✅ The employee **who** was hired... | People → who/whom |
| ❌ The report who was submitted... | ✅ The report **that/which** was submitted... | Things → that/which |
| ❌ The officer that's record is clean... | ✅ The officer **whose** record is clean... | Possession → whose |

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** The government employee ___ performance was outstanding received a commendation.
- (a) who (b) whom (c) **whose** (d) which
- *Explanation:* The pronoun shows possession (the performance belongs to the employee) → "whose."

**Medium:** The memorandum, ___ was circulated last Monday, contains the new guidelines.
- (a) that (b) **which** (c) who (d) whom
- *Explanation:* Nonrestrictive clause (set off by commas) referring to a thing → "which."

**Hard:** The applicant ___ the panel determined to be most qualified was offered the position.
- (a) who (b) **whom** (c) whose (d) which
- *Explanation:* "The panel" is the subject of "determined"; the pronoun is the object → "whom." Test: "The panel determined him to be qualified" → "him" works → whom.

### 4.8 Indefinite Pronouns

Indefinite pronouns refer to **nonspecific** people or things. They are critical for the CSE because their **number** (singular or plural) determines verb and pronoun agreement.

#### Singular Indefinite Pronouns (ALWAYS singular)

These take singular verbs and singular pronoun references:

| Always Singular |
|----------------|
| everyone, everybody, everything |
| someone, somebody, something |
| anyone, anybody, anything |
| no one, nobody, nothing |
| each, every |
| either, neither |
| one, another |
| much, little |

**Examples:**
- **Everyone** has submitted **his or her** application. (NOT "their")
- **Each** of the employees **is** responsible for **his or her** own workstation.
- **Neither** of the candidates **was** qualified.
- **Somebody** left **his or her** ID at the front desk.

#### Plural Indefinite Pronouns (ALWAYS plural)

These take plural verbs and plural pronoun references:

| Always Plural |
|--------------|
| both |
| few |
| many |
| several |
| others |

**Examples:**
- **Both** of the applicants **have** submitted **their** requirements.
- **Many** of the employees **were** absent.
- **Several** have already completed **their** training.
- **Few** understand the complexity of **their** responsibilities.

#### Indefinite Pronouns That Can Be Singular OR Plural

These depend on the noun in the prepositional phrase that follows:

| Singular or Plural |
|-------------------|
| all |
| any |
| most |
| none |
| some |

**Singular (uncountable noun):**
- **All** of the budget **has** been allocated.
- **Some** of the information **is** confidential.
- **None** of the equipment **was** damaged.

**Plural (countable noun):**
- **All** of the employees **have** been notified.
- **Some** of the documents **are** missing.
- **None** of the applicants **were** disqualified.

#### Agreement Rules Summary

| Antecedent Type | Pronoun Agreement | Example |
|----------------|-------------------|---------|
| everyone, each, etc. | his or her (singular) | Each must submit **his or her** form. |
| both, many, several | their (plural) | Both submitted **their** reports. |
| all, some, none + uncountable | its (singular) | All of the water lost **its** clarity. |
| all, some, none + countable | their (plural) | All of the staff submitted **their** forms. |

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** ___ of the employees was absent during the fire drill.
- (a) Both (b) Many (c) **One** (d) Several
- *Explanation:* The verb "was" is singular, so the subject must be singular → "One."

**Medium:** Each of the department heads must submit ___ annual report by December.
- (a) their (b) **his or her** (c) its (d) our
- *Explanation:* "Each" is singular → requires singular pronoun "his or her."

**Hard:** None of the documents ___ been signed by the authorized officer.
- (a) **has** (b) have (c) having (d) had been
- *Explanation:* "Documents" is countable and plural, so "None of the documents" takes a plural verb "have." However, traditional formal grammar (tested in CSE) often treats "none" as singular ("not one") → "has." The CSE typically accepts "has" for "none."

### 4.9 Reciprocal Pronouns

Reciprocal pronouns express **mutual action** — two or more people doing the same thing to one another.

#### The Two Reciprocal Pronouns

| Pronoun | Usage |
|---------|-------|
| **each other** | two people/things |
| **one another** | three or more people/things |

*Note:* In modern usage, many grammarians accept them interchangeably, but the CSE may test the traditional distinction.

#### Examples

- The two supervisors congratulated **each other** on the successful audit.
- The five department heads communicated with **one another** regularly.
- The manager and the clerk helped **each other** with the filing.
- All team members must respect **one another** in the workplace.

#### Possessive Form

Reciprocal pronouns can be possessive:
- They reviewed **each other's** reports. (NOT "each others'")
- The departments shared **one another's** resources.

#### Common Errors

| Error | Correction |
|-------|-----------|
| ❌ The two colleagues helped **one another**. | ✅ The two colleagues helped **each other**. |
| ❌ They reviewed **each others** work. | ✅ They reviewed **each other's** work. |
| ❌ The partners blamed **themselves**. (if mutual) | ✅ The partners blamed **each other**. |

#### CSE-Style Example

**Medium:** The members of the five-person committee consulted ___ before making a decision.
- (a) each other (b) **one another** (c) themselves (d) theirselves
- *Explanation:* Five people (more than two) performing mutual action → "one another."

### 4.10 Intensive Pronouns

Intensive pronouns have the **same form** as reflexive pronouns (-self/-selves) but serve a different purpose: they **emphasize** a noun or pronoun. They can be removed without changing the sentence's meaning.

#### How to Identify Intensive vs. Reflexive

| Type | Can Be Removed? | Example |
|------|----------------|---------|
| **Intensive** (emphasis) | Yes — sentence still works | The director **herself** approved the memo. |
| **Reflexive** (object = subject) | No — sentence breaks | The director hurt **herself**. |

**Test:** Remove the -self pronoun. If the sentence still makes grammatical sense, it's intensive.
- "The director approved the memo." ✅ Still works → **intensive**
- "The director hurt." ❌ Incomplete → **reflexive**

#### Usage Patterns

1. **Immediately after the noun (most common):**
   - The president **himself** signed the executive order.
   - The employees **themselves** organized the event.

2. **At the end of the clause:**
   - I prepared the entire report **myself**.
   - She completed the audit **herself**.

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** The mayor ___ attended the ribbon-cutting ceremony.
- (a) him (b) his (c) **himself** (d) he
- *Explanation:* The pronoun emphasizes "the mayor" — it's intensive. "Himself" adds emphasis that the mayor personally attended.

**Medium:** The documents were prepared by the staff, but the director reviewed them ___.
- (a) **herself** (b) her (c) hers (d) she
- *Explanation:* "Herself" emphasizes that the director personally did the reviewing — intensive use at end of clause.

### 4.11 Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement

This is the **most heavily tested** pronoun concept on the CSE. A pronoun must agree with its antecedent in **number** (singular/plural), **person** (1st/2nd/3rd), and **gender** (masculine/feminine/neutral).

#### Rule 1: Singular Antecedents Take Singular Pronouns

- The **employee** must submit **his or her** timesheet. (NOT "their")
- **Each student** is responsible for **his or her** own materials.
- The **company** released **its** annual report. (NOT "their")

#### Rule 2: Plural Antecedents Take Plural Pronouns

- The **employees** must submit **their** timesheets.
- **Both candidates** presented **their** platforms.
- The **departments** exceeded **their** targets.

#### Rule 3: Compound Antecedents Joined by "and" — Plural

- **The director and the supervisor** submitted **their** evaluations.
- **Juan and Maria** completed **their** training.

#### Rule 4: Compound Antecedents Joined by "or" / "nor" — Match the Nearest

- Either the **manager** or the **employees** must adjust **their** schedules.
- Either the **employees** or the **manager** must adjust **his or her** schedule.
- Neither **Juan** nor **Pedro** submitted **his** report on time.

**Rule:** The pronoun agrees with the antecedent CLOSEST to it.

#### Rule 5: Collective Nouns — Singular or Plural Based on Meaning

**Singular (acting as one unit):**
- The **committee** announced **its** decision.
- The **team** submitted **its** final report.

**Plural (acting as individuals):**
- The **committee** could not agree among **themselves**.
- The **team** went to **their** respective stations.

#### Rule 6: Indefinite Pronoun Antecedents

- **Everyone** must bring **his or her** own lunch. (singular)
- **Several** of the staff brought **their** laptops. (plural)
- **Each** of the boys did **his** best. (singular)

#### Tricky Patterns

| Pattern | Correct | Why |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Every + singular noun | Every **student** must submit **his or her** work. | "Every" makes it singular |
| Each + singular noun | Each **officer** filed **his or her** report. | "Each" is always singular |
| One of the + plural noun | One of the **employees** lost **his or her** badge. | "One" is the antecedent, not "employees" |
| The number of | **The number** of applicants **has** increased. | "Number" is singular with "the" |
| A number of | **A number** of applicants **have** arrived. | "A number of" = many (plural) |

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** Every government employee must wear ___ ID at all times.
- (a) their (b) **his or her** (c) its (d) our
- *Explanation:* "Every government employee" is singular → singular pronoun "his or her."

**Medium:** Neither the supervisor nor the clerks have submitted ___ performance reports.
- (a) his or her (b) **their** (c) its (d) his
- *Explanation:* With "neither...nor," the pronoun agrees with the nearest antecedent "clerks" (plural) → "their."

**Hard:** The committee, after hours of deliberation, finally reached ___ consensus on the proposed budget.
- (a) their (b) **its** (c) his or her (d) our
- *Explanation:* "Committee" is a collective noun acting as a single unit (reaching one consensus) → singular "its."

### 4.12 Ambiguous Pronoun References

A pronoun reference is **ambiguous** when the reader cannot determine which noun the pronoun refers to. The CSE tests your ability to identify and correct these errors.

#### Types of Ambiguous References

**1. Two possible antecedents:**
- ❌ **Ambiguous:** When the director met with the supervisor, **she** expressed concern about the budget.
  - Who is "she"? The director or the supervisor?
- ✅ **Clear:** When the director met with the supervisor, **the director** expressed concern about the budget.
- ✅ **Clear:** The director expressed concern about the budget when she met with the supervisor.

**2. Vague "this," "that," "it," or "which" without a clear antecedent:**
- ❌ **Vague:** The office was understaffed and the equipment was outdated. **This** caused delays.
  - What is "this"? The understaffing? The outdated equipment? Both?
- ✅ **Clear:** The office was understaffed and the equipment was outdated. **This combination of problems** caused delays.

**3. Implied antecedent (the noun isn't actually stated):**
- ❌ **Implied:** In the **government**, **they** require all employees to wear IDs.
  - Who is "they"? "Government" is not a person.
- ✅ **Clear:** **Government officials** require all employees to wear IDs.

**4. Too far from antecedent:**
- ❌ **Distant:** The supervisor reviewed the report. She checked the data, verified the sources, and consulted with three department heads. **She** then approved it.
  - While technically clear, the distance can cause confusion in complex sentences.

#### How to Fix Ambiguous References

| Strategy | Example |
|----------|---------|
| Repeat the noun | "The director expressed concern..." |
| Restructure the sentence | Move clauses so the pronoun is near its antecedent |
| Use a summary noun | "This staffing problem..." instead of "This..." |
| Split into two sentences | Separate the ideas for clarity |

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Medium:** Which sentence has an ambiguous pronoun reference?
- (a) The manager told the clerk to submit his report.
- (b) **The manager told the supervisor that he would be transferred.**
- (c) Maria submitted her application on time.
- (d) The employees completed their assigned tasks.
- *Explanation:* In (b), "he" could refer to either "the manager" or "the supervisor."

**Hard:** The department issued a memo about the policy revision. ___ clarified the new procedures.
- (a) **It** (b) This (c) They (d) Which
- *Explanation:* "It" clearly refers to "memo" (singular, nearest logical antecedent). "This" would be vague without a following noun. "They" has no plural antecedent.

### 4.13 Who vs. Whom — Complete Guide

This distinction is one of the most frequently tested pronoun concepts on the CSE.

#### The Core Rule

| Pronoun | Case | Function | Replaces |
|---------|------|----------|----------|
| **who** | Subjective | Subject of verb | he, she, they |
| **whom** | Objective | Object of verb/preposition | him, her, them |
| **whose** | Possessive | Shows ownership | his, her, their |

#### The Substitution Method (Most Reliable)

**Step 1:** Rearrange the clause into a statement.
**Step 2:** Substitute he/him (or she/her).
**Step 3:** If "he/she" fits → **who**. If "him/her" fits → **whom**.

| Question/Clause | Rearranged | Substitution | Answer |
|----------------|------------|--------------|--------|
| ___ called the meeting? | He called the meeting. | "he" works | **who** |
| ___ did you invite? | You invited him. | "him" works | **whom** |
| The person ___ I spoke to... | I spoke to him. | "him" works | **whom** |
| The officer ___ handles complaints... | He handles complaints. | "he" works | **who** |
| ___ do you think will be promoted? | You think he will be promoted. | "he" works | **who** |

#### Tricky Patterns

**1. Interrupting expressions ("do you think," "I believe," "we know"):**
These are parenthetical — ignore them when determining case.
- **Who** do you think will be promoted? (NOT "whom")
  - Remove "do you think": "Who will be promoted?" → He will be promoted. ✅
- The employee **who** I believe deserves the award... 
  - Remove "I believe": "who deserves the award" → He deserves. ✅

**2. After prepositions:**
- To **whom** should I address the letter? (formal)
- The colleague with **whom** I worked was efficient.
- For **whom** was the memo intended?

**3. In relative clauses:**
- The applicant **who** scored highest was hired. (subject of "scored")
- The applicant **whom** the panel selected was hired. (object of "selected")

#### Memory Aid

**Who = He** (both end in vowel-like sounds, both are subjects)
**Whom = Him** (both end in "m," both are objects)

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** ___ is the newly appointed department head?
- (a) Whom (b) **Who** (c) Whose (d) Which
- *Explanation:* Subject of "is" → "Who." Test: "He is the newly appointed head." ✅

**Medium:** The employee to ___ the commendation was given expressed gratitude.
- (a) who (b) **whom** (c) whose (d) which
- *Explanation:* Object of preposition "to" → "whom." Test: "The commendation was given to him." ✅

**Hard:** ___ do you believe is the most qualified candidate for the position?
- (a) Whom (b) **Who** (c) Whose (d) Whomever
- *Explanation:* Remove "do you believe": "___ is the most qualified candidate" → subject of "is" → "Who." The interrupting expression doesn't change the case.

### 4.14 Common Pronoun Errors

#### Error 1: Double Subjects

Using a pronoun immediately after the noun it refers to, creating a redundant subject.

- ❌ The director **she** approved the budget.
- ✅ The director approved the budget.
- ✅ **She** approved the budget.

- ❌ My colleagues **they** always arrive on time.
- ✅ My colleagues always arrive on time.

#### Error 2: Wrong Pronoun Case

Using subject pronouns where object pronouns are needed, or vice versa.

- ❌ The award was given to **he** and **I**.
- ✅ The award was given to **him** and **me**.

- ❌ **Her** and **me** attended the seminar.
- ✅ **She** and **I** attended the seminar.

- ❌ Just between you and **I**, the policy is unfair.
- ✅ Just between you and **me**, the policy is unfair.

#### Error 3: Unclear Antecedents

- ❌ When the supervisor met with the employee, **he** was nervous.
- ✅ When the supervisor met with the employee, **the employee** was nervous.

#### Error 4: Incorrect Agreement

- ❌ Everyone must submit **their** report by Friday.
- ✅ Everyone must submit **his or her** report by Friday.

- ❌ The company announced **their** new policy.
- ✅ The company announced **its** new policy.

- ❌ Neither of the applicants passed **their** exam.
- ✅ Neither of the applicants passed **his or her** exam.

#### Error 5: Reflexive Pronoun Misuse

- ❌ The report was prepared by my colleague and **myself**.
- ✅ The report was prepared by my colleague and **me**.

- ❌ **Myself** will handle the project.
- ✅ **I** will handle the project.

#### Error 6: Pronoun Shifts

Changing person mid-sentence without reason.

- ❌ When **one** studies hard, **you** will pass the exam.
- ✅ When **one** studies hard, **one** will pass the exam.
- ✅ When **you** study hard, **you** will pass the exam.

- ❌ If **a person** wants to succeed, **they** must work hard.
- ✅ If **a person** wants to succeed, **he or she** must work hard.

#### Error 7: Who/Whom Confusion

- ❌ The employee **whom** was promoted deserved it.
- ✅ The employee **who** was promoted deserved it. (subject of "was promoted")

- ❌ **Who** did you give the memo to?
- ✅ **Whom** did you give the memo to? (object of "give")
- ✅ To **whom** did you give the memo? (formal)

### Exam Strategies

#### Strategy 1: Identify the Pronoun's Role First

Before looking at choices, determine what the blank needs:
- Is it a **subject**? → who, I, he, she, we, they
- Is it an **object**? → whom, me, him, her, us, them
- Is it **possessive**? → whose, my, his, her, our, their, mine, yours
- Is it **reflexive**? → Does subject = object? → -self/-selves

#### Strategy 2: Find the Antecedent

For agreement questions:
1. Locate the noun the pronoun refers to
2. Determine if it's singular or plural
3. Match the pronoun's number

**Watch for traps:**
- "Each of the employees" → antecedent is "each" (singular), NOT "employees"
- "One of the boys" → antecedent is "one" (singular), NOT "boys"

#### Strategy 3: The Removal Test for Compounds

For "_____ and I" vs. "_____ and me" questions:
1. Remove the other person
2. Read the sentence with just the pronoun
3. Choose whichever sounds correct alone

- "The director invited (she/her) and (I/me)."
  - "The director invited I" ❌ → "me"
  - "The director invited she" ❌ → "her"
  - Answer: "The director invited **her** and **me**."

#### Strategy 4: The He/Him Test for Who/Whom

- Rearrange into a statement
- Substitute he or him
- He → who; Him → whom

#### Strategy 5: Elimination by Grammar Rule

1. Eliminate choices that violate number agreement
2. Eliminate choices that use wrong case
3. Eliminate nonstandard forms (hisself, theirselves)
4. Choose from remaining options

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Pronoun Case in Compound Structures

**Question:** The supervisor assigned the project to my colleague and ___.
- (a) I (b) myself (c) **me** (d) mine

**Step-by-step:**
1. "To" is a preposition → needs object pronoun
2. Remove "my colleague and": "assigned the project to ___"
3. "assigned the project to I" ❌
4. "assigned the project to myself" ❌ (subject ≠ object; "supervisor" ≠ "me")
5. "assigned the project to me" ✅
6. Answer: **(c) me**

### Example 2: Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement

**Question:** Each of the newly hired employees must complete ___ probationary period.
- (a) their (b) **his or her** (c) its (d) our

**Step-by-step:**
1. Find the antecedent: "Each" (not "employees")
2. "Each" is singular indefinite pronoun
3. Singular antecedent → singular pronoun
4. "their" is plural ❌
5. "its" is for things, not people ❌
6. "our" doesn't match person ❌
7. Answer: **(b) his or her**

### Example 3: Who vs. Whom

**Question:** The applicant ___ the committee interviewed was highly qualified.
- (a) **whom** (b) who (c) whose (d) which

**Step-by-step:**
1. Isolate the relative clause: "___ the committee interviewed"
2. Rearrange: "the committee interviewed ___"
3. Substitute: "the committee interviewed him" ✅ (not "he")
4. Him → whom
5. Answer: **(a) whom**

### Example 4: Reflexive vs. Object Pronoun

**Question:** Please submit the signed documents to the HR officer or ___.
- (a) myself (b) **me** (c) I (d) mine

**Step-by-step:**
1. Subject of sentence: "you" (implied)
2. Object of "to": the person receiving documents
3. Is subject = object? "You" ≠ "me" → NOT reflexive
4. "myself" is wrong (reflexive requires same subject/object)
5. Object of preposition "to" → object pronoun "me"
6. Answer: **(b) me**

### Example 5: Ambiguous Reference

**Question:** Which sentence contains an ambiguous pronoun reference?
- (a) Maria told her supervisor that she would be late.
- (b) The clerk filed the documents in their proper folders.
- (c) **When the manager spoke to the employee, he seemed upset.**
- (d) The committee submitted its final report.

**Step-by-step:**
1. (a) "she" = Maria (the one who would be late) — could be ambiguous but context suggests Maria
2. (b) "their" = documents' → clear
3. (c) "he" = manager or employee? → **ambiguous** — cannot determine who seemed upset
4. (d) "its" = committee's → clear
5. Answer: **(c)**

## Key Takeaways

- **Personal pronouns** change form based on case: subject (I, he, she), object (me, him, her), possessive (my, his, her)
- **Possessive pronouns NEVER use apostrophes**: its, your, their, whose (not it's, you're, they're, who's)
- **Reflexive pronouns** (-self/-selves) are used ONLY when subject and object are the same person
- **Who = subject** (he/she); **Whom = object** (him/her) — use the substitution test
- **Singular indefinite pronouns** (everyone, each, neither) take singular pronoun references (his or her)
- **Pronoun-antecedent agreement** requires matching number, person, and gender
- **Ambiguous references** occur when a pronoun could refer to more than one antecedent
- For compound structures, **remove the other person** and test the pronoun alone
- **"Between you and me"** — always object case after prepositions
- **Collective nouns** are singular when acting as a unit, plural when acting as individuals
- **That** introduces restrictive clauses (no commas); **which** introduces nonrestrictive clauses (commas)

## Summary

Pronouns replace nouns to create fluid, efficient communication. The CSE tests eight major pronoun types (personal, possessive, reflexive, intensive, demonstrative, interrogative, relative, indefinite) plus two critical skill areas: pronoun-antecedent agreement and ambiguous reference detection. The most common exam traps involve compound structures ("between you and I" errors), indefinite pronoun agreement ("everyone...their"), who/whom confusion, and reflexive pronoun misuse ("submit to myself"). Master the substitution tests (he/him for who/whom, removal test for compounds) and you can solve virtually any CSE pronoun question through systematic elimination rather than guessing.

## Memory Aids

**For Who vs. Whom:**
- "Who did it? He did." (both subjects)
- "To whom? To him." (both objects, both end in M)

**For Reflexive Pronoun Rule:**
- "Mirror Rule" — reflexive pronouns are like mirrors: the subject must see ITSELF in the object. If the subject and object are different people, no mirror (no reflexive).

**For Possessive Pronouns (no apostrophe):**
- "Possessive pronouns are already possessive — they don't need an apostrophe's help."
- Remember: his, hers, its, ours, yours, theirs — NONE have apostrophes.

**For Singular Indefinite Pronouns:**
- **ONE** mnemonic: every**one**, some**one**, any**one**, no **one**, each **one** — they all contain "one" (singular!)

**For Compound Pronoun Case:**
- "Strip and test" — remove the other person, test the pronoun alone.

**For Agreement with "or"/"nor":**
- "Nearest wins" — the pronoun matches the antecedent closest to it.
