# Modifiers

## Explanations

### Introduction

**Modifiers** are words, phrases, or clauses that describe, limit, or qualify other elements in a sentence. They add detail, precision, and clarity to writing by answering questions like *which one?*, *what kind?*, *how?*, *when?*, *where?*, and *to what extent?* Without modifiers, sentences would be bare and vague. With incorrect modifiers, sentences become confusing, ambiguous, or unintentionally humorous.

### Why Modifiers Matter

Modifiers are the difference between clear communication and chaos. Consider:
- **Without modifier:** The employee submitted a report.
- **With modifier:** The *newly hired* employee submitted a *detailed quarterly* report *yesterday*.

In government writing — memoranda, legal documents, policy directives, and official correspondence — modifier placement determines whether a sentence is precise or dangerously ambiguous. A misplaced modifier in a legal document can change the meaning of an entire provision.

### Why Modifiers Are Tested in the CSE

The Grammar and Correct Usage section of the Philippine Civil Service Examination consistently tests modifiers because:
- Government documents require absolute clarity — ambiguous modifiers create legal and administrative problems
- Misplaced and dangling modifiers are among the most common writing errors in professional Filipino English
- Correct modifier usage signals the ability to write clear, unambiguous official communication
- Modifier errors often create unintentionally absurd meanings that undermine professional credibility
- The CSE tests whether examinees can identify and correct unclear sentence structures

### Common Mistakes Examinees Make

1. **Misplaced modifiers** — placing a modifier too far from the word it describes ("She served food to the guests on paper plates" — were the guests on paper plates?)
2. **Dangling modifiers** — starting a sentence with a modifying phrase that has no logical subject ("Walking to the office, the rain started" — the rain was not walking)
3. **Squinting modifiers** — placing a modifier between two words it could modify ("The employee who works quickly finishes tasks" — does "quickly" modify "works" or "finishes"?)
4. **Misplaced limiting modifiers** — putting "only," "almost," "nearly," or "just" in the wrong position, changing the sentence's meaning
5. **Faulty comparisons** — using comparative/superlative forms incorrectly ("more better," "most fastest")
6. **Broken parallelism in modifiers** — mixing modifier forms in a series

---

### Learning Objectives

After this lesson, you should be able to:
- Identify different types of modifiers (adjectives, adverbs, phrases, clauses)
- Recognize correct and incorrect modifier placement in sentences
- Detect and correct misplaced modifiers that create ambiguity
- Identify and fix dangling modifiers that lack a logical subject
- Properly place limiting modifiers (only, almost, nearly, just, even)
- Use comparative and superlative forms correctly
- Maintain parallel structure in sentences with multiple modifiers
- Solve CSE-style modifier questions with confidence under time pressure

### 4.1 What Are Modifiers?

#### Definition

A **modifier** is any word, phrase, or clause that provides additional information about another word in a sentence. Modifiers describe, limit, identify, or quantify the words they modify. The term comes from the Latin *modificare* — "to limit, regulate, or restrain."

#### Grammatical Purpose

Modifiers serve three core functions:
1. **Description** — adding detail (a *tall* building, she spoke *softly*)
2. **Identification** — specifying which one (the report *on the desk*, the employee *who was promoted*)
3. **Limitation** — narrowing scope (*only* three applicants, *almost* everyone)

#### Types of Modifiers

| Type | What It Modifies | Example |
|------|-----------------|---------|
| Adjective | Nouns/pronouns | The *efficient* employee filed the report. |
| Adverb | Verbs/adjectives/other adverbs | She worked *diligently* on the project. |
| Phrase | Nouns or verbs | The man *in the gray suit* is the director. |
| Clause | Nouns or verbs | The memo *that was issued yesterday* clarified the policy. |

#### The Golden Rule of Modifier Placement

**Place modifiers as close as possible to the word they modify.**

This single rule prevents most modifier errors. When a modifier is separated from its target word, the sentence becomes ambiguous or illogical.

- ✅ She *almost* drove the car for **300 kilometers**. (she drove close to 300 km)
- ❌ She drove the car for *almost* 300 kilometers. (same meaning, but less precise placement)
- ❌ *Almost*, she drove the car for 300 kilometers. (unclear what "almost" modifies)

#### Examples by Difficulty

**Easy:**
- The *new* employee attended the orientation. (adjective modifying "employee")
- She spoke *clearly* during the presentation. (adverb modifying "spoke")
- The files *on the desk* need your signature. (phrase modifying "files")

**Medium:**
- *Having completed the training*, the officer resumed her regular duties. (participial phrase modifying "officer")
- The policy *that the committee approved last month* takes effect today. (clause modifying "policy")
- The director spoke *only* to the department heads about the reorganization. (limiting modifier)

**Hard:**
- The memorandum *issued pursuant to Executive Order No. 292* mandates that all government employees *covered by the Civil Service Law* shall submit their SALNs *within the prescribed period*. (multiple modifiers in formal register)
- *Notwithstanding the provisions of the preceding section*, the Commission may, *in meritorious cases* and *upon recommendation of the department head*, grant an extension *not exceeding thirty days*. (legal/formal modifier-heavy sentence)

**CSE-Style:**
- Choose the sentence with correct modifier placement:
  - (a) Walking to the office, the rain started to fall.
  - (b) The rain started to fall walking to the office.
  - (c) Walking to the office, she noticed the rain starting to fall.
  - (d) She noticed walking to the office the rain starting to fall.
  - Answer: **(c)** — "Walking to the office" correctly modifies "she," the logical subject performing the action.

### 4.2 Adjective Modifiers

#### What Are Adjective Modifiers?

Adjectives modify **nouns** and **pronouns**. They answer the questions: *Which one? What kind? How many? How much?*

#### Types of Adjectives

##### Descriptive Adjectives

Describe qualities or characteristics of a noun:
- The *efficient* clerk processed the documents.
- She submitted a *comprehensive* report.
- The *old* building needs renovation.

##### Quantitative Adjectives

Indicate amount or number:
- *Several* employees attended the seminar.
- She reviewed *three* applications.
- *Much* effort was needed to complete the project.

##### Demonstrative Adjectives

Point to specific nouns:
- *This* memorandum supersedes the previous one.
- *Those* documents must be filed immediately.
- *That* policy has been revised.

##### Proper Adjectives

Derived from proper nouns:
- The *Philippine* government issued the directive.
- She attended a *European* conference.
- The *Malacanang* press briefing was scheduled at noon.

#### Adjective Placement Rules

##### Before the Noun (Attributive Position)

Most adjectives come BEFORE the noun they modify:
- A *dedicated* public servant
- The *annual* performance review
- A *comprehensive* training program

##### After a Linking Verb (Predicative Position)

Adjectives can follow linking verbs (is, was, seems, appears, becomes):
- The report is *accurate*.
- The supervisor seems *satisfied*.
- The results appear *promising*.

#### Order of Multiple Adjectives

When multiple adjectives modify the same noun, they follow a specific order:

| Order | Category | Example |
|-------|----------|---------|
| 1 | Opinion/Quality | beautiful, excellent, terrible |
| 2 | Size | large, small, tiny |
| 3 | Age | old, new, young |
| 4 | Shape | round, square, rectangular |
| 5 | Color | red, blue, white |
| 6 | Origin | Filipino, American, Japanese |
| 7 | Material | wooden, metal, paper |
| 8 | Purpose/Type | training (manual), filing (cabinet) |

**Examples:**
- A *beautiful large old rectangular wooden* desk (opinion → size → age → shape → material)
- The *new white government* vehicle (age → color → purpose)
- An *excellent young Filipino* civil servant (opinion → age → origin)

**CSE Tip:** The exam rarely tests adjective order directly, but knowing it helps you identify awkward or incorrect sentences.

#### Common Adjective Errors

| Error | Incorrect | Correct |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Using adverb instead of adjective after linking verb | She feels *badly* about it. | She feels *bad* about it. |
| Confusing adjective/adverb | The food tastes *well*. | The food tastes *good*. |
| Double adjective | The *most prettiest* design | The *prettiest* design |

### 4.3 Adverb Modifiers

#### What Are Adverb Modifiers?

Adverbs modify **verbs**, **adjectives**, and **other adverbs**. They answer: *How? When? Where? How often? To what degree?*

#### Types of Adverbs

##### Manner (How?)

- She completed the task *efficiently*.
- The clerk spoke *politely* to the applicant.
- He *carefully* reviewed the documents.

##### Place (Where?)

- The meeting will be held *here*.
- Please submit the form *downstairs*.
- The files were stored *inside*.

##### Time (When?)

- The director will arrive *tomorrow*.
- She *already* submitted her report.
- The policy takes effect *immediately*.

##### Frequency (How often?)

- He *always* arrives on time.
- The committee meets *quarterly*.
- She *rarely* takes a leave of absence.

##### Degree (To what extent?)

- The report was *extremely* detailed.
- She is *very* qualified for the position.
- The task was *almost* impossible.

#### Adverb Placement Rules

##### Modifying a Verb

Adverbs of manner usually come AFTER the verb or object:
- She completed the report *quickly*. ✅
- She *quickly* completed the report. ✅ (also acceptable)
- *Quickly* she completed the report. ✅ (emphasis on speed)

##### Modifying an Adjective

Adverbs come BEFORE the adjective:
- The *extremely* detailed report impressed the director.
- She is *highly* qualified for the position.
- The *newly* appointed officer attended the briefing.

##### Modifying Another Adverb

Adverbs come BEFORE the adverb they modify:
- She works *very* efficiently.
- He spoke *quite* clearly during the hearing.
- The project progressed *remarkably* quickly.

##### Frequency Adverbs

Usually placed BEFORE the main verb but AFTER "be" and auxiliary verbs:
- She *always* submits her reports on time. (before main verb)
- He is *usually* punctual. (after "be")
- They have *never* been late. (after auxiliary)

#### Adverb vs. Adjective Confusion

| After Action Verbs → Adverb | After Linking Verbs → Adjective |
|------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| She works *hard*. | The test was *hard*. |
| He ran *fast*. | The car is *fast*. |
| She spoke *loudly*. | The music was *loud*. |

**Tricky Cases:**
- She looks *beautiful*. ✅ (linking verb → adjective describes "she")
- She looked *carefully* at the document. ✅ (action verb → adverb describes how she looked)

### 4.4 Phrases as Modifiers

#### Participial Phrases

A participial phrase begins with a present participle (-ing) or past participle (-ed/-en) and acts as an adjective.

**Present Participle:**
- *Working overtime*, the employee finished the project. (modifies "employee")
- The supervisor, *reviewing the applications*, noticed an error. (modifies "supervisor")

**Past Participle:**
- *Exhausted by the long meeting*, the staff took a break. (modifies "staff")
- The memo, *signed by the director*, was distributed to all departments. (modifies "memo")

**Critical Rule:** The participial phrase MUST modify the subject of the main clause (or the noun nearest to it).

- ✅ *Running late*, **Maria** took a taxi to the office. (Maria was running late)
- ❌ *Running late*, **the taxi** was taken by Maria. (the taxi was not running late)

#### Infinitive Phrases

An infinitive phrase (to + verb) can function as an adjective or adverb modifier:

**As Adjective (modifying a noun):**
- She has the ability *to lead effectively*. (modifies "ability")
- The decision *to postpone the meeting* was wise. (modifies "decision")

**As Adverb (modifying a verb — showing purpose):**
- He studied hard *to pass the civil service exam*. (why he studied)
- The committee met *to discuss the budget proposal*. (why they met)

#### Prepositional Phrases

A prepositional phrase (preposition + object) can function as an adjective or adverb:

**As Adjective (modifying a noun):**
- The report *on the desk* needs your signature. (which report?)
- The employee *from the HR department* called. (which employee?)

**As Adverb (modifying a verb):**
- She works *with great dedication*. (how she works)
- The meeting was held *in the conference room*. (where it was held)
- Submit the form *before the deadline*. (when to submit)

#### Appositive Phrases

An appositive renames or identifies a noun and is set off by commas:

- **Director Santos**, *the head of the department*, approved the request.
- The Civil Service Commission, *the central personnel agency of the government*, administers the CSE.
- My supervisor, *a veteran of twenty years in public service*, mentored me.

### 4.5 Clauses as Modifiers

#### Adjective Clauses (Relative Clauses)

Adjective clauses modify nouns and begin with relative pronouns (who, whom, whose, which, that) or relative adverbs (where, when, why).

**Examples:**
- The employee **who submitted the report early** received a commendation. (modifies "employee")
- The policy **that was revised last month** is now in effect. (modifies "policy")
- The office **where the meeting will be held** is on the third floor. (modifies "office")

**Restrictive vs. Nonrestrictive:**
- Restrictive (essential, no commas): The employees **who passed the exam** will be promoted.
- Nonrestrictive (extra info, commas): The employees, **who all passed the exam**, will be promoted.

#### Adverb Clauses

Adverb clauses modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. They begin with subordinating conjunctions and answer when, where, why, how, or under what condition.

**Time:** *When the director arrives*, the meeting will begin.
**Cause:** She was promoted *because she excelled in her duties*.
**Condition:** *If you submit late*, your application will be rejected.
**Concession:** *Although the task was difficult*, she completed it on time.
**Purpose:** She studied hard *so that she could pass the exam*.

**Placement:**
- Beginning of sentence (comma required): *Because it was raining*, the event was moved indoors.
- End of sentence (usually no comma): The event was moved indoors *because it was raining*.

### 4.6 Misplaced Modifiers

#### What Is a Misplaced Modifier?

A **misplaced modifier** is a word, phrase, or clause placed too far from the word it modifies, causing confusion or unintended meaning. The modifier appears to modify the wrong word.

#### Why This Matters

Misplaced modifiers are one of the MOST TESTED concepts on the CSE because they create absurd or ambiguous meanings in professional writing.

#### Common Patterns

##### Misplaced Phrases

| Incorrect (Misplaced) | Problem | Correct |
|----------------------|---------|---------|
| She served food to the guests **on paper plates**. | Were the guests on paper plates? | She served food **on paper plates** to the guests. |
| The supervisor gave a presentation to the staff **wearing a blue suit**. | Was the staff wearing a blue suit? | **Wearing a blue suit**, the supervisor gave a presentation to the staff. |
| I saw a dog walking to the office **with a broken leg**. | Was the office broken? | Walking to the office, I saw a dog **with a broken leg**. |
| He read a report about fraud **in his office**. | Was the fraud in his office? | **In his office**, he read a report about fraud. |

##### Misplaced Clauses

| Incorrect | Correct |
|-----------|---------|
| The employee filed the complaint **who was harassed**. | The employee **who was harassed** filed the complaint. |
| She bought a car for her daughter **that was on sale**. | She bought a car **that was on sale** for her daughter. |

#### How to Fix Misplaced Modifiers

1. **Identify** what the modifier is supposed to describe
2. **Move** the modifier next to the word it logically modifies
3. **Reread** to confirm the sentence is clear

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** Choose the correct sentence.
- (a) The manager discussed the policy with employees that was outdated.
- (b) The manager discussed the policy that was outdated with employees.
- Answer: **(b)** — "that was outdated" correctly modifies "policy," not "employees."

**Medium:** Choose the correct sentence.
- (a) Covered in dust, the librarian found the old book on the top shelf.
- (b) The librarian found the old book covered in dust on the top shelf.
- (c) The librarian found the old book on the top shelf covered in dust.
- (d) On the top shelf, the librarian found the old book covered in dust.
- Answer: **(d)** — Each modifier is closest to what it logically describes.

**Hard:** Choose the sentence free of modifier errors.
- (a) The committee almost reviewed all of the applications before the deadline.
- (b) The committee reviewed almost all of the applications before the deadline.
- Answer: **(b)** — "Almost" modifies "all" (nearly all applications), not "reviewed" (they did review, not almost reviewed).

### 4.7 Dangling Modifiers

#### What Is a Dangling Modifier?

A **dangling modifier** is a modifying phrase (usually at the beginning of a sentence) that does not logically modify any word in the sentence. The intended subject is missing or is not the grammatical subject of the main clause.

#### Why "Dangling"?

The modifier "dangles" because it has nothing to attach to — the word it should modify is absent from the sentence.

#### The Rule

When a sentence begins with a modifying phrase, the subject of the main clause MUST be the person or thing performing the action in that phrase.

#### Common Dangling Modifier Patterns

##### Dangling Participial Phrases

| Incorrect (Dangling) | Problem | Correct |
|---------------------|---------|---------|
| **Walking to the office**, the rain started to fall. | The rain was not walking. | **Walking to the office**, *she* got caught in the rain. |
| **After reviewing the documents**, the report was approved. | The report did not review documents. | **After reviewing the documents**, *the director* approved the report. |
| **Having completed the training**, a certificate was issued. | The certificate did not complete training. | **Having completed the training**, *the employee* received a certificate. |
| **Exhausted from overtime**, the bed looked inviting. | The bed was not exhausted. | **Exhausted from overtime**, *she* found the bed inviting. |

##### Dangling Infinitive Phrases

| Incorrect | Correct |
|-----------|---------|
| **To pass the CSE**, daily study is recommended. | **To pass the CSE**, *you* should study daily. |
| **To qualify for promotion**, the requirements must be met. | **To qualify for promotion**, *an employee* must meet the requirements. |

##### Dangling Prepositional/Elliptical Phrases

| Incorrect | Correct |
|-----------|---------|
| **At the age of five**, my father taught me to read. | **At the age of five**, *I* was taught to read by my father. |
| **While on leave**, the office was renovated. | **While on leave**, *she learned that* the office was renovated. |

#### How to Fix Dangling Modifiers

**Method 1: Change the subject of the main clause**
- ❌ *Driving to work*, the accident was witnessed.
- ✅ *Driving to work*, **I** witnessed the accident.

**Method 2: Turn the modifier into a full clause**
- ❌ *Walking to the office*, the rain started.
- ✅ **While she was walking** to the office, the rain started.

#### Identification Strategy

Ask: "Who or what is performing the action in the opening phrase?"
Then check: "Is that person/thing the subject of the main clause?"
If NO → the modifier is dangling.

**Test:**
- "*Having studied all night*, the exam seemed easy."
- Who studied all night? → The student (implied but NOT the subject)
- What is the subject? → "the exam"
- Did the exam study? → NO → DANGLING

**Fix:** "*Having studied all night*, **she** found the exam easy."

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** Which sentence has a dangling modifier?
- (a) Tired from the long meeting, the staff took a break.
- (b) Tired from the long meeting, a break was taken by the staff.
- Answer: **(b)** — "a break" cannot be tired. The modifier dangles.

**Medium:** Choose the correct sentence.
- (a) After passing the examination, the position was offered to her.
- (b) After passing the examination, she was offered the position.
- (c) After passing the examination, they offered her the position.
- Answer: **(b)** — "she" is the one who passed the examination.

**Hard:** Identify the sentence WITHOUT a dangling modifier.
- (a) To be considered for the promotion, all requirements must be submitted by the applicant.
- (b) To be considered for the promotion, the applicant must submit all requirements.
- (c) To be considered for the promotion, submission of all requirements is necessary.
- Answer: **(b)** — "the applicant" is the one being considered for promotion.

### 4.8 Squinting Modifiers

#### What Is a Squinting Modifier?

A **squinting modifier** (also called a "two-way modifier") is placed between two words and could logically modify either one, creating ambiguity.

#### Examples

| Ambiguous (Squinting) | Interpretation 1 | Interpretation 2 | Fix |
|----------------------|-------------------|-------------------|-----|
| The supervisor said **on Monday** the report was due. | The supervisor spoke on Monday. | The report is due on Monday. | **On Monday**, the supervisor said the report was due. OR The supervisor said the report was due **on Monday**. |
| Employees who exercise **regularly** perform better. | They exercise regularly. | They regularly perform better. | Employees who **regularly** exercise perform better. OR Employees who exercise perform better **on a regular basis**. |
| She told him **frequently** to check the inventory. | She told him many times. | He should check frequently. | She **frequently** told him to check the inventory. OR She told him to check the inventory **frequently**. |

#### How to Fix Squinting Modifiers

Move the modifier clearly to ONE side — either before or after the word it modifies — so there is no ambiguity.

**Rule:** If a modifier could attach to either the word before it or the word after it, reposition it so only one interpretation is possible.

#### CSE-Style Example

**Medium:** Which sentence is ambiguous due to a squinting modifier?
- (a) The director who spoke briefly left the room.
- (b) The director briefly spoke and then left the room.
- (c) The director spoke and briefly left the room.
- Answer: **(a)** — "briefly" could modify "spoke" (spoke for a short time) or "left" (left shortly after speaking).

### 4.9 Limiting Modifiers

#### What Are Limiting Modifiers?

Limiting modifiers are words that restrict or limit the meaning of another word. The most common are: **only, almost, nearly, just, even, hardly, merely, simply, scarcely**.

#### Why Placement Matters

The position of a limiting modifier DRAMATICALLY changes the meaning of a sentence. This is one of the most tested modifier concepts on the CSE.

#### "Only" — The Most Tested Limiting Modifier

Watch how "only" changes meaning depending on placement:

| Sentence | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| **Only** she submitted the report on time. | No one else submitted it — she alone did. |
| She **only** submitted the report on time. | She did nothing else — just submitted it. |
| She submitted **only** the report on time. | She submitted the report and nothing else. |
| She submitted the report **only** on time. | She submitted it on time and no other way (barely). |
| She submitted the report on time **only**. | Emphasis that it was merely on time, nothing special. |

**The Rule:** Place "only" (and other limiting modifiers) DIRECTLY BEFORE the word or phrase it limits.

#### Other Limiting Modifiers

##### Almost / Nearly

- He **almost** failed every subject. (He came close to failing but didn't.)
- He failed **almost** every subject. (He failed most subjects.)

##### Just

- She **just** told the director about the problem. (She merely told — didn't do more.)
- She told **just** the director about the problem. (She told only the director — no one else.)

##### Even

- **Even** the director attended the orientation. (Surprisingly, the director came too.)
- The director **even** attended the orientation. (The director did this in addition to other things.)

##### Hardly / Scarcely / Barely

These are negative limiting modifiers:
- She **hardly** spoke during the meeting. (She spoke very little.)
- He **barely** passed the examination. (He passed by a very small margin.)

#### Common Errors with Limiting Modifiers

| Incorrect | Correct | Why |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| She only ate **three** slices. | She ate only **three** slices. | "Only" limits "three," not "ate" |
| He almost drove **200 km**. | He drove almost **200 km**. | He did drive; the distance was almost 200 |
| I nearly read **all** the files. | I read nearly **all** the files. | I did read; the quantity was nearly all |

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** Choose the sentence where "only" is correctly placed.
- (a) She only wants to submit one form.
- (b) She wants to submit only one form.
- Answer: **(b)** — "only" correctly limits "one form."

**Medium:** What does this sentence mean? "The supervisor just reviewed the quarterly report."
- (a) The supervisor reviewed it a moment ago.
- (b) The supervisor merely reviewed it (didn't approve it).
- Answer: **Ambiguous** — both interpretations are possible. Better: "The supervisor reviewed just the quarterly report" OR "The supervisor just now reviewed the quarterly report."

### 4.10 Comparative and Superlative Modifiers

#### Comparative Forms (Comparing Two)

Used when comparing TWO items:

| Type | Rule | Example |
|------|------|---------|
| Short adjectives (1 syllable) | add -er | tall → tall**er**, fast → fast**er** |
| Adjectives ending in -y | change y to i, add -er | happy → happi**er**, easy → easi**er** |
| Long adjectives (2+ syllables) | use "more" | efficient → **more** efficient |
| Adverbs ending in -ly | use "more" | quickly → **more** quickly |

**Structure:** X is [comparative] **than** Y.
- She is *more qualified* **than** the other applicant.
- This report is *better* **than** the previous one.
- He works *more efficiently* **than** his predecessor.

#### Superlative Forms (Comparing Three or More)

Used when comparing THREE or more items:

| Type | Rule | Example |
|------|------|---------|
| Short adjectives | add -est | tall → tall**est**, fast → fast**est** |
| Adjectives ending in -y | change y to i, add -est | happy → happi**est** |
| Long adjectives | use "most" | efficient → **most** efficient |
| Adverbs ending in -ly | use "most" | quickly → **most** quickly |

**Structure:** X is **the** [superlative] of/in/among [group].
- She is *the most qualified* **among** all applicants.
- This is *the best* report **in** the department.
- He works *the most efficiently* **of** all the clerks.

#### Irregular Comparisons

| Base | Comparative | Superlative |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| good/well | better | best |
| bad/badly | worse | worst |
| much/many | more | most |
| little | less | least |
| far | farther/further | farthest/furthest |

#### Common Comparison Errors

| Error | Incorrect | Correct | Rule |
|-------|-----------|---------|------|
| Double comparative | *more better* | better | Never combine "more" with -er |
| Double superlative | *most fastest* | fastest | Never combine "most" with -est |
| Incomplete comparison | She is taller. | She is taller **than her sister**. | State what is being compared |
| Wrong form for number | She is the *better* of the five. | She is the **best** of the five. | 3+ items → superlative |
| Wrong form for number | He is the *best* of the two. | He is the **better** of the two. | 2 items → comparative |
| Comparing unlike things | Her report is better than *Maria*. | Her report is better than **Maria's**. | Compare report to report |

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** Choose the correct sentence.
- (a) She is the most smartest student in class.
- (b) She is the smartest student in class.
- Answer: **(b)** — Never use "most" with "-est."

**Medium:** Choose the correct sentence.
- (a) Between the two applicants, she is the best qualified.
- (b) Between the two applicants, she is the better qualified.
- Answer: **(b)** — Comparing two items requires the comparative form.

**Hard:** Choose the correct sentence.
- (a) The new policy is more stricter than the old one.
- (b) The new policy is stricter than the old one.
- (c) The new policy is more strict than the old one.
- Answer: **(b)** or **(c)** — both are acceptable, but (b) is preferred for one-syllable adjectives. (a) is always wrong (double comparative).

### 4.11 Parallel Modifiers

#### What Is Parallelism in Modifiers?

When multiple modifiers are joined by a conjunction, they must be in the **same grammatical form**. This is called parallel structure.

#### The Rule

All items in a series of modifiers must match:
- Adjective + Adjective + Adjective
- Adverb + Adverb + Adverb
- Phrase + Phrase + Phrase

#### Examples

| Non-Parallel (Wrong) | Parallel (Correct) |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| The report was *accurate*, *detailed*, and *written with clarity*. | The report was *accurate*, *detailed*, and *clear*. |
| She works *efficiently*, *with dedication*, and *quick*. | She works *efficiently*, *dedicatedly*, and *quickly*. |
| The job requires someone *intelligent*, *hardworking*, and *who has experience*. | The job requires someone *intelligent*, *hardworking*, and *experienced*. |
| He is known for *being punctual*, *his efficiency*, and *working hard*. | He is known for *being punctual*, *being efficient*, and *working hard*. |

#### Parallelism with Correlative Conjunctions

Correlative conjunctions (both...and, not only...but also, either...or, neither...nor) require parallel modifiers:

- She is **both** *efficient* **and** *reliable*. ✅ (adjective + adjective)
- He is **not only** *qualified* **but also** *experienced*. ✅ (adjective + adjective)
- ❌ She is **both** *efficient* **and** *a hard worker*. (adjective + noun phrase — not parallel)

#### CSE-Style Example

**Medium:** Choose the sentence with correct parallel structure.
- (a) The ideal employee is hardworking, punctual, and shows initiative.
- (b) The ideal employee is hardworking, punctual, and initiative-showing.
- (c) The ideal employee is hardworking, punctual, and proactive.
- Answer: **(c)** — Three parallel adjectives.

### 4.12 Common Modifier Errors

#### Error 1: Misplaced Modifier

- ❌ The supervisor only reprimanded **two** employees. (Did the supervisor do nothing else?)
- ✅ The supervisor reprimanded only **two** employees. (Only two, not more.)

#### Error 2: Dangling Modifier

- ❌ **After filing the report**, the office was closed.
- ✅ **After filing the report**, the clerk closed the office.

#### Error 3: Squinting Modifier

- ❌ The employee who works **quickly** finishes tasks.
- ✅ The employee who **quickly** works finishes tasks. OR The employee who works finishes tasks **quickly**.

#### Error 4: Faulty Comparison

- ❌ Manila is bigger than **any city** in the Philippines. (Manila IS a city in the Philippines)
- ✅ Manila is bigger than **any other city** in the Philippines.

#### Error 5: Incomplete Comparison

- ❌ This policy is better. (Better than what?)
- ✅ This policy is better **than the previous one**.

#### Error 6: Double Negative Modifiers

- ❌ She can't **hardly** finish the task. (double negative)
- ✅ She can **hardly** finish the task.
- ✅ She **can't** finish the task.

#### Error 7: Misused Adjective/Adverb

- ❌ She performed **good** on the exam. ("good" is an adjective)
- ✅ She performed **well** on the exam. ("well" is an adverb modifying "performed")

#### Error 8: Vague Modifier Reference

- ❌ The manager told the clerk that **his** report was late. (Whose report — the manager's or the clerk's?)
- ✅ The manager told the clerk, "**Your** report is late."

### 4.13 Step-by-Step Rules for Modifier Questions

#### Rule 1: Identify the Modifier

Ask: What word, phrase, or clause is adding description or limitation?

#### Rule 2: Identify What It Should Modify

Ask: Logically, what is this modifier describing? Who or what is performing the action?

#### Rule 3: Check Proximity

Is the modifier NEXT TO the word it modifies? If not, it may be misplaced.

#### Rule 4: Check the Subject (for Opening Phrases)

If the sentence starts with a modifying phrase, the subject of the main clause MUST be the logical doer of that phrase.

#### Rule 5: Check for Ambiguity

Could the modifier attach to more than one word? If yes, it's squinting — reposition it.

#### Rule 6: Check Limiting Modifier Placement

Is "only," "just," "almost," "nearly," or "even" directly before the word it limits?

#### Rule 7: Check Comparison Structure

- Two items → comparative (-er / more)
- Three+ items → superlative (-est / most)
- No double forms (more better, most fastest)
- Complete the comparison (than what?)

### 4.14 Exam Strategies

#### Strategy 1: Read for Logic First

Before analyzing grammar, ask: "Does this sentence make logical sense?" Modifier errors often create absurd meanings:
- "Flying over the city, the buildings looked tiny." (Buildings don't fly.)
- "Covered in chocolate, the children enjoyed the cake." (Were the children covered in chocolate?)

If the sentence sounds absurd, look for a misplaced or dangling modifier.

#### Strategy 2: Find the Opening Phrase Subject

For sentences beginning with a participial phrase:
1. Read the opening phrase
2. Ask: "Who is doing this action?"
3. Check: Is that person the subject of the main clause?
4. If NO → dangling modifier → eliminate that choice

#### Strategy 3: Track "Only" and Other Limiters

When you see "only," "just," "almost," "nearly," or "even":
1. Identify what word the limiter is supposed to restrict
2. Check if the limiter is directly before that word
3. If not → the sentence likely has a misplaced limiting modifier

#### Strategy 4: Elimination for Comparison Questions

1. Eliminate any choice with a double comparative/superlative (more better, most fastest)
2. Check if the comparison is between two (comparative) or three+ (superlative)
3. Ensure the comparison is complete (than what? of what group?)
4. Ensure like things are compared (report vs. report, not report vs. person)

#### Strategy 5: Parallel Structure Check

When modifiers are in a series:
1. Identify the conjunction (and, or, but)
2. Check that all items match in form
3. Eliminate choices with mixed forms

### Worked Examples

#### Example 1: Dangling Modifier

**Problem:** Choose the correct sentence.
- (a) Having completed the training program, a certificate was awarded to each participant.
- (b) Having completed the training program, each participant was awarded a certificate.

**Step-by-step:**
1. Opening phrase: "Having completed the training program"
2. Who completed the training? → each participant
3. Check (a): Subject is "a certificate" — did the certificate complete training? NO → dangling
4. Check (b): Subject is "each participant" — did participants complete training? YES → correct
5. **Answer: (b)**

#### Example 2: Misplaced Modifier

**Problem:** Choose the sentence with correct modifier placement.
- (a) The director approved the proposal for the new building in her office.
- (b) In her office, the director approved the proposal for the new building.

**Step-by-step:**
1. In (a): "in her office" could modify "building" (the building is in her office?) — ambiguous
2. In (b): "In her office" clearly modifies where the director approved — unambiguous
3. **Answer: (b)**

#### Example 3: Limiting Modifier

**Problem:** Choose the sentence that means "She submitted one form and nothing else."
- (a) She only submitted one form.
- (b) She submitted only one form.
- (c) Only she submitted one form.

**Step-by-step:**
1. (a) "only submitted" → she did nothing but submit (didn't review, didn't sign)
2. (b) "only one form" → the quantity was limited to one
3. (c) "Only she" → no one else submitted
4. The meaning "one form and nothing else" = **(b)**

## Key Takeaways

- **Modifiers** add detail, description, and precision to sentences — they answer which one, what kind, how, when, where, and to what extent
- **Place modifiers as close as possible** to the word they modify — this is the single most important rule
- **Dangling modifiers** occur when the opening phrase has no logical subject in the main clause — fix by making the doer the subject
- **Misplaced modifiers** occur when a modifier is too far from its target — fix by moving it adjacent to the correct word
- **Squinting modifiers** sit between two words they could modify — fix by repositioning to eliminate ambiguity
- **Limiting modifiers** (only, just, almost, nearly, even) must be placed directly before the word they limit
- **Comparatives** (-er/more) compare two; **superlatives** (-est/most) compare three or more — never double them
- **Parallel modifiers** in a series must match in grammatical form
- On the CSE, read for **logical sense first** — absurd meanings signal modifier errors

## Summary

Modifiers are essential tools for clear, precise communication — and they are consistently tested on the Philippine Civil Service Examination because modifier errors create the kind of ambiguity that is unacceptable in government writing. The three most critical concepts are: (1) dangling modifiers, where the opening phrase has no logical subject; (2) misplaced modifiers, where the modifier is too far from its target word; and (3) limiting modifiers like "only," where position determines meaning. Master the proximity rule — keep modifiers next to what they modify — and you will handle modifier questions with confidence on exam day.
