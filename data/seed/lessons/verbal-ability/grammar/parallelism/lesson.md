# Parallelism

## Explanations

### Introduction

**Parallelism** (also called **parallel structure** or **parallel construction**) is the use of the same grammatical form for elements that are equal in function within a sentence. When you join words, phrases, or clauses with conjunctions or place them in a list, each element must follow the same structural pattern. This principle of grammatical balance is one of the most important — and most frequently tested — concepts in the Philippine Civil Service Examination.

### Why Parallelism Matters

Parallelism is the architectural blueprint of clear writing. It creates:
- **Rhythm** — Balanced sentences flow naturally and are easier to read
- **Clarity** — Readers instantly understand how ideas relate to each other
- **Professionalism** — Government documents, memoranda, and official correspondence demand structural precision
- **Persuasion** — Parallel structures make arguments more compelling and memorable

Without parallelism, sentences become awkward, confusing, and unprofessional:
- ❌ "The duties include **planning** events, **coordination** of schedules, and **to prepare** reports."
- ✅ "The duties include **planning** events, **coordinating** schedules, and **preparing** reports."

### Why Parallelism Is Tested in the CSE

The Grammar and Correct Usage section of the Civil Service Examination consistently tests parallelism because:
- Government writing requires precision and structural consistency
- Parallelism errors are among the most common grammar mistakes in professional Filipino English writing
- Correct parallel structure signals the ability to organize complex ideas logically
- Many CSE questions present sentences with subtle structural imbalances that test grammatical awareness
- Official documents (executive orders, memoranda, policy directives) rely heavily on lists and series that demand parallel construction

### Common Mistakes Examinees Make

1. **Mixing gerunds and infinitives** in a series ("She likes **reading**, **to swim**, and **biking**")
2. **Inconsistent verb forms** after correlative conjunctions ("He **not only sings** but also **is dancing**")
3. **Unequal comparisons** ("Running is more tiring **than to walk**")
4. **Broken list structures** where one item differs grammatically from the others
5. **Misplaced correlative conjunctions** that create structural imbalance
6. **Mixing active and passive voice** within parallel elements
7. **Inconsistent noun/verb forms** in paired ideas ("The goal is **efficiency** and **to reduce** costs")

---

### Learning Objectives

After this lesson, you should be able to:
- Identify parallel structures in sentences and recognize when balance is maintained
- Apply parallelism correctly with words, phrases, clauses, and lists
- Recognize and correct faulty parallelism in all its common forms
- Use parallel structure with coordinating conjunctions (FANBOYS)
- Use parallel structure with correlative conjunctions (either...or, not only...but also, etc.)
- Maintain parallelism in comparisons using "than" and "as...as"
- Improve sentence balance, clarity, and professional tone
- Solve CSE-style parallelism questions accurately under time pressure

### 4.1 What Is Parallelism?

#### Definition

**Parallelism** is the grammatical principle that requires elements performing the same function in a sentence to share the same grammatical form. When two or more ideas are connected — whether by conjunctions, in lists, or through comparison — they must be expressed in matching structures.

Think of parallelism as architectural symmetry: just as a building looks balanced when its columns are the same height, a sentence sounds balanced when its connected elements share the same grammatical shape.

#### The Core Principle

**Equal function = Equal form**

If you join three activities, all three must be in the same form:
- All gerunds: **reading**, **writing**, **speaking**
- All infinitives: **to read**, **to write**, **to speak**
- All nouns: **patience**, **dedication**, **integrity**

You cannot mix forms:
- ❌ **reading**, **to write**, and **speech** (gerund + infinitive + noun)

#### Why It Works

Parallel structure works because the human brain processes patterns efficiently. When a reader encounters a series of items in the same form, the brain recognizes the pattern and processes each item faster. Breaking the pattern forces the reader to stop and re-parse the sentence.

#### Examples by Difficulty

**Easy:**
- ✅ The employee is **efficient**, **reliable**, and **punctual**. (adjective + adjective + adjective)
- ❌ The employee is **efficient**, **reliable**, and **comes on time**. (adjective + adjective + verb phrase)

**Medium:**
- ✅ The memorandum instructed staff **to update** their records, **to submit** their forms, and **to attend** the orientation. (infinitive + infinitive + infinitive)
- ❌ The memorandum instructed staff **to update** their records, **submitting** their forms, and **attendance** at the orientation. (infinitive + gerund + noun)

**Hard:**
- ✅ The committee recommended **that the budget be increased**, **that additional staff be hired**, and **that the timeline be extended**. (noun clause + noun clause + noun clause)
- ❌ The committee recommended **increasing the budget**, **that additional staff be hired**, and **to extend the timeline**. (gerund phrase + noun clause + infinitive phrase)

**CSE-Style:**
- The job requires **typing** documents, ___ correspondence, and **organizing** files.
  - (a) to answer (b) **answering** (c) answer (d) answered
  - *Explanation:* The series uses gerunds (typing, ___, organizing), so the missing item must also be a gerund → "answering."

### 4.2 Parallelism with Words

The simplest form of parallelism involves single words of the same part of speech joined by a conjunction or placed in a series.

#### Nouns with Nouns

When listing nouns, all items must be nouns (or noun phrases of similar structure):

- ✅ The civil servant demonstrated **integrity**, **competence**, and **dedication**.
- ❌ The civil servant demonstrated **integrity**, **being competent**, and **dedication**.

- ✅ The requirements include a **diploma**, a **transcript**, and a **certificate**.
- ❌ The requirements include a **diploma**, **transcribing** records, and a **certificate**.

#### Verbs with Verbs

When listing actions, all verbs must be in the same tense and form:

- ✅ The supervisor **reviewed**, **edited**, and **approved** the report. (past + past + past)
- ❌ The supervisor **reviewed**, **editing**, and **approved** the report. (past + gerund + past)

- ✅ She **plans**, **organizes**, and **executes** projects efficiently. (present + present + present)
- ❌ She **plans**, **organizing**, and **executes** projects efficiently.

#### Adjectives with Adjectives

- ✅ The policy is **clear**, **fair**, and **enforceable**. (adj + adj + adj)
- ❌ The policy is **clear**, **fair**, and **can be enforced**. (adj + adj + verb phrase)

- ✅ He is **intelligent**, **diligent**, and **resourceful**.
- ❌ He is **intelligent**, **diligent**, and **has many resources**.

#### Adverbs with Adverbs

- ✅ She works **quickly**, **accurately**, and **independently**. (adverb + adverb + adverb)
- ❌ She works **quickly**, **with accuracy**, and **independently**. (adverb + prep phrase + adverb)

#### Common Traps

The CSE often tests whether you can spot a single word that breaks the pattern:

| Faulty | Correct | Problem |
|--------|---------|---------|
| honest, loyal, and **having courage** | honest, loyal, and **courageous** | noun phrase among adjectives |
| to file, to sort, and **organizing** | to file, to sort, and **to organize** | gerund among infinitives |
| speaks clearly, writes well, and **good listener** | speaks clearly, writes well, and **listens attentively** | noun among verb phrases |

### 4.3 Parallelism with Phrases

When phrases are joined, each phrase must follow the same structural pattern.

#### Infinitive Phrases (to + verb)

All items must be infinitive phrases:

- ✅ The training aims **to improve** skills, **to build** confidence, and **to enhance** productivity.
- ❌ The training aims **to improve** skills, **building** confidence, and **enhanced** productivity.

- ✅ She wants **to pass** the exam, **to get** promoted, and **to serve** the public.
- ❌ She wants **to pass** the exam, **getting** promoted, and **public service**.

#### Gerund Phrases (verb + -ing)

All items must be gerund phrases:

- ✅ His duties include **managing** the team, **preparing** reports, and **coordinating** with other departments.
- ❌ His duties include **managing** the team, **preparation** of reports, and **coordinating** with other departments.

- ✅ **Swimming** in the morning, **jogging** in the afternoon, and **stretching** at night keep him fit.
- ❌ **Swimming** in the morning, **an afternoon jog**, and **stretching** at night keep him fit.

#### Prepositional Phrases

All items must be prepositional phrases with consistent structure:

- ✅ The documents are **in the cabinet**, **on the shelf**, and **under the desk**.
- ❌ The documents are **in the cabinet**, **on the shelf**, and **the desk has some underneath**.

- ✅ She excels **in planning**, **in execution**, and **in evaluation**.
- ❌ She excels **in planning**, **at executing**, and **in evaluation**.

#### Participial Phrases

- ✅ **Exhausted by the workload**, **frustrated by the delays**, and **discouraged by the lack of support**, the employee resigned.
- ❌ **Exhausted by the workload**, **the delays frustrated him**, and **discouraged by the lack of support**, the employee resigned.

#### Mixed Phrase Errors (Common CSE Trap)

The most common error is mixing phrase types:

| Faulty (Mixed) | Correct (Parallel) | Fix |
|----------------|-------------------|-----|
| **to plan** events, **coordinating** staff, **preparation** of materials | **planning** events, **coordinating** staff, **preparing** materials | All gerund phrases |
| **with dedication**, **efficiently**, **in a timely manner** | **with dedication**, **with efficiency**, **with timeliness** | All prepositional phrases |
| **Filing** documents, **to answer** calls, **data entry** | **Filing** documents, **answering** calls, **entering** data | All gerund phrases |

### 4.4 Parallelism with Clauses

When clauses are joined, they must share the same structure — either all independent, all dependent of the same type, or all following the same internal pattern.

#### Parallel Independent Clauses

- ✅ **The director approved the budget**, **the staff implemented the plan**, and **the committee monitored the progress**.
- ❌ **The director approved the budget**, **implementation of the plan by the staff**, and **the committee monitored the progress**.

#### Parallel Dependent Clauses

- ✅ The supervisor asked **that the report be revised**, **that the data be verified**, and **that the deadline be extended**.
- ❌ The supervisor asked **that the report be revised**, **to verify the data**, and **that the deadline be extended**.

- ✅ She explained **how the system works**, **how the data is processed**, and **how the reports are generated**.
- ❌ She explained **how the system works**, **the processing of data**, and **how the reports are generated**.

#### Parallel "That" Clauses

In formal writing, when multiple "that" clauses follow a verb, maintain the pattern:

- ✅ The memo stated **that all employees must attend**, **that tardiness will not be tolerated**, and **that exceptions require written approval**.
- ❌ The memo stated **that all employees must attend**, **tardiness will not be tolerated**, and **that exceptions require written approval**.

**Rule:** Either repeat "that" before every clause or omit it from all (though repeating is clearer in formal writing).

#### CSE-Style Example

**Hard:** The commission determined **that the policy was outdated**, ___, and **that immediate revision was necessary**.
- (a) the implementation was flawed
- (b) **that the implementation was flawed**
- (c) flawed implementation
- (d) implementing it was flawed

*Explanation:* The pattern is "that + clause" repeated three times. Only (b) maintains the parallel "that" clause structure.

### 4.5 Parallelism in Lists and Series

Lists and series are the most common context for parallelism errors — and the most heavily tested on the CSE.

#### The Rule

Every item in a list or series must be in the same grammatical form. This applies whether the list has two items or ten.

#### Two-Item Lists

- ✅ The position requires **experience** and **dedication**. (noun + noun)
- ❌ The position requires **experience** and **being dedicated**. (noun + gerund phrase)

- ✅ She is responsible for **planning** and **executing** the project. (gerund + gerund)
- ❌ She is responsible for **planning** and **the execution of** the project. (gerund + noun phrase)

#### Three-Item Lists (Most Common on CSE)

- ✅ The applicant must be **honest**, **competent**, and **dedicated**. (adj + adj + adj)
- ❌ The applicant must be **honest**, **competent**, and **show dedication**. (adj + adj + verb phrase)

- ✅ The job involves **typing** letters, **filing** documents, and **answering** phone calls. (gerund + gerund + gerund)
- ❌ The job involves **typing** letters, **filing** documents, and **phone calls must be answered**. (gerund + gerund + clause)

#### Longer Lists

- ✅ The training covered **time management**, **communication skills**, **conflict resolution**, **leadership development**, and **stress management**. (all noun phrases)
- ❌ The training covered **time management**, **how to communicate**, **conflict resolution**, **developing leadership**, and **stress management**. (mixed forms)

#### Vertical Lists (Bullet Points)

In official documents, bulleted lists must also be parallel:

**Correct:**
The employee shall:
- submit weekly reports
- attend all mandatory meetings
- maintain accurate records
- comply with office policies

**Incorrect:**
The employee shall:
- submit weekly reports
- attendance at all mandatory meetings
- maintain accurate records
- compliance with office policies

#### Signal: "Include," "Involve," "Require," "Consist of"

These verbs frequently introduce lists on the CSE. Whatever form follows the first item must continue:

- The duties **include** monitoring, evaluating, and reporting. ✅
- The duties **include** monitoring, evaluation, and to report. ❌
- The requirements **consist of** a diploma, a transcript, and a clearance. ✅
- The requirements **consist of** a diploma, transcribing records, and a clearance. ❌

### 4.6 Parallelism with Coordinating Conjunctions

Coordinating conjunctions (FANBOYS: **for, and, but, or, nor, yet, so**) join elements of equal grammatical rank. Whatever appears on one side of the conjunction must match the form on the other side.

#### AND (Addition)

"And" is the most common conjunction that requires parallelism:

- ✅ She **reviewed** the document and **signed** it. (past verb + past verb)
- ❌ She **reviewed** the document and **signing** it. (past verb + gerund)

- ✅ The policy is **comprehensive** and **enforceable**. (adj + adj)
- ❌ The policy is **comprehensive** and **can be enforced**. (adj + verb phrase)

#### BUT / YET (Contrast)

- ✅ The task was **difficult** but **rewarding**. (adj + adj)
- ❌ The task was **difficult** but **gave rewards**. (adj + verb phrase)

- ✅ He works **slowly** yet **accurately**. (adverb + adverb)
- ❌ He works **slowly** yet **with accuracy**. (adverb + prep phrase)

#### OR (Alternative)

- ✅ You may submit the form **in person** or **by mail**. (prep phrase + prep phrase)
- ❌ You may submit the form **in person** or **mailing** it. (prep phrase + gerund)

- ✅ The applicant must **pass the exam** or **complete the training**. (verb phrase + verb phrase)
- ❌ The applicant must **pass the exam** or **completion of the training**. (verb phrase + noun phrase)

#### NOR (Negative Addition)

- ✅ She neither **complained** nor **resigned**. (past verb + past verb)
- ❌ She neither **complained** nor **was resigning**. (past verb + past continuous)

#### FOR (Reason)

- ✅ He was commended, for he was **diligent** and **punctual**. (adj + adj after "for")
- ❌ He was commended, for he was **diligent** and **always coming on time**.

#### SO (Result)

- ✅ The deadline was extended, so employees could **review** their work and **correct** errors. (base verb + base verb)
- ❌ The deadline was extended, so employees could **review** their work and **the correction of** errors.

#### The Test

**Before the conjunction** and **after the conjunction** — do they match?

| Before | Conjunction | After | Parallel? |
|--------|------------|-------|-----------|
| noun | and | noun | ✅ |
| gerund | and | infinitive | ❌ |
| adjective | but | adjective | ✅ |
| verb phrase | or | noun phrase | ❌ |
| prep phrase | and | prep phrase | ✅ |

### 4.7 Parallelism with Correlative Conjunctions

Correlative conjunctions are **pairs** that work together. Whatever grammatical structure follows the first word of the pair MUST match the structure that follows the second word. This is one of the MOST TESTED parallelism concepts on the CSE.

#### The Five Correlative Pairs

| Pair | Function |
|------|----------|
| either...or | choice between two |
| neither...nor | negation of both |
| both...and | inclusion of both |
| not only...but also | emphatic addition |
| whether...or | two possibilities |

#### The Placement Rule

Each part of the correlative pair must be placed **directly before** the parallel element it introduces. The structure after "either" must mirror the structure after "or."

#### Either...Or

- ✅ You may submit **either** in person **or** by mail. (prep phrase // prep phrase)
- ❌ You may **either** submit in person **or** by mail. (verb phrase // prep phrase — unbalanced)

- ✅ **Either** the director **or** the supervisor must sign. (noun // noun)
- ❌ **Either** the director must sign **or** the supervisor. (clause // noun — unbalanced)

#### Neither...Nor

- ✅ She **neither** complained **nor** resigned. (verb // verb)
- ❌ She **neither** complained **nor** did she resign. (verb // clause)

- ✅ The report was **neither** accurate **nor** complete. (adj // adj)
- ❌ The report was **neither** accurate **nor** was it complete.

#### Both...And

- ✅ She is **both** efficient **and** reliable. (adj // adj)
- ❌ She is **both** efficient **and** she is reliable. (adj // clause)

- ✅ The program **both** trains employees **and** evaluates performance. (verb phrase // verb phrase)
- ❌ The program **both** trains employees **and** performance evaluation. (verb phrase // noun phrase)

#### Not Only...But Also

This pair is the most frequently tested and the most error-prone:

- ✅ He is **not only** qualified **but also** experienced. (adj // adj)
- ❌ He **not only** is qualified **but also** experienced. (verb+adj // adj — misplaced)

- ✅ She **not only** passed the exam **but also** topped it. (verb phrase // verb phrase)
- ❌ She **not only** passed the exam **but also** was the top scorer. (verb phrase // different verb phrase structure)

- ✅ The policy is **not only** comprehensive **but also** enforceable. (adj // adj)
- ❌ The policy **not only** is comprehensive **but also** enforceable.

#### Whether...Or

- ✅ I don't know **whether** to apply **or** to wait. (infinitive // infinitive)
- ❌ I don't know **whether** to apply **or** waiting. (infinitive // gerund)

- ✅ **Whether** you agree **or** disagree, the policy stands. (verb // verb)
- ❌ **Whether** you agree **or** in disagreement, the policy stands.

#### Placement Precision (Critical for CSE)

The most common error is placing the first correlative word too early, creating imbalance:

| Incorrect Placement | Correct Placement | Why |
|--------------------|-------------------|-----|
| She **not only** is smart **but also** hardworking. | She is **not only** smart **but also** hardworking. | After "not only" = adj; after "but also" = adj ✅ |
| He **either** wants to stay **or** leave. | He wants **either** to stay **or** to leave. | After "either" = infinitive; after "or" = infinitive ✅ |
| They **both** reviewed and **approved** the plan. | They **both** reviewed **and** approved the plan. | After "both" = verb; after "and" = verb ✅ |

#### CSE-Style Examples

**Easy:** She is ___ intelligent ___ hardworking.
- (a) either...or (b) neither...nor (c) **both...and** (d) whether...or
- *Explanation:* "Both...and" includes two positive qualities.

**Medium:** The new policy **not only** improves efficiency ___ reduces costs.
- (a) and also (b) **but also** (c) and (d) but
- *Explanation:* "Not only" must be paired with "but also."

**Hard:** The director decided to ___ postpone the meeting ___ cancel it entirely.
- (a) neither...nor (b) **either...or** (c) both...and (d) not only...but also
- *Explanation:* Two alternative actions (postpone or cancel) require "either...or."

### 4.8 Parallelism in Comparisons

When comparing two things using "than," "as...as," or "like/unlike," the compared elements must be in the same grammatical form.

#### Comparisons with "Than"

- ✅ **Running** a marathon is harder than **swimming** a mile. (gerund // gerund)
- ❌ **Running** a marathon is harder than **to swim** a mile. (gerund // infinitive)

- ✅ **To lead** is more difficult than **to follow**. (infinitive // infinitive)
- ❌ **To lead** is more difficult than **following**. (infinitive // gerund)

- ✅ She prefers **working** in the field **rather than sitting** in the office. (gerund // gerund)
- ❌ She prefers **working** in the field **rather than to sit** in the office.

#### Comparisons with "As...As"

- ✅ **Filing** documents is as important as **answering** phone calls. (gerund // gerund)
- ❌ **Filing** documents is as important as **to answer** phone calls.

- ✅ He is as **dedicated** as he is **competent**. (adjective // adjective)
- ❌ He is as **dedicated** as **his competence**. (adjective // noun)

#### Comparisons with "Like" and "Unlike"

- ✅ **Like** the previous director, the new one emphasizes transparency. (noun // noun)
- ❌ **Like** the previous director **emphasized** transparency, the new one does too. (mixing clause with noun)

- ✅ **Unlike** writing reports, **presenting** them requires public speaking skills. (gerund // gerund)
- ❌ **Unlike** writing reports, **presentations** require public speaking skills. (gerund // noun)

#### The "Rather Than" Construction

- ✅ She chose **to resign** rather than **to compromise** her principles. (infinitive // infinitive)
- ✅ She chose **resigning** rather than **compromising** her principles. (gerund // gerund)
- ❌ She chose **to resign** rather than **compromising** her principles. (infinitive // gerund)

#### Common Comparison Errors on the CSE

| Faulty | Correct | Rule |
|--------|---------|------|
| Swimming is better exercise than **to run**. | Swimming is better exercise than **running**. | gerund = gerund |
| **To err** is human; **forgiving** is divine. | **To err** is human; **to forgive** is divine. | infinitive = infinitive |
| He likes reading more than **to write**. | He likes reading more than **writing**. | gerund = gerund |
| Working here is as rewarding as **any other job**. | Working here is as rewarding as **working** anywhere else. | gerund = gerund |

### 4.9 Parallelism in Pairs and Paired Ideas

English has many fixed expressions and sentence patterns that present ideas in pairs. Both elements of the pair must match grammatically.

#### Common Paired Structures

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| A, not B | The goal is **efficiency**, not **being slow**. ❌ → **efficiency**, not **waste**. ✅ |
| A rather than B | She chose **to stay** rather than **to leave**. ✅ |
| from A to B | The office operates from **planning** to **execution**. ✅ |
| between A and B | Choose between **staying** and **leaving**. ✅ |

#### Paired Adjectives

- ✅ The report was **thorough** and **well-organized**. (adj + adj)
- ❌ The report was **thorough** and **organized it well**. (adj + clause)

#### Paired Actions

- ✅ The supervisor **praised** the team and **recommended** them for an award. (past verb + past verb)
- ❌ The supervisor **praised** the team and **recommending** them for an award.

#### Paired Reasons

- ✅ He resigned **because of the low salary** and **because of the poor working conditions**. (prep phrase + prep phrase)
- ❌ He resigned **because of the low salary** and **the working conditions were poor**.

#### Balanced Sentences

Some sentences deliberately present two contrasting ideas in parallel form for rhetorical effect:

- "Ask not what your country can do for you; ask what you can do for your country." — JFK
- "**To err** is human; **to forgive** is divine." — Alexander Pope
- "Government is not the solution **to** our problem; government **is** the problem." — Reagan

These work because both halves mirror each other structurally.

### 4.10 Faulty Parallelism

Faulty parallelism occurs when joined elements do NOT share the same grammatical form. Learning to spot and fix these errors is the core skill tested on the CSE.

#### Type 1: Mixed Word Forms

| Faulty | Correct | Analysis |
|--------|---------|----------|
| She is **creative**, **intelligent**, and **has determination**. | She is **creative**, **intelligent**, and **determined**. | adj + adj + adj |
| The job requires **patience**, **skill**, and **being organized**. | The job requires **patience**, **skill**, and **organization**. | noun + noun + noun |
| He is **honest**, **hardworking**, and **a leader**. | He is **honest**, **hardworking**, and **capable**. | adj + adj + adj |

#### Type 2: Mixed Phrase Types

| Faulty | Correct | Analysis |
|--------|---------|----------|
| She enjoys **reading** books, **to watch** movies, and **hiking**. | She enjoys **reading** books, **watching** movies, and **hiking**. | gerund + gerund + gerund |
| The memo instructed us **to file** reports, **organizing** data, and **to attend** meetings. | The memo instructed us **to file** reports, **to organize** data, and **to attend** meetings. | infinitive + infinitive + infinitive |
| He spoke **with confidence**, **clearly**, and **in a loud voice**. | He spoke **with confidence**, **with clarity**, and **with volume**. | prep + prep + prep |

#### Type 3: Mixed Clause Structures

| Faulty | Correct |
|--------|---------|
| The director said **that the budget was approved** and **we should start immediately**. | The director said **that the budget was approved** and **that we should start immediately**. |
| She asked **whether the report was ready** and **can we submit it today**. | She asked **whether the report was ready** and **whether we could submit it today**. |

#### Type 4: Correlative Conjunction Imbalance

| Faulty | Correct |
|--------|---------|
| He **not only** is a teacher **but also** a coach. | He is **not only** a teacher **but also** a coach. |
| She **either** wants to stay **or** leaving. | She wants **either** to stay **or** to leave. |
| **Neither** the report was accurate **nor** complete. | The report was **neither** accurate **nor** complete. |

#### Type 5: Comparison Imbalance

| Faulty | Correct |
|--------|---------|
| **To walk** is healthier than **riding** a car. | **Walking** is healthier than **riding** a car. |
| She prefers **typing** to **handwrite**. | She prefers **typing** to **handwriting**. |
| His work is better than **what his colleague does**. | His work is better than **his colleague's**. |

#### How to Fix Faulty Parallelism

**Step 1:** Identify the conjunction or structure that joins elements.
**Step 2:** Identify the grammatical form of the first element.
**Step 3:** Check if all other elements match that form.
**Step 4:** Convert non-matching elements to the same form.

**Example Fix:**
- Faulty: "The duties include **planning** events, **coordination** of staff, and **to prepare** materials."
- Step 1: "include" introduces a list joined by commas and "and"
- Step 2: First element = gerund phrase ("planning events")
- Step 3: Second = noun phrase ("coordination of staff") ❌; Third = infinitive phrase ("to prepare materials") ❌
- Step 4: Convert all to gerunds → "**planning** events, **coordinating** staff, and **preparing** materials."

### 4.11 Parallelism in Formal Writing

In government and professional communication, parallelism is not optional — it is expected. The CSE tests this because civil servants must write clearly.

#### In Memoranda and Official Correspondence

**Parallel (Professional):**
> The following actions are required:
> 1. Submit the accomplished form to the HR Division.
> 2. Attach two copies of your latest performance rating.
> 3. Secure clearance from your immediate supervisor.

**Non-Parallel (Unprofessional):**
> The following actions are required:
> 1. Submit the accomplished form to the HR Division.
> 2. Two copies of your latest performance rating should be attached.
> 3. Clearance from your immediate supervisor.

#### In Policy Statements

**Parallel:**
> The agency shall ensure **that public funds are used efficiently**, **that services are delivered promptly**, and **that accountability is maintained at all levels**.

**Non-Parallel:**
> The agency shall ensure **that public funds are used efficiently**, **prompt delivery of services**, and **maintaining accountability at all levels**.

#### In Job Descriptions

**Parallel:**
> The position requires the ability to:
> - analyze complex data
> - prepare comprehensive reports
> - communicate findings effectively
> - coordinate with multiple stakeholders

**Non-Parallel:**
> The position requires the ability to:
> - analyze complex data
> - preparation of comprehensive reports
> - effective communication of findings
> - coordinating with multiple stakeholders

#### In Resolutions and Directives

**Parallel:**
> RESOLVED, that the committee shall **review** the existing policy, **propose** amendments, and **submit** recommendations within thirty days.

**Non-Parallel:**
> RESOLVED, that the committee shall **review** the existing policy, **the proposal of** amendments, and **submitting** recommendations within thirty days.

### 4.12 Common Parallelism Errors

This section catalogs the most frequent parallelism errors tested on the CSE, organized by error type.

#### Error 1: Mixing Gerunds and Infinitives

- ❌ She likes **swimming**, **to jog**, and **cycling**.
- ✅ She likes **swimming**, **jogging**, and **cycling**.
- ✅ She likes **to swim**, **to jog**, and **to cycle**.

**Rule:** Choose one form (gerund OR infinitive) and use it consistently throughout the series.

#### Error 2: Mixing Nouns and Verbs

- ❌ The seminar focused on **leadership**, **communication**, and **how to manage** time.
- ✅ The seminar focused on **leadership**, **communication**, and **time management**.

#### Error 3: Inconsistent Verb Tenses

- ❌ He **reviewed** the files, **is checking** the data, and **will submit** the report.
- ✅ He **reviewed** the files, **checked** the data, and **submitted** the report.

**Exception:** Tense shifts are acceptable when describing actions at genuinely different times.

#### Error 4: Mixing Active and Passive Voice

- ❌ The committee **reviewed** the proposal, and the budget **was approved** by the director.
- ✅ The committee **reviewed** the proposal, and the director **approved** the budget.

#### Error 5: Unequal Items After Correlatives

- ❌ She is **not only** a skilled writer **but also** she manages the team.
- ✅ She is **not only** a skilled writer **but also** an effective manager.

#### Error 6: Broken Comparisons

- ❌ **To teach** is more rewarding than **an office job**.
- ✅ **Teaching** is more rewarding than **working** in an office.

#### Error 7: Inconsistent List Introductions

- ❌ The employee must: file reports, attendance at meetings, and submit evaluations.
- ✅ The employee must: file reports, attend meetings, and submit evaluations.

#### Error 8: Article/Preposition Inconsistency

When repeating articles or prepositions in a series, be consistent:

- ✅ She visited **the** library, **the** museum, and **the** park. (article repeated)
- ✅ She visited the library, museum, and park. (article used once, applies to all)
- ❌ She visited **the** library, museum, and **the** park. (inconsistent)

### 4.13 Step-by-Step Rules for Identifying and Correcting Parallelism

#### Rule 1: Find the Conjunction or Joining Structure

Look for:
- Coordinating conjunctions: and, but, or, nor, yet, so
- Correlative conjunctions: either...or, neither...nor, both...and, not only...but also
- Commas in a series (A, B, and C)
- Comparison words: than, as...as, rather than, like, unlike

#### Rule 2: Identify the First Element's Form

What grammatical form does the first item take?
- Noun? All items must be nouns
- Gerund? All items must be gerunds
- Infinitive? All items must be infinitives
- Adjective? All items must be adjectives
- Prepositional phrase? All items must be prepositional phrases
- Clause? All items must be clauses of the same type

#### Rule 3: Check Every Other Element

Does each subsequent element match the first? If not, you have found faulty parallelism.

#### Rule 4: For Correlatives, Check Placement

What immediately follows "not only"? The same structure must immediately follow "but also."
- not only + adjective then but also + adjective
- not only + verb phrase then but also + verb phrase
- not only + noun then but also + noun

#### Rule 5: For Comparisons, Match Both Sides

What appears before "than"? The same form must appear after "than."
- gerund + than + gerund
- infinitive + than + infinitive
- noun + than + noun

### 4.14 Exam Strategies

#### Strategy 1: Identify the Pattern First

Before looking at the answer choices, identify the grammatical pattern established by the other elements in the sentence. Then find the choice that matches.

Example: The duties include filing documents, ___, and answering calls.
- Pattern: gerund + gerund, so the missing item must be a gerund
- Look for the gerund among the choices

#### Strategy 2: Check Correlative Placement

For "not only...but also" questions:
1. Find "not only" and note what word or phrase immediately follows it
2. Find "but also" and check if the same type of word or phrase follows
3. If not, the sentence has faulty parallelism

#### Strategy 3: Elimination by Form

If the question asks you to choose the correct sentence:
1. Identify the conjunction or series structure
2. Check each choice for consistent grammatical forms
3. Eliminate choices where forms do not match
4. The remaining choice with perfect parallelism is correct

#### Strategy 4: Watch for Subtle Third-Item Errors

The CSE often makes the error subtle. Two items are parallel but the third breaks the pattern:
- "She is smart, diligent, and has integrity."
- The first two (adjectives) seem correct, but the third (verb phrase) breaks parallelism.

#### Strategy 5: Comparisons Must Match Forms

For "than" and "as...as" questions:
1. Identify what comes before the comparison word
2. The answer must be in the same form
3. Eliminate choices in different forms

### Quick Recap

| Rule | Remember |
|------|----------|
| Words in a series | Same part of speech (all nouns, all verbs, all adjectives) |
| Phrases in a series | Same phrase type (all gerund, all infinitive, all prepositional) |
| Clauses in a series | Same clause structure (all "that" clauses, all questions, etc.) |
| After coordinating conjunctions | Same form on both sides of and/but/or |
| After correlative conjunctions | Same form after each part of the pair |
| In comparisons (than, as...as) | Same form on both sides of the comparison word |
| In lists and bullet points | Every item starts with the same grammatical form |
| In formal writing | Maintain structural consistency throughout |

### Memory Aids

**The Mirror Rule:** Whatever you see on one side of a conjunction, you should see the same type on the other side - like a grammatical mirror.

**The Column Test:** Stack list items vertically. If they all look like they belong in the same column of a table, they are parallel.

**FANBOYS Check:** Whenever you see and, but, or, nor, yet, so - immediately check what is on each side.

**Correlative Pairs - The "After" Test:** Look at what comes AFTER each word in the pair. Those two things must match.

**The Gerund-Infinitive Rule:** Never mix -ing forms with to + verb forms in the same series. Pick one and stick with it.

## Summary

Parallelism is the grammatical principle that elements performing the same function in a sentence must share the same grammatical form. It applies to words, phrases, clauses, lists, comparisons, and any structure joined by conjunctions. The Civil Service Examination tests parallelism heavily because government writing demands structural precision and clarity.

The most effective exam strategy is to identify the conjunction or joining structure first, determine the grammatical form of the first element, and then verify that all other elements match. For correlative conjunctions, check what immediately follows each part of the pair. For comparisons, ensure both sides of "than" or "as...as" use the same form. Master these patterns and you will handle parallelism questions with confidence on exam day.
