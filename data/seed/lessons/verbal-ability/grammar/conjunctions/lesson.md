# Conjunctions

## Explanations

### Introduction

**Conjunctions** are words that join other words, phrases, or clauses together. They are the glue that holds sentences together, creating logical flow and coherence in writing. Without conjunctions, every idea would stand alone as a short, choppy sentence — making communication inefficient and difficult to follow.

### Why Conjunctions Matter

Conjunctions serve as the logical connectors of language. They answer critical questions about how ideas relate:
- **Addition?** — She is intelligent *and* hardworking.
- **Contrast?** — He studied hard, *but* he failed.
- **Cause/Effect?** — She was late *because* of the traffic.
- **Choice?** — You may submit it today *or* tomorrow.
- **Condition?** — *If* you pass the exam, you will be promoted.

In government communication — memoranda, legal documents, policy directives, and official correspondence — conjunctions determine the logical relationships between clauses. A wrong conjunction can reverse the meaning of an entire sentence.

### Why Conjunctions Are Tested in the CSE

The Grammar and Correct Usage section of the Philippine Civil Service Examination consistently tests conjunctions because:
- Government documents require precise logical connections between ideas
- Conjunction errors are among the most common grammar mistakes in Filipino English writing
- Correct conjunction usage signals the ability to construct complex, professional-level sentences
- Many CSE questions test the examinee's ability to identify logical relationships between clauses
- Run-on sentences, comma splices, and sentence fragments — all conjunction-related errors — are heavily penalized in official writing

### Common Mistakes Examinees Make

1. Using **"and"** when the relationship is contrast (should be **"but"**)
2. Creating **run-on sentences** by joining clauses without proper conjunctions or punctuation
3. Creating **comma splices** by using a comma alone to join independent clauses
4. Breaking **parallel structure** with correlative conjunctions ("not only...but also")
5. Confusing **subordinating conjunctions** with prepositions ("although" vs. "despite")
6. Using **"so"** when the relationship is contrast, not cause-effect
7. Misusing **"unless"** (forgetting it means "if not")
8. Creating **sentence fragments** by starting with a subordinating conjunction and never completing the thought

---

### Learning Objectives

After this lesson, you should be able to:
- Identify different types of conjunctions (coordinating, subordinating, correlative)
- Correctly use coordinating conjunctions with proper punctuation
- Recognize and use subordinating conjunctions to show logical relationships
- Apply correlative conjunctions with correct parallel structure and subject-verb agreement
- Maintain parallelism in sentences joined by conjunctions
- Distinguish between conjunctions and transitional expressions
- Identify and correct common conjunction errors (run-ons, comma splices, fragments)
- Solve CSE-style conjunction questions with confidence under time pressure

### 4.1 What Are Conjunctions?

#### Definition

A **conjunction** is a word (or pair of words) that connects words, phrases, or clauses within a sentence. The word "conjunction" comes from Latin *conjungere* — "to join together."

#### Grammatical Function

Conjunctions create relationships between sentence elements. They tell the reader HOW two ideas connect:
- Are they equal? (coordinating)
- Is one dependent on the other? (subordinating)
- Are they paired alternatives or additions? (correlative)

#### Importance in Sentence Flow

Without conjunctions:
> The employee was qualified. She did not get the job. The interview panel found her answers unsatisfactory.

With conjunctions:
> The employee was qualified, **but** she did not get the job **because** the interview panel found her answers unsatisfactory.

The second version is clearer, more professional, and communicates the logical chain of events.

#### Examples by Difficulty

**Easy:**
- She is smart **and** diligent. (addition)
- He ran fast, **but** he lost the race. (contrast)
- Study hard **or** you will fail. (alternative)

**Medium:**
- **Although** the budget was limited, the project was completed on time. (concession)
- The director approved the proposal **because** it aligned with the agency's goals. (cause)
- **Neither** the manager **nor** the supervisor was informed of the change. (correlative negation)

**Hard:**
- The committee recommended the policy revision **not only** because it addressed fiscal concerns **but also** because it streamlined interdepartmental coordination — a priority **since** the reorganization took effect. (multiple conjunction types in one sentence)
- **Unless** the applicant submits all requirements **before** the deadline, the application will be deemed incomplete, **and** no extension shall be granted **regardless of** the reason for delay. (legal/formal register)

**CSE-Style:**
- She studied hard, ___ she passed the examination.
  - Answer: **so** (cause-and-effect: studying caused passing)
- The employee wanted a promotion, ___ he did not meet the qualifications.
  - Answer: **but** (contrast between desire and reality)

### 4.2 Coordinating Conjunctions

#### The FANBOYS Rule

There are exactly **seven** coordinating conjunctions in English. The mnemonic **FANBOYS** helps you remember them:

| Letter | Conjunction | Function | Example |
|--------|------------|----------|---------|
| **F** | for | reason/cause | She left early, **for** she was feeling ill. |
| **A** | and | addition | He filed the report **and** submitted the form. |
| **N** | nor | negative addition | She did not call, **nor** did she send an email. |
| **B** | but | contrast | The task was difficult, **but** she completed it. |
| **O** | or | alternative/choice | Submit it today **or** tomorrow. |
| **Y** | yet | contrast (surprise) | He is young, **yet** he is very capable. |
| **S** | so | result/effect | It rained heavily, **so** the event was postponed. |

#### What "Coordinating" Means

"Coordinating" means **joining equals**. Coordinating conjunctions connect:
- Word + Word: bread **and** butter
- Phrase + Phrase: in the morning **or** in the afternoon
- Clause + Clause: She studied hard, **and** she passed the exam.

Both elements joined must be of **equal grammatical rank**.

#### Detailed Breakdown of Each FANBOYS Conjunction

##### FOR (reason/cause)

"For" as a conjunction means "because" — it introduces a reason. It is more formal than "because."

- She was promoted, **for** she consistently exceeded her targets.
- The office closed early, **for** a typhoon was approaching.

> **Important:** "For" as a conjunction ALWAYS has a comma before it and joins two independent clauses. Do not confuse it with "for" as a preposition (e.g., "This is for you").

##### AND (addition)

"And" adds information. It is the most common conjunction in English.

- The director reviewed **and** approved the proposal.
- She is efficient, **and** she is always on time.
- Submit the form, the ID, **and** the supporting documents. (serial/Oxford comma)

##### NOR (negative addition)

"Nor" adds a negative idea to a previous negative statement. It requires **inverted word order** (auxiliary before subject) in the second clause.

- She did not attend the meeting, **nor** did she send a representative.
- The report was not accurate, **nor** was it submitted on time.
- He has never been late, **nor** has he ever been absent.

> **Key Rule:** After "nor," the subject and auxiliary verb are inverted (like a question).

##### BUT (contrast)

"But" shows opposition or contrast between two ideas.

- The task was simple, **but** many employees made errors.
- He applied for the position, **but** he was not shortlisted.
- She is strict **but** fair.

##### OR (alternative/choice)

"Or" presents alternatives or choices.

- You may file the complaint in person **or** online.
- Submit the report by Friday, **or** you will face a penalty.
- Is the meeting at 9:00 **or** 10:00?

##### YET (contrast with surprise)

"Yet" is similar to "but" but adds a sense of surprise or unexpectedness.

- She is inexperienced, **yet** she handles the job well.
- The policy is controversial, **yet** it has broad support.
- He failed the first attempt, **yet** he did not give up.

> **Difference from "but":** "Yet" implies that the second idea is surprising given the first. "But" simply states contrast.

##### SO (result/effect)

"So" introduces a result or consequence of the first clause.

- The deadline was extended, **so** we have more time to prepare.
- She studied diligently, **so** she topped the examination.
- The printer broke down, **so** we had to use the one on the second floor.

#### Comma Rules for Coordinating Conjunctions

| Situation | Comma? | Example |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Joining two **independent clauses** | YES | She studied hard**,** and she passed. |
| Joining two **words** | NO | bread and butter |
| Joining two **short phrases** | NO | in the morning or in the afternoon |
| Joining three or more items (series) | YES (Oxford comma recommended) | pens**,** papers**,** and folders |

**The Critical Rule:** When a coordinating conjunction joins two **complete sentences** (independent clauses), place a **comma before** the conjunction.

- ✅ The employee filed the report**,** and the supervisor approved it.
- ❌ The employee filed the report and the supervisor approved it. (missing comma — run-on)
- ❌ The employee filed the report, the supervisor approved it. (comma splice — no conjunction)

#### Common Mistakes with Coordinating Conjunctions

| Mistake | Incorrect | Correct |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Comma splice | She was tired, she went home. | She was tired, **so** she went home. |
| Run-on | She was tired she went home. | She was tired, **so** she went home. |
| Wrong conjunction | He studied hard, **but** he passed. | He studied hard, **so** he passed. |
| Unnecessary comma | She is smart**,** and talented. | She is smart and talented. (no comma for two words) |
| Missing inversion after "nor" | She didn't call, nor she emailed. | She didn't call, **nor did she** email. |

### 4.3 Subordinating Conjunctions

#### What Is Subordination?

Subordination means making one clause **dependent** on another. A subordinating conjunction turns an independent clause into a **dependent (subordinate) clause** that cannot stand alone as a sentence.

- Independent: *She passed the exam.*
- Dependent: ***Because** she passed the exam* (incomplete — needs a main clause)
- Complete: ***Because** she passed the exam, she was promoted.*

#### Common Subordinating Conjunctions by Function

##### Cause and Effect

| Conjunction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| because | She was promoted **because** she excelled in her duties. |
| since | **Since** the budget was approved, the project can proceed. |
| as | **As** the deadline approached, the team worked overtime. |
| now that | **Now that** the policy is in effect, all employees must comply. |

##### Contrast and Concession

| Conjunction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| although | **Although** he was qualified, he was not hired. |
| though | **Though** the task was difficult, she completed it. |
| even though | **Even though** it rained, the event continued. |
| whereas | She prefers email, **whereas** he prefers phone calls. |
| while | **While** the plan has merits, it also has risks. |

##### Condition

| Conjunction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| if | **If** you submit late, your application will be rejected. |
| unless | **Unless** you have a valid ID, you cannot enter. |
| provided that | You may leave early **provided that** your work is done. |
| as long as | The permit is valid **as long as** you renew it annually. |
| in case | Bring an umbrella **in case** it rains. |

##### Time

| Conjunction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| before | Submit the form **before** the deadline. |
| after | **After** the meeting ended, the staff returned to work. |
| when | **When** the bell rings, the exam begins. |
| while | **While** the director was speaking, no one interrupted. |
| until | Wait here **until** your name is called. |
| as soon as | **As soon as** the memo is signed, distribute it. |
| once | **Once** the training is complete, you may resume your duties. |
| since | She has been absent **since** Monday. (time, not cause) |

##### Purpose

| Conjunction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| so that | She studied hard **so that** she could pass. |
| in order that | The policy was revised **in order that** it might be clearer. |

#### Punctuation Rules for Subordinating Conjunctions

| Structure | Comma? | Example |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Dependent clause FIRST** | YES — comma after the dependent clause | **Because it rained,** the event was canceled. |
| **Independent clause FIRST** | Usually NO comma | The event was canceled **because it rained.** |

**Memory trick:** If the sentence starts with the subordinating conjunction, use a comma after the dependent clause. If the main clause comes first, no comma is usually needed.

#### Important Notes

1. A dependent clause CANNOT stand alone:
   - ❌ **Because she studied hard.** (fragment)
   - ✅ **Because she studied hard,** she passed the exam.

2. "Since" can mean CAUSE or TIME — context determines which:
   - Cause: **Since** you are here, let's begin. (= because)
   - Time: She has been waiting **since** 8:00 AM. (= from that point)

3. "While" can mean CONTRAST or TIME:
   - Contrast: **While** I agree with the goal, I disagree with the method.
   - Time: **While** the director was speaking, everyone listened.

### 4.4 Correlative Conjunctions

#### What Are Correlative Conjunctions?

Correlative conjunctions are **pairs** of conjunctions that work together to join equal grammatical elements. They always come in fixed pairs — you cannot use one without the other.

#### The Five Main Correlative Pairs

| Pair | Function | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| either...or | choice between two | **Either** the manager **or** the supervisor must sign. |
| neither...nor | negation of both | **Neither** the report **nor** the memo was submitted. |
| both...and | inclusion of both | She is **both** efficient **and** reliable. |
| not only...but also | emphatic addition | He is **not only** qualified **but also** experienced. |
| whether...or | two possibilities | I don't know **whether** to apply **or** to wait. |

#### Rules for Correlative Conjunctions

##### Rule 1: Parallel Structure

The elements after each part of the pair MUST be grammatically parallel (same form).

| Incorrect | Correct | Why |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| She is **both** smart **and** has talent. | She is **both** smart **and** talented. | adjective + adjective |
| He **not only** sings **but also** is a dancer. | He **not only** sings **but also** dances. | verb + verb |
| **Either** you study **or** failing is certain. | **Either** you study, **or** you fail. | clause + clause |

##### Rule 2: Subject-Verb Agreement with "Either...Or" and "Neither...Nor"

The verb agrees with the **nearer** subject (the one closest to the verb).

| Example | Rule Applied |
|---------|-------------|
| **Either** the director **or** the managers **are** responsible. | "managers" (plural) is nearer → plural verb |
| **Either** the managers **or** the director **is** responsible. | "director" (singular) is nearer → singular verb |
| **Neither** the employees **nor** the supervisor **was** informed. | "supervisor" (singular) is nearer → singular verb |
| **Neither** the supervisor **nor** the employees **were** informed. | "employees" (plural) is nearer → plural verb |

##### Rule 3: "Both...And" Always Takes a Plural Verb

- **Both** the manager **and** the supervisor **are** attending. (always plural)

##### Rule 4: Placement Precision

Place each part of the correlative pair **directly before** the element it introduces.

| Incorrect | Correct |
|-----------|---------|
| She **not only** is smart **but also** hardworking. | She is **not only** smart **but also** hardworking. |
| He **either** wants to stay **or** leave. | He wants **either** to stay **or** to leave. |

### 4.5 Conjunctions and Parallelism

#### Why Parallelism Matters

**Parallelism** (also called parallel structure) means using the same grammatical form for elements that are joined by a conjunction. This is one of the MOST TESTED concepts on the CSE.

#### The Rule

When a conjunction joins two or more elements, those elements must match in grammatical form:
- Noun + Noun
- Verb + Verb
- Adjective + Adjective
- Phrase + Phrase
- Clause + Clause

#### Examples of Parallel vs. Non-Parallel Structure

| Non-Parallel (Wrong) | Parallel (Correct) | Fix |
|---------------------|-------------------|-----|
| She likes **reading** and **to write**. | She likes **reading** and **writing**. | gerund + gerund |
| He is **smart**, **diligent**, and **has courage**. | He is **smart**, **diligent**, and **courageous**. | adj + adj + adj |
| The job requires **typing**, **filing**, and **to answer phones**. | The job requires **typing**, **filing**, and **answering phones**. | gerund + gerund + gerund |
| She **not only** passed the exam **but also** was getting promoted. | She **not only** passed the exam **but also** got promoted. | past tense + past tense |
| **Either** submit the form **or** you can email it. | **Either** submit the form **or** email it. | imperative + imperative |

#### Parallelism with Coordinating Conjunctions

- The supervisor **reviewed**, **edited**, and **approved** the report. ✅ (verb + verb + verb)
- The policy is **clear**, **fair**, and **enforceable**. ✅ (adj + adj + adj)
- She works **in the office** and **at home**. ✅ (prep phrase + prep phrase)

#### Parallelism with Correlative Conjunctions

- He is **both** a teacher **and** a mentor. ✅ (noun + noun)
- She **neither** complained **nor** resigned. ✅ (verb + verb)
- The plan is **not only** ambitious **but also** achievable. ✅ (adj + adj)

#### CSE Trap: Broken Parallelism

The CSE frequently presents sentences with broken parallelism as answer choices. Train yourself to spot mismatched forms:

- ❌ The duties include **planning** events, **coordinating** schedules, and **to prepare** reports.
- ✅ The duties include **planning** events, **coordinating** schedules, and **preparing** reports.

### 4.6 Conjunctions in Compound Sentences

#### What Is a Compound Sentence?

A **compound sentence** contains two or more **independent clauses** joined by:
1. A coordinating conjunction (with a comma), OR
2. A semicolon, OR
3. A semicolon + transitional expression + comma

#### Pattern: Independent Clause + , + FANBOYS + Independent Clause

- The director approved the budget**,** **and** the project commenced.
- She applied for the position**,** **but** she was not selected.
- Submit the report by Friday**,** **or** you will receive a warning.

#### Common Errors in Compound Sentences

##### Run-On Sentence (Fused Sentence)

Two independent clauses with NO punctuation or conjunction between them.

- ❌ She was tired she went home early.
- ✅ She was tired**,** **so** she went home early.

##### Comma Splice

Two independent clauses joined by ONLY a comma (no conjunction).

- ❌ She was tired**,** she went home early.
- ✅ She was tired**,** **so** she went home early.
- ✅ She was tired**;** she went home early.

##### How to Fix Run-Ons and Comma Splices

| Method | Example |
|--------|---------|
| Add a coordinating conjunction | She was tired**, so** she went home. |
| Use a semicolon | She was tired**;** she went home. |
| Make two sentences | She was tired**.** She went home. |
| Subordinate one clause | **Because** she was tired, she went home. |

### 4.7 Conjunctions in Complex Sentences

#### What Is a Complex Sentence?

A **complex sentence** contains one **independent clause** and at least one **dependent clause** joined by a subordinating conjunction.

#### Two Possible Orders

**Order 1: Dependent clause first (comma required)**
- **Because the typhoon was approaching,** the office closed early.
- **Although he was qualified,** he did not get the position.
- **If you need assistance,** please contact the HR department.

**Order 2: Independent clause first (usually no comma)**
- The office closed early **because the typhoon was approaching.**
- He did not get the position **although he was qualified.**
- Please contact the HR department **if you need assistance.**

#### Punctuation Summary

| Structure | Punctuation |
|-----------|-------------|
| Subordinate clause + main clause | COMMA between them |
| Main clause + subordinate clause | Usually NO comma |
| Exception: contrast clauses (although, whereas, while) | Sometimes a comma even when main clause is first |

#### Examples of Complex Sentences by Relationship

**Cause:** The project was delayed **because** funding was insufficient.
**Time:** **After** the meeting concluded, the staff returned to their desks.
**Condition:** **If** the applicant meets all requirements, the permit will be issued.
**Concession:** **Although** the policy is unpopular, it remains in effect.
**Purpose:** She enrolled in the training **so that** she could qualify for promotion.

### 4.8 Commonly Confused Conjunctions

#### BECAUSE vs. SINCE

| Because | Since |
|---------|-------|
| Specifically states cause | Can mean cause OR time |
| Stronger causal link | Weaker/assumed causal link |
| Used when the reason is NEW information | Used when the reason is KNOWN/obvious |

- **Because** he cheated, he was disqualified. (the reason is the main point)
- **Since** everyone is here, let's begin. (the reason is obvious/given)
- She has been absent **since** Monday. (TIME — not cause)

**CSE Tip:** If the question tests time vs. cause, check whether the sentence uses perfect tense. "Since" + perfect tense usually = time.

#### ALTHOUGH vs. DESPITE

| Although | Despite |
|----------|---------|
| Conjunction (followed by a CLAUSE) | Preposition (followed by a NOUN/GERUND) |
| **Although** it rained, we continued. | **Despite** the rain, we continued. |
| **Although** she was tired, she worked. | **Despite** being tired, she worked. |

- ❌ **Although** the rain, we continued. (wrong — "although" needs a clause)
- ❌ **Despite** it rained, we continued. (wrong — "despite" needs a noun/gerund)

#### WHILE vs. DURING

| While | During |
|-------|--------|
| Conjunction (followed by a CLAUSE) | Preposition (followed by a NOUN) |
| **While** I was speaking, he interrupted. | **During** my speech, he interrupted. |
| **While** the meeting was ongoing, no one left. | **During** the meeting, no one left. |

#### UNLESS vs. UNTIL

| Unless | Until |
|--------|-------|
| Means "if not" (CONDITION) | Means "up to the point when" (TIME) |
| **Unless** you study, you will fail. | Wait **until** your name is called. |
| **Unless** approved, the request is denied. | The office is open **until** 5 PM. |

**Key difference:** "Unless" = condition. "Until" = time endpoint.

#### SO vs. THEREFORE

| So | Therefore |
|----|-----------|
| Coordinating conjunction | Transitional expression (conjunctive adverb) |
| Comma before: She was late**, so** she missed it. | Semicolon before, comma after: She was late**; therefore,** she missed it. |
| Informal/neutral | Formal |

#### BUT vs. HOWEVER

| But | However |
|-----|---------|
| Coordinating conjunction | Transitional expression (conjunctive adverb) |
| Comma before: He tried**, but** he failed. | Semicolon before, comma after: He tried**; however,** he failed. |
| Cannot start a formal sentence (debated) | Can start a sentence: **However,** he failed. |

### 4.9 Transitional Expressions vs. Conjunctions

#### What Are Transitional Expressions?

Transitional expressions (also called **conjunctive adverbs**) show logical relationships between independent clauses, similar to conjunctions. However, they are **NOT conjunctions** — they are adverbs, and they follow different punctuation rules.

#### Common Transitional Expressions

| Function | Transitional Expression |
|----------|------------------------|
| Contrast | however, nevertheless, nonetheless, on the other hand, instead |
| Result | therefore, consequently, thus, hence, as a result |
| Addition | moreover, furthermore, in addition, also, besides |
| Example | for example, for instance, specifically |
| Time | meanwhile, subsequently, afterward, then |
| Summary | in conclusion, in summary, overall |

#### Punctuation Differences: Conjunction vs. Transitional Expression

| Type | Pattern | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Coordinating conjunction | clause**,** conjunction clause | She tried**,** but she failed. |
| Transitional expression | clause**;** transition**,** clause | She tried**;** however**,** she failed. |
| Transitional expression (new sentence) | clause**.** Transition**,** clause | She tried**.** However**,** she failed. |

#### Why This Matters for the CSE

The CSE tests whether you can distinguish between conjunctions and transitional expressions because the **punctuation is different**:

- ❌ She was qualified, however, she was not hired. (COMMA SPLICE — "however" is not a conjunction)
- ✅ She was qualified; however, she was not hired.
- ✅ She was qualified. However, she was not hired.
- ✅ She was qualified, but she was not hired. ("but" IS a conjunction)

### 4.10 Common Conjunction Errors

#### Error 1: Sentence Fragments

A dependent clause standing alone without a main clause.

- ❌ **Because** she was late.
- ✅ **Because** she was late, she missed the orientation.
- ❌ **Although** the report was submitted on time.
- ✅ **Although** the report was submitted on time, it contained errors.

#### Error 2: Run-On Sentences

Two independent clauses with no punctuation or conjunction.

- ❌ The meeting started late the director was stuck in traffic.
- ✅ The meeting started late **because** the director was stuck in traffic.

#### Error 3: Comma Splices

Two independent clauses joined by only a comma.

- ❌ She passed the exam, she was very happy.
- ✅ She passed the exam, **and** she was very happy.
- ✅ She passed the exam; she was very happy.

#### Error 4: Incorrect Parallelism

Mismatched grammatical forms after conjunctions.

- ❌ The job requires **typing**, **filing**, and **to answer** the phone.
- ✅ The job requires **typing**, **filing**, and **answering** the phone.

#### Error 5: Misuse of Correlatives

Wrong placement or broken parallel structure with correlative pairs.

- ❌ He **not only** is a teacher **but also** a coach.
- ✅ He is **not only** a teacher **but also** a coach.

#### Error 6: Wrong Conjunction for the Logical Relationship

- ❌ She studied hard, **but** she passed. (illogical — "but" implies contrast)
- ✅ She studied hard, **so** she passed. (logical — cause and effect)
- ❌ He was absent, **so** he submitted his report on time. (illogical)
- ✅ He was absent, **but** he submitted his report on time. (logical — contrast)

#### Error 7: Double Conjunctions

Using two conjunctions for the same relationship.

- ❌ **Although** she was tired, **but** she continued working.
- ✅ **Although** she was tired, she continued working.
- ✅ She was tired, **but** she continued working.

**Rule:** You cannot use a subordinating conjunction AND a coordinating conjunction for the same pair of clauses. Choose one.

#### Error 8: "Unless" Errors

Forgetting that "unless" already contains a negative meaning ("if not").

- ❌ **Unless** you **don't** submit the form, you will be disqualified. (double negative)
- ✅ **Unless** you submit the form, you will be disqualified.

### 4.11 Step-by-Step Rules for Choosing Conjunctions

#### Rule 1: Identify the Logical Relationship

Ask yourself: How do these two ideas relate?

| Relationship | Use |
|-------------|-----|
| Addition | and, both...and, not only...but also, moreover |
| Contrast | but, yet, although, however, whereas |
| Cause/Reason | because, since, for, as |
| Effect/Result | so, therefore, consequently |
| Choice/Alternative | or, either...or, whether...or |
| Condition | if, unless, provided that, as long as |
| Time | before, after, when, while, until, since |
| Concession | although, even though, though |

#### Rule 2: Determine Clause Independence

- Can BOTH parts stand alone as sentences? → Use a **coordinating conjunction** (with comma) or a **semicolon + transitional expression**.
- Can only ONE part stand alone? → The other part uses a **subordinating conjunction**.

#### Rule 3: Check Punctuation

- Coordinating conjunction joining clauses → comma BEFORE the conjunction
- Subordinating conjunction at the START → comma AFTER the dependent clause
- Subordinating conjunction in the MIDDLE → usually no comma
- Transitional expression → semicolon before, comma after

#### Rule 4: Verify Parallelism

If the conjunction joins elements within a clause, check that all joined elements match in form:
- ✅ **reading**, **writing**, and **speaking** (all gerunds)
- ❌ **reading**, **writing**, and **to speak** (mixed forms)

#### Rule 5: Check Subject-Verb Agreement (Correlatives)

For "either...or" and "neither...nor," the verb agrees with the **nearer** subject.
For "both...and," the verb is always **plural**.

#### Rule 6: Elimination Technique for the CSE

When unsure:
1. Read the sentence and identify the logical relationship between clauses.
2. Eliminate conjunctions that express the WRONG relationship.
3. Check punctuation — does the answer choice match the sentence structure?
4. Verify parallelism if correlative conjunctions are involved.
5. Read the complete sentence with your chosen answer to confirm it sounds logical.

### 4.12 Exam Strategies

#### Strategy 1: Identify the Relationship First

Before looking at the choices, determine what logical relationship the sentence needs:
- Does the second clause AGREE with the first? → and, also, moreover
- Does the second clause CONTRADICT the first? → but, yet, although, however
- Does the second clause RESULT from the first? → so, therefore, consequently
- Does the second clause give a REASON for the first? → because, since, for
- Does the second clause present an ALTERNATIVE? → or, either...or

#### Strategy 2: Watch for Contrast Traps

The CSE loves testing whether you can distinguish cause-effect from contrast:
- "She studied hard, ___ she passed." → **so** (cause → effect)
- "She studied hard, ___ she failed." → **but/yet** (expectation violated)

#### Strategy 3: Spot the Double Conjunction Error

If you see "Although...but" or "Because...so" in a sentence, it's WRONG. Only one conjunction per clause pair.

#### Strategy 4: Check for Comma Splices

If two complete sentences are joined by ONLY a comma, the answer likely needs a conjunction:
- "She was tired, ___ she went home." → needs "so" or "and"

#### Strategy 5: Parallelism Quick-Check

For correlative conjunction questions, immediately check what comes after EACH part of the pair. They must match:
- "not only **smart** but also **diligent**" ✅ (adj + adj)
- "not only **smart** but also **has diligence**" ❌ (adj + clause)

#### Strategy 6: "Unless" = "If Not"

Mentally replace "unless" with "if not." If the sentence still makes sense, "unless" is correct:
- "Unless you study, you will fail" = "If you do not study, you will fail" ✅

### 4.14 Mini Practice Set

**Question 1:** She wanted to attend the seminar, ___ she had a conflicting schedule.
- Answer: **but** (contrast between desire and obstacle)

- Answer: **Unless** (condition: if the manager does NOT approve)

**Question 3:** He is ___ intelligent ___ hardworking.
- Answer: **both...and** (inclusion of two positive qualities)

**Question 4:** The employee was reprimanded ___ he violated the attendance policy.
- Answer: **because** (reason/cause)

**Question 5:** Submit the requirements on time, ___ your application will be disapproved.
- Answer: **or** (alternative/consequence)

**Question 6:** ___ it was raining heavily, the outdoor ceremony continued.
- Answer: **Although** (concession — the rain did not stop the ceremony)

**Question 7:** She has ___ a degree in accounting ___ five years of experience.
- Answer: **not only...but also** (emphatic addition)

**Question 8:** The office will remain closed ___ further notice.
- Answer: **until** (time endpoint)

**Question 9:** He studied all night, ___ he passed the examination with flying colors.
- Answer: **so** (result of studying)

**Question 10:** ___ the director ___ the assistant manager was available for comment.
- Answer: **Neither...nor** (negation of both)

**Question 11:** She completed the report ___ she left the office.
- Answer: **before** (time sequence)

**Question 12:** The policy applies to all employees, ___ they are regular or contractual.
- Answer: **whether** (two possibilities)

**Question 13:** He is qualified for the position, ___ he lacks the required experience.
- Answer: **yet/but** (contrast — qualified but lacking experience)

**Question 14:** ___ you finish early, you may leave.
- Answer: **If** (condition)

**Question 15:** The team worked overtime, ___ the project was still not completed on time.
- Answer: **but/yet** (contrast — effort did not produce expected result)

### 4.15 Quick Recap

#### Conjunction Types Summary

| Type | What It Does | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Coordinating | Joins EQUALS (word+word, clause+clause) | for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so |
| Subordinating | Makes one clause DEPENDENT on another | because, although, if, when, unless |
| Correlative | Works in PAIRS to join equal elements | either...or, neither...nor, both...and |

#### Key Punctuation Rules

1. **Comma + FANBOYS** = joins two independent clauses
2. **Subordinator at start** = comma after the dependent clause
3. **Subordinator in middle** = usually no comma
4. **Semicolon + transitional expression + comma** = joins independent clauses (NOT a conjunction)
5. **Never:** comma alone between two independent clauses (= comma splice)

#### Parallelism Rules

- Conjunctions join MATCHING grammatical forms
- Correlative pairs require PARALLEL elements after each part
- Check: noun+noun, verb+verb, phrase+phrase, clause+clause

#### Subject-Verb Agreement with Correlatives

- either...or / neither...nor → verb agrees with NEARER subject
- both...and → ALWAYS plural verb

#### The "Double Conjunction" Rule

- Never use BOTH a subordinating and coordinating conjunction for the same clause pair
- ❌ Although...but / Because...so / Even though...yet
- ✅ Choose ONE: either subordinate or coordinate

### 4.16 Memory Aids

#### FANBOYS Mnemonic

**F**or - **A**nd - **N**or - **B**ut - **O**r - **Y**et - **S**o

Memorize this acronym. There are ONLY seven coordinating conjunctions. If a word is not one of these seven, it is NOT a coordinating conjunction.

#### "SWABi" for Subordinating Conjunctions

**S**ince - **W**hen - **A**fter - **B**efore - **I**f

(Plus: because, although, unless, until, while, though, even though, whereas, as, once, so that, provided that, as long as, now that, in case, as soon as)

#### Correlative Pairs Memory Trick

Think of them as "dance partners" — they ALWAYS come in pairs:
- either ↔ or
- neither ↔ nor
- both ↔ and
- not only ↔ but also
- whether ↔ or

#### Comma Rule Memory Trick

**"Before FANBOYS, after SWABI"**
- Put the comma BEFORE a coordinating conjunction (between clauses)
- Put the comma AFTER a dependent clause (when it starts the sentence)

#### "Unless = If Not" Shortcut

Whenever you see "unless," mentally substitute "if...not":
- "Unless you study" = "If you do not study"
- If the substitution works, "unless" is correct.

#### Contrast vs. Cause-Effect Quick Test

Read the two clauses. Ask: "Is the second clause SURPRISING given the first?"
- YES → contrast (but, yet, although, however)
- NO, it FOLLOWS logically → cause-effect (so, because, therefore)
